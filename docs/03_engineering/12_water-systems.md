# Water systems

## Why water systems matter

Water supports germination, transport within the plant, temperature regulation and growth. It can also carry contamination, create electrical hazards and hide problems when distribution is poorly understood.

Safe water and direct crop observation remain the baseline. Treatment or automation should solve a demonstrated need.

## Keep the water path visible

The complete path should be understandable:

1. source;
2. storage;
3. treatment, if justified;
4. distribution;
5. tray or substrate;
6. drainage or reuse decision.

Unknown containers, hidden hoses and unlabelled additives weaken traceability.

## Source and storage

Assess the water source before adding equipment. Storage should use suitable, cleanable materials and prevent contamination, stagnation and accidental mixing. Water and electrical zones must remain separated.

## Irrigation by evidence

Choose watering methods according to crop, tray, substrate and work process. Useful observations may include:

- amount applied;
- tray mass before and after watering;
- visible moisture distribution;
- drainage amount;
- crop response;
- water temperature, pH or conductivity when relevant.

Pump runtime alone does not prove how much water reached the crop.

## Drainage is information

Drainage may reveal uneven application, blocked outlets or excessive watering. It should be directed safely and inspected rather than treated only as waste.

## Cleaning and contamination control

The system must be accessible for emptying, inspection and cleaning. Avoid dead ends and components that cannot be opened or replaced. Cleaning methods and materials must be suitable for food production and recorded where necessary.

## Automation in stages

Begin with understandable manual watering and good records. Add measurement, assisted dosing and automatic control only after the water path and crop response are known. Manual operation must remain possible during failure.

Leakage, overflow, dry-running pumps, blocked lines and stuck valves need safe responses. Automation should stop or warn when its evidence becomes stale or contradictory.

## Experimental inputs

Willow water, aspirin, hydrogen peroxide, ceramic filtration and electroculture are not part of routine production. They belong in controlled experiments after the basic water system is stable and food-safety implications are understood.
