# Lighting

## Why lighting matters

Light supports photosynthesis and influences form, colour, water use, temperature and growth timing. The goal is not the brightest shelf, but suitable and reasonably even light for the crop at its current stage.

## Daylight first, when practical

Useful daylight can reduce artificial-light demand. It must still be evaluated for heat, seasonal change, direction and uneven exposure. Artificial light provides predictability where daylight is insufficient or variable.

## Useful quantities

| Quantity | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Watts | Electrical power used by the lamp |
| Lumens | Human-visible light output |
| Lux | Visible light on a surface |
| PPFD | Plant-relevant photon rate at the crop |
| DLI | Plant-relevant light received over a day |
| Spectrum | Distribution of light by wavelength |

The current BH1750 measures lux. It is useful for trends and basic spatial comparisons, but it is not a professional plant-light reference.

## Measure where the plants are

Light should be assessed at crop height, across several tray positions. A single central reading can hide dark edges or hotspots. Record lamp height, shelf, crop stage, daylight conditions and measurement method.

Cameras may support comparison of plant spacing, canopy coverage and uneven development. Consistent viewpoint and lighting are essential.

## Layout before more power

Improve distribution before adding lamp power. Consider:

- lamp height and spacing;
- shelf width;
- reflective surfaces that remain cleanable and safe;
- tray position;
- daylight direction;
- dimming and zoning.

A proposed moving or counterbalanced lighting system should remain an experiment until it proves biological benefit, mechanical safety and lower total energy use than a simpler layout.

## Photoperiod and darkness

Plants need an intentional light schedule. The ERP should record timing, intensity or control state and relevant crop stage. A relay state confirms that a command was sent, not that useful light reached the crop.

## Lighting, climate and water

More light may increase heat and water demand. Lamps, climate control and irrigation should be evaluated as one biological system.

## Failure and safety

The system must show lamp or measurement failure clearly. Cables and fittings require protection from water. Human working conditions, glare and safe access also matter.

## Growth path

Start with repeatable lux surveys and direct crop observation. Improve layout, then compare with a plant-light reference when decisions justify it. Automated supplemental lighting should follow validated measurements and safe control boundaries.
