# Humanitarian nutrition and local resilience

## The long-term purpose

The project aims to help build an honest, transparent and sustainable food chain. One possible future contribution is improving access to nutrient-rich food where diets lack vitamins and minerals.

This purpose requires care. Assistance should strengthen local capacity rather than create dependency on imported products, equipment or knowledge.

## A bridge, not a replacement

Freeze-dried microgreens or other products may sometimes support emergency response, difficult transport routes, seasonal gaps or temporary shortages. They should complement local food systems where appropriate, not replace them.

The first question is therefore not “What can be exported?” but “What would support health and resilience in this place?”

## Local food comes first

Local crops, growers, knowledge, taste, climate, water, energy and infrastructure must shape the solution. Microgreens may be useful in some settings and unsuitable in others.

Responsible work should be developed with communities and relevant local organisations. Outside assumptions cannot replace local experience.

## Knowledge may be more valuable than product

Sharing the knowledge needed to build a local microgreens system can create more lasting value than repeated exports. Open cultivation methods, transparent ERP records and understandable engineering documents can help people adapt the system to local conditions.

Knowledge sharing may include:

- seed and crop selection;
- biological cultivation;
- safe water and hygiene;
- low-cost monitoring;
- local equipment repair;
- traceability and business records;
- preservation methods where useful.

The goal is not to copy one Dutch rack elsewhere. It is to make the underlying knowledge available for local design.

## A responsible funding loop

Production and sales in economically stronger markets may provide resources for research, open documentation and carefully chosen nutrition work. This creates an opportunity to turn commercial learning into shared knowledge.

It also creates a threat: humanitarian language could become marketing, local producers could be displaced or communities could become dependent on imported products. These risks must remain visible in project decisions.

## Conditions for action

A humanitarian application should proceed only when:

- a real nutritional need is established;
- local partners participate in design and evaluation;
- food safety and cultural suitability are addressed;
- local production has been considered first;
- long-term maintenance and supply are realistic;
- benefits and harms can be evaluated transparently.

## Measuring success

Success is not measured only by kilograms shipped. More meaningful indicators include improved local capability, reduced dependency, knowledge retained locally, suitable nutrition outcomes and systems that can continue without permanent outside control.

## Relationship to the project

The ERP supports transparency from seed to final use. Freeze-drying may support storage and transport. Open engineering documentation supports adaptation and learning. Together, these tools can contribute, but they do not determine what a community needs.

## Closing principle

The strongest contribution may not be sending more products. It may be helping more people develop, understand and own food systems that fit their own environment.
