# Rack architecture

## Why the rack matters

The rack is more than a frame. It brings plants, trays, light, air, water, power, observations and human work together. A good rack supports biology and remains easy to clean, inspect, change and understand.

## Design from the crop outward

Shelf height, tray spacing and services should follow the crop and work process. The design must provide:

- enough space for healthy growth;
- even and adjustable lighting;
- air movement without damaging plants;
- safe watering and drainage;
- access for sowing, inspection and harvest;
- separation between water and electrical systems;
- room for observation and portable instruments.

## A modular rack

The rack can be understood as five connected layers:

| Layer | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Structure | Stability, load support and adjustment |
| Cultivation | Trays, crop positions and drainage |
| Lighting | Lamps, mounts and light control |
| Services | Water, power, data and airflow routes |
| Observation | Human access, cameras and instruments |

Each layer should be replaceable or adjustable without rebuilding everything else.

## Identity and position

Every rack and shelf needs a stable identifier. Tray positions should be clear enough to repeat a measurement or find a batch. Labels or QR codes may link the physical position to the ERP.

Identifiers must not change when a crop changes. The database can then connect batches, measurements, maintenance and equipment to the same physical place over time.

## Structure and materials

The frame must safely carry wet trays, lights, cables and temporary instruments. It should resist tipping and remain stable during daily work. Surfaces should be smooth, durable and accessible for cleaning. Hidden water traps, sharp edges and absorbent materials should be avoided.

## Light, air and water

Lamp height and shelf spacing should allow adjustment without unsafe improvisation. Air should move through the crop zone rather than only around the room. Water should follow a visible route, with leakage and overflow directed away from electrical equipment.

Rack design affects all three systems. A change in shelf height can alter light, temperature, airflow and water use, so changes should be recorded and evaluated together.

## Measurement-ready design

A measurement-ready rack includes defined positions, safe temporary mounts, accessible low-voltage connections and clear cable routes. It supports the Portable Biological Laboratory without forcing expensive instruments onto every shelf.

## Human work

The operator must be able to see, reach and remove trays without awkward lifting or contact with cables. Controls, isolation points and labels should be visible. A rack that is efficient for equipment but difficult for people is not a good design.

## Growth path

Begin with one stable manual rack. Standardise its identifiers, tray layout and service routes. Add portable measurement points and limited automation only after the physical design works well in daily use. A multi-rack design should grow from proven modules rather than assumptions.
