# Strategic monitoring

## Why priorities are necessary

Monitoring consumes money, energy, maintenance and human attention. The project should therefore monitor what protects crops, improves decisions or creates useful knowledge. More sensors do not automatically produce better cultivation.

## What monitoring can contribute

Monitoring may:

- protect cultivation from preventable loss;
- support daily decisions;
- reveal spatial or seasonal patterns;
- improve traceability;
- test ideas and equipment;
- prepare carefully validated automation.

Direct attention to the plants remains essential. Technology should help people notice and understand, not make them distant from the crop.

## Capability levels

| Capability | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Baseline | Understand normal conditions and crop responses |
| Protective | Warn while useful action is still possible |
| Diagnostic | Investigate a known problem |
| Reference | Verify other measurements |
| Research | Answer a defined question with controlled evidence |
| Exploratory | Test whether a new method has value |

Not every variable needs every capability. Portable equipment is often better for diagnosis and research than permanent installation.

## How to set priorities

A monitoring proposal should be judged by:

- biological importance;
- consequence of missing the change;
- time available to respond;
- ability to take useful action;
- current uncertainty;
- reliability of the proposed evidence;
- coverage and reuse;
- maintenance and data burden;
- learning value.

High-consequence, actionable changes deserve earlier attention. Interesting measurements with no clear use belong in experiments, not permanent infrastructure.

## Priority classes

- **Priority 1 — essential:** protects safety, crop survival or core traceability.
- **Priority 2 — high value:** improves frequent and meaningful decisions.
- **Priority 3 — development:** useful after the baseline is stable.
- **Priority 4 — exploratory:** investigated on a limited scale.

Priorities are reviewed when the crop, rack, business scale or available knowledge changes.

## Current direction

For the present project, a trustworthy baseline is more valuable than a large network. Temperature, humidity, light and direct crop observation can establish that baseline. Deeper spatial surveys, reference measurements and imaging can be provided by the Portable Biological Laboratory.

Indicator plants may be introduced when they answer a defined question and can be observed consistently. Their response must not be treated as a diagnosis on its own.

## When monitoring should stop

A measurement may be removed when it no longer changes a decision, duplicates better evidence, creates excessive maintenance or cannot be interpreted reliably. Removing weak monitoring improves the quality of the remaining system.
