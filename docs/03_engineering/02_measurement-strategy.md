# Measurement strategy

## Why measurement needs a strategy

A precise sensor does not guarantee a useful measurement. The question, position, timing and interpretation matter just as much. Measurement should therefore begin with a biological or operational question.

Examples include:

- Is one part of a shelf warmer than another?
- Does the crop receive light evenly?
- Does a change in watering improve growth without increasing disease risk?
- Is an indicator plant responding before the production crop?

If the question is unclear, collecting more values usually creates noise rather than understanding.

## Write the measurement statement

Before measuring, define:

- the question;
- the crop, batch or system involved;
- the variable or visible response;
- the location and period;
- the required comparison;
- the decision the result may support.

This small statement prevents a measurement from losing its purpose.

## Choose the right evidence

Evidence may be:

- **Direct:** temperature measured at crop height.
- **Proxy:** tray mass used as an indication of water loss.
- **Derived:** daily light integral calculated from repeated light measurements.
- **Human:** a grower records colour, smell or visible stress.
- **Visual:** images compare plant spacing or canopy development.
- **Laboratory:** a sample is tested using a controlled method.

Several forms can support one another, but combining them does not automatically remove uncertainty.

## Measure in space and time

One location may hide variation across a rack. One moment may miss daily changes. The strategy should match the expected pattern:

- use several positions for spatial differences;
- use repeated readings for cycles and trends;
- use the Portable Biological Laboratory for surveys;
- keep a fixed baseline where loss of information could cause harm.

Record position precisely. A rack and shelf identifier may be combined with a marked position or QR code linked to the ERP.

## Understand measurement quality

Important terms include:

| Term | Practical meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Range | Values the instrument can measure |
| Resolution | Smallest displayed change |
| Accuracy | Closeness to the accepted value |
| Repeatability | Similarity under the same conditions |
| Response time | Speed of reaction to change |
| Drift | Change in performance over time |
| Uncertainty | Remaining doubt around the result |

The required quality depends on the decision. A rough survey may be enough to find a warm zone, while research comparisons may need a reference instrument and documented uncertainty.

## Use thresholds carefully

Not every threshold means danger. The ERP should distinguish:

- a preferred biological range;
- an advisory level;
- an operational warning;
- a safety limit;
- a research boundary.

Crop stage and duration matter. A short deviation may have a different meaning from a prolonged one.

## Validate before routine use

New methods should first be compared with direct observation or a trusted reference. Check whether placement, heat, water, lighting or handling changes the result. A method becomes routine only when it is understandable and repeatable.

## A practical starting point

For the first rack:

1. define one useful question;
2. measure a small number of meaningful positions;
3. record crop and environmental context;
4. compare results with plant observations;
5. decide whether continued measurement adds value.
