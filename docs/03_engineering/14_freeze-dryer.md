# Freeze dryer

## Why freeze-drying matters

Freeze-drying may preserve nutrient-rich products for longer and reduce dependence on refrigerated transport. Within this project it is a future core technology, not merely a secondary preservation method.

The aim is to complement suitable local food production, not replace it.

## Current status

The project does not yet operate its own freeze dryer. Early work should therefore focus on product requirements, outsourced trials, food safety, packaging and realistic energy use before a purchase is considered.

## How the process works

The product is frozen. Pressure is lowered, allowing ice to leave mainly through sublimation. A later drying phase removes more tightly bound moisture. The process protects many qualities better than high-temperature drying, but results depend on the product, preparation, loading and cycle.

Dry does not mean sterile. Clean cultivation, hygienic handling and suitable packaging remain necessary.

## Define the product first

Before choosing a machine, establish:

- intended crop or mixture;
- fresh and dry batch size;
- acceptable colour, structure and flavour;
- nutrient or quality evidence required;
- target shelf life;
- packaging and rehydration needs;
- expected market or humanitarian use.

The machine should follow the product requirement.

## Batch control

Each trial should record:

- source cultivation batch;
- harvest and preparation method;
- loading pattern and mass;
- freezing and drying cycle;
- final mass and endpoint evidence;
- packaging time and material;
- storage conditions;
- quality observations and test results.

The endpoint must be verified. A cycle finishing does not by itself prove that the product is dry enough for safe storage.

## Packaging

Freeze-dried material can rapidly absorb moisture. Packaging should follow the drying cycle without unnecessary delay and should match the required barrier, portion size, transport conditions and shelf life.

## Energy and scale

Freeze-drying is energy intensive. Judge it by the useful finished product, not only by machine power. Consider energy per dry kilogram, avoided waste, storage life, transport mass and whether outsourced production is currently more sensible.

## Development path

1. define the product and evidence needs;
2. perform small outsourced trials;
3. evaluate quality, packaging and cost;
4. establish repeatable batches;
5. consider ownership only when scale and use justify it.

## Humanitarian direction

Export may sometimes provide temporary nutritional support, but long-term value may come from sharing cultivation, processing and ERP knowledge so communities can build locally appropriate systems. This direction is developed in [Humanitarian nutrition and local resilience](18_humanitarian-nutrition-and-local-resilience.md).
