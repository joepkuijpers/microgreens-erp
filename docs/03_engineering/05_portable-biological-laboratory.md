# Portable biological laboratory

## The central idea

The project does not need expensive instruments on every shelf. It needs racks that are ready to receive reliable instruments when deeper investigation is useful.

The Portable Biological Laboratory is a controlled set of instruments, mounts and methods that can move between racks. Fixed sensors provide the baseline; portable equipment provides detail; external laboratories remain available for measurements that cannot be performed responsibly on site.

## Three levels of observation

1. **Fixed baseline monitoring** follows essential conditions continuously.
2. **Portable measurement** compares positions, verifies sensors and supports experiments.
3. **External analysis** provides specialist methods, independent verification or food-safety testing.

This structure controls cost while preserving a path to professional evidence.

## Measurement-ready racks

A measurement-ready rack provides:

- safe mounting points;
- defined measurement positions;
- protected power and data connections;
- clear cable routes;
- rack and shelf identifiers;
- space for temporary equipment;
- an ERP record of its configuration.

Positions should be precise enough to repeat. A QR code can link a physical identifier such as `GLD004` to its database record. The code is only an example; the identifier structure must be documented before wider use.

## A portable kit

Modules may include:

- temperature and humidity reference instruments;
- plant-light measurement;
- portable imaging with a fixed viewpoint;
- weighing equipment;
- water-quality instruments;
- sample containers and labels;
- cleaning and calibration materials.

The contents should follow real questions. The laboratory should not become a collection of unused devices.

## A clear deployment

Before moving the kit, define the question and positions. At the rack, confirm identity, mount the instrument without disturbing the crop, allow it to stabilise and record the measurement context. After use, inspect, clean and return the equipment.

The ERP should record:

- instrument identity;
- rack, shelf and position;
- crop and batch;
- start and end time;
- calibration state;
- operator;
- method and relevant notes.

Automatic identification may later reduce manual entry, but the system must remain understandable when automation is unavailable.

## Useful applications

The portable laboratory can:

- map temperature, humidity or light across a rack;
- compare fixed sensors with a reference;
- check plant spacing and canopy development with consistent images;
- investigate an unusual crop response;
- support small controlled experiments;
- prepare well-labelled samples for external analysis.

## Hygiene and safety

Portable equipment can move contamination between racks. Surfaces must be cleanable, contact with plants should be minimised and clean-to-unclean movement should be planned. Electrical equipment must remain protected from water and unstable mounting.

## Development path

Begin with one well-organised measurement kit. Standardise mounts, identifiers and records only after practical use has shown what is needed. Later stages may add docking, automatic identification and multi-site comparison.
