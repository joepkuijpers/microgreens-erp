# Experimental inputs

## Why this chapter exists

New cultivation ideas can be valuable, but an interesting mechanism is not the same as a safe or useful production method. Experimental inputs are kept separate from routine water and crop management so that curiosity does not weaken food safety, traceability or the baseline.

## Current decision

The present priority is to understand normal growth using safe water, known seed, suitable substrate and controlled environmental conditions. The following ideas are not approved routine inputs:

- ceramic plant-pot filtration;
- willow water;
- aspirin or acetylsalicylic acid;
- hydrogen peroxide as a crop input;
- electroculture applied through water.

This does not mean that every idea is rejected permanently. It means that none should enter production without a defined reason, safety review and controlled comparison.

## Ceramic plant-pot filtration

Terracotta may contain unknown minerals, glazes, residues or manufacturing treatments. A pot marked with a country of manufacture is not automatically suitable for filtering water used in food production. Only materials with appropriate food-contact evidence should be considered.

## Willow water

Willow contains biologically active compounds and is traditionally associated with plant propagation. Its composition varies by species, plant part, season, preparation and storage. That variability makes routine dosing and interpretation difficult.

## Aspirin

Acetylsalicylic acid may influence plant stress responses, but household tablets can contain coatings and additives. A possible biological effect does not establish a safe concentration or food-production use.

## Hydrogen peroxide

Hydrogen peroxide can be used in specific cleaning or treatment contexts, but concentration, contact time, residues, material compatibility and worker safety must be controlled. It must not be added casually to living crops.

## Electroculture through water

Electrical experiments involving water create both scientific and safety questions. The proposed mechanism, voltage, current path, materials and possible electrochemical products would need to be defined before any test.

## A controlled return

An experimental input may be reconsidered when:

- the biological question is clear;
- food and worker safety have been reviewed;
- the material is identified and traceable;
- a control group is available;
- success and stopping criteria are defined;
- the crop remains outside sale or consumption unless safety is established.

The ERP should record the hypothesis, preparation, dose, batch, observations, result and decision. Negative results are useful and should remain visible.
