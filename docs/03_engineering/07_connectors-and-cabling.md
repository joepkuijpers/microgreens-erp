# Connectors and cabling

## Why connections deserve attention

Small wiring faults can create false measurements, equipment failure or unsafe conditions. Connections should therefore be visible, labelled and appropriate for water, movement and repeated maintenance.

## Keep systems separate

The rack contains four connection classes:

- mains power;
- low-voltage power;
- data and sensor signals;
- temporary laboratory connections.

Power, data and water should use separate routes. Where paths must cross, the crossing should be short, protected and easy to inspect.

## Choose connectors by use

A connector should match voltage, current, environment and frequency of use. Important qualities include:

- protection against incorrect insertion;
- secure locking where movement is likely;
- strain relief;
- moisture resistance where justified;
- available documentation and replacement parts;
- enough capacity without unnecessary size.

Exposed breadboard wiring is suitable for controlled development, not permanent wet-rack installation.

## Cable routing

Cables should follow fixed, accessible routes. They must not hang over trays, touch sharp edges or carry the weight of attached equipment. Provide service loops only where movement or maintenance requires them.

Do not bundle sensitive sensor signals beside switching mains cables when interference may affect readings.

## Identification

Both cable ends should show the same stable identifier. The label should connect the physical cable with its ERP or wiring record. Colour can help recognition, but colour alone is not a reliable identity.

For each interface, record:

- purpose;
- connector type;
- voltage or signal;
- pinout;
- source and destination;
- cable length;
- installation date.

## Portable connections

The Portable Biological Laboratory needs connectors designed for repeated use. Docking should not make it possible to connect an instrument to the wrong voltage or bus. Temporary cables must not create trip, snag or contamination risks.

## Commissioning and fault finding

Before use, inspect routing and strain relief, confirm polarity and pinout, check isolation between systems and verify communication. When a fault occurs, work from the power source towards the device and record the confirmed cause.

Repairs should restore the documented design. Repeated failure at the same point is a reason to redesign the connection, not merely replace it again.
