# Climate control

## Why climate control exists

Climate control creates conditions in which plants can develop well and people can operate safely. It should respond to biological evidence, not force every crop into one fixed number.

## Three climate scales

- **Room climate** describes the shared cultivation space.
- **Rack climate** shows the influence of rack layout, lamps and airflow.
- **Crop-zone climate** describes what plants experience around their leaves and trays.

One room sensor cannot explain every shelf. Portable surveys can reveal differences before permanent control zones are added.

## Important observations

Climate understanding may combine:

- temperature and relative humidity;
- airflow and ventilation state;
- light and lamp heat;
- watering and evaporation;
- plant appearance and growth;
- indicator-plant responses;
- external weather or room conditions.

Humidity alone does not describe condensation or plant water stress. Duration, temperature, airflow and crop stage matter.

## From advice to automation

Control should develop in stages:

1. observe conditions and crop responses;
2. provide clear advice;
3. test equipment responses;
4. automate limited actions within safe boundaries;
5. coordinate systems only after validation.

Heating, cooling, fans, humidification, lighting and watering can influence one another. Independent rules may conflict, so coordinated control needs a clear priority order.

## Ranges and deadbands

Use biologically meaningful ranges rather than constant switching around one setpoint. A deadband prevents equipment from rapidly turning on and off. Warnings should consider how far and how long conditions have moved outside the preferred range.

## Daylight and energy

Sunlight can provide useful light but may also create heat and uneven conditions. Artificial light also adds heat. Climate and lighting decisions should therefore be evaluated together rather than as separate systems.

## Safe operation

Every control function needs:

- manual override;
- a defined safe state;
- limits on continuous operation;
- clear response to missing or stale measurements;
- a record of commands and results.

A failed sensor must not trigger uncontrolled heating, cooling, humidification or ventilation.

## ERP records

The ERP should preserve the observation, interpretation, decision, command and later outcome as separate records. This makes it possible to learn whether an intervention actually improved plant conditions.
