# Welcome

Welcome to the Microgreens ERP project.

This project began as software for managing microgreen production.

It has grown into a long-term effort to develop a transparent and sustainable food chain where biology, technology and continuous learning strengthen one another.

The ERP is one component of that vision—not the destination itself.

# Project Overview

The project combines several disciplines:

- Biological production
- Cultivation
- Laboratory research
- Sensor technology
- Automation
- ERP software
- Documentation

Together these form a continuously improving knowledge system rather than isolated projects.

# Purpose of the Documentation

Documentation exists to preserve knowledge.

It records not only *what* has been built, but also *why* decisions were made, allowing future contributors to understand the reasoning behind the project.

The documentation grows alongside the project while remaining organised and easy to navigate.

# Current Project Status

The project is under active development.

The documentation is being restructured into stable knowledge domains while the ERP continues to evolve.

The objective is to build a solid foundation that can support years of future development.

# Introduction

The Growth Cycle describes how the project learns.

It is not a software workflow or a business process.
It is the philosophy that guides research, development, cultivation and documentation.

Every improvement begins with observation and ends by strengthening the foundation for future generations.

# The Seven Pillars of Growth

1. Observe
2. Understand
3. Experience
4. Develop
5. Contribute
6. Share
7. Strengthen

These pillars form one continuous cycle rather than independent steps.

# Experience — The Foundation of Growth

Experience transforms observation into understanding.

Experience allows theory to become practical wisdom and forms the foundation for responsible innovation.

# Introduction

The Project Foundation defines the principles upon which the Microgreens ERP project is built.

# Vision

An honest, transparent and sustainable food chain.

A chain in which technology and nature strengthen one another.

A chain that minimises waste, shares knowledge openly and continuously improves.

A chain that reconnects future generations with the origin of life.

# Mission

To develop an honest and sustainable food chain.

Transparency is the standard.

Innovation and automation are applied with purpose.

Every stage of the food chain is evaluated, improved and documented.

- Sustainability

# Design Philosophy

Technology should adapt to biology, not biology to technology.

Automation exists to support observation, understanding and responsible decision-making rather than replacing them.

# Founder's Notes

# Introduction

The Strategic Foundation translates philosophy into long-term direction.

Each strategy supports the same vision while focusing on a specific discipline.

# Food Chain Strategy

The project aims to strengthen local food production while reducing waste through better cultivation, preservation and knowledge sharing.

Freeze drying is viewed as an enabling technology for efficient storage and transport—not as a replacement for fresh food.

# Research Strategy

Every experiment should increase understanding.

Successful and unsuccessful outcomes are documented to strengthen future decisions.

# AI & Automation Strategy

Artificial intelligence supports analysis.

Automation performs repetitive tasks.

Humans remain responsible for judgement, ethics and long-term direction.

# Human Flourishing

People should earn their living by living, learning, experiencing, creating, contributing and strengthening the foundation for future generations—not by competing with machines.

# Terminology Guide

## Food chain

Use **food chain** for the connected process from cultivation to preservation, storage, transport and use.

Avoid **value chain** unless discussing a specific economic concept.

## Biological Production

The domain concerned with living systems affecting crop health, including pests, diseases, beneficial organisms, microorganisms and biodiversity.

## Cultivation

The practical process of growing crops, including substrates, water, nutrition, lighting, germination, environmental control and harvesting.

## ERP

Enterprise Resource Planning software used to connect production, inventory, labour, sales, sensors and knowledge.

## Living Knowledge Base

The evolving collection of observations, experiments, species information and lessons learned.

## Sustainability

A measurable balance between ecological responsibility, resource efficiency, economic feasibility and long-term resilience.

## Sustaindoity

A project expression describing the point at which sustainability moves from calculation and testing into continuous practical action.

# Editorial Standard

The handbook uses British English.

## Writing principles

- Use sentence case headings.
- Prefer clear and direct sentences.
- Give each document one primary purpose.
- Avoid repeating the same philosophy in multiple places.
- Use cross-references where another document already explains a subject.
- Separate stable principles from changing implementation details.
- Define specialist terms when first introduced.
- Avoid unnecessary marketing language.
- Do not use the word *holistic*.
- Use *food chain* rather than *value chain* where appropriate.

## Markdown principles

- Use one `#` heading for the document title.
- Use `##` for major sections.
- Use `###` only where needed.
- Keep lists focused.
- Use fenced code blocks for diagrams and commands.
- Use relative links between repository documents.

# Revision Policy

The handbook should remain stable enough to serve as a reliable entry point.

## Patch revision

Use a patch revision for:

- Spelling corrections
- Broken links
- Minor formatting improvements
- Clarifications that do not change meaning

Example: `v2.0.1`

## Minor revision

Use a minor revision for:

- New sections
- Improved diagrams
- New knowledge domains
- Significant additions that preserve the existing structure

Example: `v2.1`

## Major revision

Use a major revision when:

- The project structure changes substantially
- The philosophy is redefined
- Major parts are reorganised
- The role of the handbook changes

Example: `v3.0`

Every revision should be recorded in the revision history.
