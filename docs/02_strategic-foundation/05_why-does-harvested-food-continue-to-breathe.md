# Chapter 5 — Why Does Harvested Food Continue to Breathe?

> *Life is organised. Life changes. Life flows. Life moves. Life is sustained through continuous exchange. Preservation begins by understanding that balance.*

## Introduction

A freshly harvested apple continues to consume oxygen.

A lettuce leaf continues to release water.

A potato continues to produce heat.

A seed continues to exchange gases with its surroundings.

To our eyes, these foods appear inactive.

The harvest is complete.

Growth has stopped.

The field has been left behind.

Yet biology continues.

Not because the organism is growing.

But because life does not end the moment it is harvested.

Every living cell remains connected to its surroundings.

Oxygen enters.

Carbon dioxide leaves.

Water is exchanged.

Heat is produced.

Stored nutrients are consumed.

These exchanges never truly stop.

They simply change.

Harvest does not end biological exchange.

It changes the balance between what can still enter and what can no longer be replaced.

This distinction explains one of the greatest paradoxes in food preservation.

The same process that keeps living tissues alive also slowly consumes the resources they contain.

Every breath provides energy.

Every breath also consumes reserves.

Every breath brings the harvest one step closer to deterioration.

To preserve harvested food, we must first understand why living tissues continue to exchange with the world around them.

The answer begins with respiration.

Life exists through continuous exchange.

No living organism is completely separated from its surroundings.

A tree absorbs carbon dioxide while releasing oxygen.

Roots absorb water and dissolved minerals from the soil.

Leaves release water into the atmosphere.

Animals inhale oxygen and exhale carbon dioxide.

Even microorganisms constantly exchange substances with the environment around them.

Life is therefore not a closed system.

It survives because materials and energy continuously move across its boundaries.

This exchange is not optional.

Without it, cells could not obtain nutrients.

Waste products would accumulate.

Energy could not be produced.

Growth would cease.

Repair would become impossible.

Exchange is therefore one of the defining characteristics of life.

Harvest changes this relationship in profound ways.

While attached to the parent plant, a tomato continuously receives water, sugars and nutrients.

The plant replaces resources as quickly as they are consumed.

Harvest interrupts that supply.

The fruit is no longer replenished.

Yet its cells remain alive.

They continue to breathe.

They continue to consume stored sugars.

They continue to exchange gases with the surrounding air.

For a time, the harvest remains biologically active while gradually becoming biologically isolated.

This is the beginning of deterioration.

Not because biology has stopped.

But because biology must now survive using only the resources that remain within the harvested tissue.

Respiration is therefore more than breathing.

It is the biological process that reveals how harvested life continues after harvest.

# Part II — Every Exchange Has a Purpose

If harvested tissues continue to exchange with their surroundings, an important question remains.

Why?

Why does a harvested apple continue to absorb oxygen?

Why does a potato continue to release heat?

Why does fresh lettuce continue to lose weight during storage?

These exchanges are not random.

They all serve the same fundamental purpose.

Every living cell requires energy simply to remain alive.

Even when growth has stopped, cells continue to repair damaged molecules.

Membranes must maintain their organisation.

Enzymes continue to function.

Ions are transported across cell membranes.

Proteins are continually repaired, recycled and replaced.

Life is remarkably active, even when nothing appears to be happening.

This activity comes at a cost.

Energy cannot be created by the cell itself.

It must be released from stored biological compounds.

For harvested plant tissues, these stored compounds are primarily sugars.

The plant carefully produced these sugars through photosynthesis while it was growing.

Harvest changes their purpose.

Instead of being transported throughout the living plant, they become the remaining energy reserve of the harvested tissue.

Respiration is the process through which these reserves are gradually converted into usable energy.

This conversion allows the cells to continue functioning.

But it also slowly consumes the resources that make the harvest valuable.

Every molecule of sugar used for respiration is no longer available to contribute to sweetness, texture or future growth.

The harvest therefore faces an unavoidable dilemma.

To remain alive, it must continue to respire.

Yet every breath slowly reduces the reserves upon which that life depends.

Respiration is therefore both a necessity and a limitation.

It sustains life while gradually consuming it.

Understanding this balance is essential to understanding why harvested food inevitably changes over time.

# Part III — Breathing Without Lungs

When we hear the word *respiration*, most of us immediately think of breathing.

We imagine lungs drawing air into the body and exhaling carbon dioxide.

Plants, however, have no lungs.

Neither does a harvested apple.

A potato does not inhale.

A lettuce leaf does not exhale as an animal does.

Yet every one of these living tissues continues to respire.

This is because respiration is not defined by breathing.

It is defined by the controlled release of energy stored within biological molecules.

Breathing is simply one way of supplying oxygen to support that process.

The process itself occurs inside every living cell.

There, sugars produced during photosynthesis are gradually broken down.

Rather than releasing all their energy at once, cells recover it through a carefully controlled series of chemical reactions.

This allows the energy to be captured and used for biological work.

Without this control, the energy would be released too rapidly, damaging the cell instead of sustaining it.

The products of respiration are remarkably simple.

Energy becomes available for life.

Carbon dioxide is released.

Water is formed.

Heat is generated.

These products are continuously exchanged with the surrounding environment.

The harvested tissue therefore continues to breathe in a biological sense.

It consumes oxygen.

It releases carbon dioxide.

It loses water.

It produces heat.

Every breath is evidence that the cells remain alive.

At the same time, every breath consumes part of the harvest's remaining reserves.

Respiration therefore represents a careful balance.

It provides the energy required to preserve life.

Yet it also slowly reduces the very resources that give harvested food its nutritional and commercial value.

Understanding respiration in this way reveals why preservation is never about stopping biology completely.



It is about guiding biology so that essential life processes continue as slowly and efficiently as possible.

# Part IV — Every Harvest Breathes Differently

If respiration is essential for life, another question immediately follows.

Do all harvested crops respire at the same rate?

The answer is no.

Some harvested products consume their reserves remarkably quickly.

Others continue breathing so slowly that they remain stable for months, or even years.

This difference is one of the most important factors determining shelf life.

Consider freshly harvested lettuce.

Its leaves contain living tissues that remain highly active after harvest.

Respiration continues rapidly.

Water is lost quickly.

Stored sugars are consumed.

Without careful cooling, deterioration becomes visible within days.

Compare this with a potato.

Although still alive, its tissues are far less metabolically active.

Respiration proceeds more slowly.

Stored starch provides a large energy reserve.

When stored under appropriate conditions, potatoes may remain usable for many months.

Dry cereal grains behave differently again.

Their cells enter a state of extremely low biological activity.

Respiration continues, but at such a slow rate that change becomes almost imperceptible.

Seeds take this principle even further.

Many remain alive for years while consuming only tiny amounts of their stored reserves.

Their biological clocks have not stopped.

They have simply slowed to an extraordinary degree.

These differences reveal an important principle.

Shelf life is not determined solely by the product itself.

It is determined by the biological activity that continues within it.

Products with high respiration rates generally have shorter storage lives.

Products with low respiration rates often remain stable for much longer.

This relationship also explains why preservation strategies differ.

A lettuce leaf benefits from rapid cooling immediately after harvest.

A dry grain requires protection from moisture rather than intensive refrigeration.

A potato demands conditions that minimise respiration without triggering unwanted physiological changes such as sprouting.

The biology determines the strategy.

The engineer then selects the technology that best supports that biology.

Successful preservation therefore begins not by asking how long a product should last, but by understanding how actively it continues to live.

# Part V — Changing the Air

Every breath is an exchange.

Oxygen enters.

Carbon dioxide leaves.

Water vapour is released.

Heat is produced.

As long as this exchange continues, respiration continues.

This raises an intriguing question.

What happens if we change the composition of the surrounding air?

At first, the answer appears obvious.

Less oxygen should simply reduce respiration.

In practice, biology is rarely that simple.

Living cells require oxygen to release energy efficiently.

Reducing the oxygen concentration limits their ability to maintain normal respiration.

As a result, many biological processes begin to slow.

Sugars are consumed more gradually.

Heat production decreases.

The biological clocks introduced earlier in this book progress at a slower pace.

For many harvested crops, this extends storage life considerably.

However, oxygen cannot simply be removed.

Below certain levels, cells can no longer produce sufficient energy through normal respiration.

Alternative metabolic pathways become active.

These pathways are far less efficient.

Unwanted compounds may accumulate.

Flavours change.

Textures deteriorate.

The objective is therefore not to eliminate respiration.

It is to guide it.

Carbon dioxide plays an equally important role.

Increasing its concentration often suppresses respiration and slows the growth of many microorganisms.

Yet excessive carbon dioxide may also stress living tissues and reduce product quality.

Successful atmosphere management therefore becomes an exercise in balance.

Too much oxygen accelerates biological change.

Too little oxygen damages normal metabolism.

Too much carbon dioxide may injure the harvest.

Too little provides little benefit.

The atmosphere surrounding harvested food is therefore not simply empty space.

It is part of the biological environment.

Changing that environment changes the exchanges upon which life depends.

Understanding this principle explains why controlled atmosphere storage and modified atmosphere packaging can preserve freshness without freezing or drying.

They do not stop biology.

They create conditions in which biology proceeds more slowly while remaining as healthy as possible.

# Part VI — The Cost of Every Breath

Respiration allows harvested tissues to remain alive.

Without it, cells could no longer produce the energy required for maintenance and repair.

Yet life is never without cost.

Every breath consumes part of the harvest.

Sugars that once served as energy reserves are gradually broken down.

These reserves cannot be replaced.

Harvest has ended the supply from the parent plant.

Every molecule consumed is permanently lost.

Respiration also produces heat.

For a single fruit, the amount may be almost impossible to notice.

For thousands of kilograms stored together, the effect becomes significant.

Without adequate cooling and ventilation, the heat produced by the harvest itself may accelerate further biological activity.

Life literally creates the conditions that allow it to age more rapidly.

Water is also continuously exchanged.

Respiration forms water within cells, while transpiration simultaneously allows water to leave the harvested tissue.

As moisture is lost, leaves wilt.

Fruit softens.

Texture changes.

Mass decreases.

The harvest gradually loses the qualities that define its freshness.

Carbon dioxide continues to accumulate around the product.

Oxygen continues to be consumed.

The atmosphere surrounding the harvest slowly changes together with the biology inside it.

None of these processes occur in isolation.

Each influences the others.

Higher temperatures increase respiration.

Faster respiration produces more heat.

Greater water loss alters texture.

Changing gas concentrations influence metabolism.

The biological system responds continuously to its own activity.

This illustrates one of the central principles of preservation.

Deterioration is rarely the result of a single process.

It emerges from many biological exchanges occurring simultaneously.

Successful preservation therefore depends upon understanding the system as a whole.

Cooling alone is rarely sufficient.

Humidity alone is rarely sufficient.

Atmosphere alone is rarely sufficient.

Successful preservation emerges from managing the biological system as a whole rather than optimising a single condition.

The greatest improvements arise when temperature, water and atmospheric composition are managed together.

The objective is not to stop life.

The objective is to reduce the biological cost of remaining alive.

## Closing Reflection

Life continues long after harvest.

It continues to breathe.

It continues to exchange.

It continues to consume the reserves that once supported growth.

Preservation is therefore not the prevention of change.

It is the careful management of the exchanges that sustain life while slowing the processes that gradually consume it.

Understanding respiration reveals why every harvested crop remains biologically active.

Yet respiration is only one of many biological processes that continue after harvest.

Some reactions require no living cells.

Some continue even when respiration has slowed.

To understand preservation more completely, we must next explore the remarkable world of enzymes.
