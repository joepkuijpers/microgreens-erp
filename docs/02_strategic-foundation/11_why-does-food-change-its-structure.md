# Chapter 11 — Why Does Food Change Its Structure?

> *"Water can leave a living structure. The structure must decide whether it can remain without it. Preservation begins by understanding the difference."*

## Introduction

Two products can lose the same amount of water.

Yet one remains crisp.

The other becomes soft.

One shrinks.

Another cracks.

Some products become brittle.

Others remain flexible.

Why?

The answer is not found in the water alone.

It is found in the biological structure that the water once supported.

Living tissues are not solid objects.

They are organised networks of cells, membranes, fibres and spaces, all held together by water and the molecules surrounding it.

While water occupies these structures, they maintain their shape.

As water leaves, the forces acting within the tissue begin to change.

Cells lose internal pressure.

Membranes deform.

Cell walls experience new mechanical stresses.

Proteins and carbohydrates may rearrange.

The biological organisation slowly changes.

This explains why preservation is not simply about removing water.

It is about preserving structure while water is moving.

Every preservation method therefore faces the same challenge.

Remove enough water to slow biological change.

Preserve enough structure to maintain quality.

Understanding how biological structures respond to water loss reveals why different foods require different preservation strategies.

## Part I — Living Structures Depend on Water

Every living organism possesses structure.

Leaves stand upright.

Fruit remains firm.

Roots resist compression.

Stems support growth.

Although these structures appear solid, they are supported by water.

Inside every living cell, water creates pressure against the cell wall.

This pressure, known as turgor pressure, allows many plant tissues to remain rigid without requiring large amounts of structural material.

Living plants continuously replace the water that is lost to the environment.

Harvest changes that relationship.

Water continues to leave.

Replacement stops.

As water disappears, internal pressure gradually falls.

Cells become less rigid.

The tissue begins to lose the support that water once provided.

Preservation therefore seeks not merely to remove water, but to preserve the relationships that water supports within the biological structure.om er

Understanding this principle explains why freshly harvested vegetables wilt, herbs lose firmness and flowers slowly droop.

The movement of water changes the mechanics of the entire organism.

## Part II — Shape Is a Biological Property

Shape is often mistaken for a permanent characteristic.

In reality, biological shape is actively maintained.

Every cell contributes to the organisation of the tissue surrounding it.

Neighbouring cells support one another through internal pressure, flexible cell walls and microscopic connections.

Together they create structures capable of supporting leaves, stems and fruit.

As water is lost, this cooperation gradually weakens.

Cells become smaller.

Spaces between tissues increase.

Mechanical forces redistribute throughout the structure.

Some tissues bend.

Others wrinkle.

Some collapse entirely.

The visible changes observed after harvest are therefore not random.

They are the natural consequence of a biological structure adapting to the gradual loss of water.

Preservation aims to slow or control these changes before irreversible damage occurs.

## Part III — Not Every Tissue Responds the Same Way

Every biological structure is unique.

A lettuce leaf behaves differently from a wheat grain.

A mushroom responds differently from an apple.

A strawberry differs from a carrot.

These differences arise because each organism has evolved its own architecture.

Some tissues contain large air spaces.

Others possess thick protective layers.

Some depend heavily upon internal water pressure.

Others rely more upon fibres or stored carbohydrates.

As a result, identical environmental conditions produce different outcomes.

The same drying process that preserves one product may damage another.

Successful preservation therefore begins with understanding the biology of the product itself.

Engineering follows biology—not the other way around.

## Part IV — Collapse Is Not Failure

Structural change is often viewed as damage.

Biologically, it is simply a consequence of changing conditions.

Cells cannot maintain the same organisation when one of their most important components is gradually removed.

The resulting changes are entirely predictable.

Leaves wilt.

Fruit softens.

Seeds contract.

Mushrooms shrink.

These responses are not signs that biology has stopped.

They demonstrate that biology continues to obey the same physical and biological principles established throughout this book.

Water moves.

Energy is exchanged.

Maintenance becomes impossible.

Organisation gradually changes.

Preservation seeks to guide these inevitable processes rather than prevent them completely.

The objective is not to stop change.

It is to preserve as much biological organisation as possible.

## Part V — Engineering Biological Architecture

Every preservation method influences structure.

Air drying removes water gradually through evaporation.

Freeze drying removes ice by sublimation.

Vacuum drying lowers the pressure required for water to escape.

Refrigeration slows movement without removing significant amounts of water.

Packaging changes the surrounding environment.

Although these technologies appear very different, they all address the same challenge.

How can water move while biological organisation remains intact?

This question lies at the heart of preservation engineering.

Technology does not create quality.

Quality is preserved only when engineering respects the biological structure of the organism itself.

The most successful preservation methods therefore begin with biology and adapt technology accordingly.

## Closing Reflection

Water gives life its flexibility.

It supports shape without becoming part of the structure itself.

When water moves, structure responds.

Every preserved product tells the story of how successfully its biological architecture endured that journey.

Preservation is therefore not the removal of water.

It is the preservation of biological architecture while water finds a new balance.
