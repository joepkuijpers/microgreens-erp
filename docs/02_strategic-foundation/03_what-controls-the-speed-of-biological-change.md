# Chapter 3 — What Controls the Speed of Biological Change?

> *Life does not simply contain water. Life happens through water.*

## Introduction

A freshly harvested leaf may contain more than ninety percent water.

Yet when we hold that leaf in our hands, we rarely think about water itself.

We notice its colour.

Its texture.

Its aroma.

Its freshness.

Water seems almost invisible.

It is so common that we seldom stop to ask why it matters.

If most living organisms contain large amounts of water, is water simply filling the spaces between cells?

Or does it serve a far more fundamental purpose?

Imagine removing every molecule of water from a living plant without changing anything else.

The cells would still be present.

The membranes would remain.

Proteins, DNA and countless other biological structures would still exist.

Yet life would no longer continue.

Not because the structures had disappeared.

But because the processes that made those structures function had come to a halt.

This distinction is profound.

Life is not defined only by what it is made of.

Life is defined by what it is able to do.

Almost every one of those processes depends upon water.

The biological clocks introduced in the previous chapter do not simply run through time.

They run through water.

To understand preservation, we must therefore understand the medium that allows biology itself to function.

# Part I — Water Is Everywhere

Water is so familiar that it often becomes invisible.

We drink it.

Wash with it.

Cook with it.

Watch it fall as rain and flow through rivers.

Because it is everywhere, it is easy to overlook its importance.

Yet when we look closely at living systems, an intriguing pattern begins to emerge.

Plants are largely composed of water.

Animals are largely composed of water.

Microorganisms depend upon water.

Even seeds, which appear completely dry, still contain small amounts of water essential for their survival.

Wherever life exists, water is present.

This observation is so consistent that it almost seems obvious.

Yet obvious observations often hide the most important questions.

Why should organisms with completely different structures, environments and evolutionary histories all depend so heavily upon the same substance?

A towering oak tree and a microscopic bacterium share remarkably little in appearance.

One may live for centuries.

The other may divide within minutes.

Yet both rely upon water.

This cannot be coincidence.

The answer is not simply that water is abundant on Earth.

Many substances are abundant.

Very few are essential to every known form of life.

Water is different.

Not because living organisms are filled with it.

But because life has evolved around the unique properties that water provides.

Recognising that water is everywhere is only the beginning.

The more important question is why.

Why has life become so dependent upon a substance that, at first glance, appears so ordinary?

# Part II — Water Is Never Just Water

If water were simply a liquid, life could almost certainly have evolved around another substance.

Yet despite the enormous diversity of life on Earth, every known organism depends upon water.

This remarkable consistency suggests that water possesses qualities unlike those of almost any other molecule.

At first glance, this seems surprising.

Water is colourless.

It has no distinctive shape.

It appears simple enough to be described by a formula containing only three atoms.

Few substances seem less remarkable.

Yet appearances can be deceptive.

The importance of water lies not in what it looks like, but in how it behaves.

Unlike most liquids, water readily surrounds and transports dissolved substances.

Nutrients move through it.

Minerals travel within it.

Waste products are carried away by it.

Signals between cells often depend upon it.

Life is not built upon isolated structures.

It is built upon movement.

Water makes that movement possible.

Water also stores heat remarkably well.

As environmental temperatures rise and fall, water absorbs and releases large amounts of energy without changing temperature as rapidly as many other substances.

This property protects living systems from sudden environmental fluctuations that would otherwise disrupt countless biological processes.

At the microscopic level, water behaves in equally remarkable ways.

Water molecules attract one another.

They also interact readily with many biological molecules.

These interactions allow cells to maintain their structure, enable compounds to dissolve, support transport across membranes and create the environment in which enzymes perform their work.

None of these properties exists in isolation.

Together they create conditions under which life can function.

Without them, cells would become little more than collections of disconnected molecules.

This is why water is never simply water.

It is an active participant in almost every biological process.

The structures of life provide the machinery.

Water allows that machinery to operate.

This changes the way we should think about living systems.

Biology does not merely contain water.

Biology depends upon the unique behaviour of water.

# Part III — Life Happens Through Water

Imagine a city in which every road suddenly disappears.

The buildings would remain.

The people would still be present.

The machines would still exist.

Yet nothing could move.

Food could not be delivered.

Waste could not be removed.

Communication would cease.

The city would not disappear.

It would simply stop functioning.

Life without water is remarkably similar.

The structures of a living organism may still exist.

Cells remain.

Proteins remain.

DNA remains.

Yet without water, the countless interactions that define life can no longer occur.

This distinction is fundamental.

Life is not simply built from biological structures.

Life emerges from the continuous interactions between those structures.

Water provides the environment in which those interactions become possible.

Nutrients dissolve before they can be transported.

Molecules diffuse before they can reach their destination.

Enzymes encounter their substrates because both move within an aqueous environment.

Membranes regulate movement through water.

Signals pass between cells through water.

Energy-producing reactions occur in water.

Growth occurs in water.

Repair occurs in water.

Even deterioration occurs in water.

Water is therefore far more than a component of living organisms.

It is the medium through which biology operates.

Remove water, and biological structures may remain for a time.

Remove water, and biology itself can no longer continue.

This explains why the biological clocks introduced in the previous chapter depend so strongly upon water.

Every clock represents biological activity.

Almost every biological activity requires water.

Change the availability of water, and the speed of those clocks begins to change.

The question therefore is no longer whether water is important.

The real question is how changes in water alter the behaviour of living systems.

# Part IV — Not All Water Is Available

At first, the solution to slowing biological change appears straightforward.

If water enables biology, then removing water should stop biology.

To some extent, this is true.

Drying has been used for thousands of years to preserve food.

As water is removed, biological activity slows.

Microorganisms struggle to grow.

Enzymes become less active.

Many of the biological clocks introduced in the previous chapter begin to tick more slowly.

Yet this explanation soon encounters an important problem.

Many foods that appear dry still contain considerable amounts of water.

Dried fruit is not completely dry.

Grains contain water.

Seeds contain water.

Even freeze-dried products retain a small percentage of moisture.

If water is still present, why do these products remain remarkably stable?

The answer lies in a distinction that is both subtle and fundamental.

Not all water is available.

Some water moves freely through biological tissues.

It transports dissolved compounds.

It supports chemical reactions.

It remains accessible to microorganisms and enzymes.

Other water behaves differently.

It is held tightly by proteins, carbohydrates and other biological structures.

Although it is still present, it is no longer freely available to participate in the biological processes that sustain life.

This changes the question completely.

The important question is no longer,

*"How much water is present?"*

Instead, we ask,

*"How much of that water is available to biology?"*

This single distinction explains many observations that would otherwise appear contradictory.

A fresh leaf contains abundant available water and remains biologically active.

A dry seed still contains water, yet most biological activity has slowed to an extraordinary degree.

A freeze-dried product retains moisture, but the remaining water is insufficient to support most biological processes.

The amount of water alone cannot explain these differences.

Its availability can.

Food scientists describe this concept as **water activity**.

Water activity does not measure how much water a product contains.

It measures how much water is available to support biological and chemical processes.

This makes water activity one of the most powerful concepts in preservation.

It explains why products with similar moisture contents may have completely different shelf lives.

It explains why microorganisms flourish in some environments but remain inactive in others.

# Part V — Measuring Available Water

If water availability determines the speed of biological change, another question naturally follows.

How can something as seemingly invisible as available water be measured?

Looking at a product is not enough.

Two foods may appear equally dry while behaving completely differently during storage.

One may remain stable for months.

The other may quickly develop mould, lose quality or undergo unwanted chemical changes.

The difference cannot always be seen.

It must be measured.

This is the purpose of water activity.

Unlike moisture content, which measures the total amount of water present, water activity measures the fraction of water that is available to participate in biological and chemical processes.

This distinction is essential.

Imagine two sponges containing exactly the same amount of water.

One releases water easily when gently squeezed.

The other holds its water tightly within its structure.

Although both contain the same quantity of water, they do not make that water equally available.

Biological materials behave in much the same way.

The amount of water alone tells only part of the story.

Its availability tells us far more about how biology will behave.

For this reason, water activity has become one of the most valuable measurements in food preservation.

Rather than asking,

*"How wet is this product?"*

it asks,

*"How much of its water can biology actually use?"*

This single measurement helps predict whether microorganisms can grow.

It helps estimate the speed of enzymatic reactions.

It influences texture, stability and shelf life.

Most importantly, it allows preservation to move beyond observation and towards prediction.

Instead of waiting to see whether a product remains stable, we can estimate its biological behaviour before deterioration becomes visible.

Measurement therefore changes preservation in an important way.

Observation tells us what has already happened.

Measurement helps us understand what is likely to happen next.

This transforms preservation from reacting to biological change into anticipating it.

Most importantly, it explains why successful preservation depends not simply on removing water, but on controlling its availability.

# Part VI — Guiding the River

Understanding water changes how we think about engineering.

If biology happens through water, then every attempt to preserve food is, in some way, an attempt to guide the movement and availability of water.

This does not mean removing as much water as possible.

Nor does it mean keeping as much water as possible.

Neither approach is universally correct.

Every biological system has its own balance.

Fresh lettuce depends upon abundant available water to remain crisp.

Seeds remain stable because only a small fraction of their water is biologically available.

Freeze-dried foods preserve much of their original structure by removing almost all available water while leaving the biological architecture largely intact.

Different products require different strategies.

Yet the engineering objective is always the same.

Guide the biological system towards stability while preserving the qualities that matter most.

This reflects a fundamental engineering principle.

Engineering does not seek to force biology into stability.

Instead, it seeks to create conditions in which biological change naturally slows while preserving the organisation that gives the system its value.

Sometimes this is achieved by lowering the temperature.

Lower temperatures reduce molecular movement, slowing many biological clocks simultaneously.

Sometimes the most effective approach is drying.

As available water decreases, enzymes become less active, microorganisms struggle to grow and many chemical reactions proceed more slowly.

In other situations, controlling humidity prevents products from gaining or losing water during storage.

Protective packaging reduces unwanted exchange with the surrounding environment.

Modified atmospheres alter the conditions under which biological processes continue.

Each method influences water differently.

Each changes the environment in which biology operates.

This reveals an important principle.

Engineering does not directly control life.

Engineering controls the conditions under which life continues.

The biological response follows naturally.

Seen from this perspective, preservation technologies are not isolated techniques.

They are different ways of guiding the same river.

Some slow its current.

Some redirect its flow.

Others create temporary barriers that reduce its influence.

None changes the nature of the river itself.

Successful preservation therefore begins with understanding.

Only after we understand how water supports biological change can we decide how best to guide it.

Technology does not replace biology.

It works with biology.

The better we understand the river, the more carefully we can guide its course.

## Closing Reflection

Life is organised.

Life changes.

Life flows.

Organisation gives biology its structure.

Time allows biology to change.

Water allows biology to function.

Preservation is therefore more than slowing time.

It is understanding the conditions that allow life to continue and carefully guiding them towards stability.

Before we can guide those conditions with precision, however, we must understand another force that influences almost every biological process.

Energy.
