# Chapter 28 — How Does the Human Body Use Food?

> *"Food is not consumed simply to satisfy hunger. It provides the materials and energy from which life is continuously rebuilt."*

## Introduction

Every stage of the food chain exists for a single purpose.

To support life.

Plants capture energy from sunlight and build biological structures.

Farmers cultivate these plants.

Food scientists preserve them.

Packaging protects them.

Logistics transports them.

Cooking prepares them.

Yet none of these stages represents the final destination of food.

The journey continues inside the human body.

Once food is eaten, it becomes part of another remarkable biological system.

The body does not simply store food.

It breaks it apart.

Sorts its components.

Absorbs useful molecules.

Transforms them into energy, new tissues and countless biological compounds required for life.

Every heartbeat.

Every breath.

Every thought.

Every movement.

Depends upon this continuous transformation.

The human body is therefore not separate from the food chain.

It is the final living system within it.

Understanding nutrition begins with understanding this relationship.

Food is not merely fuel.

Nor is it simply a collection of nutrients.

It is the source from which the body continuously builds, repairs and maintains itself.

## Closing Reflection

To understand how food supports life, we must first understand what the body actually requires.

Although every food differs, the needs of the body remain remarkably consistent.

Energy.

Water.

Building materials.

Regulatory compounds.

Together they make life possible.

Every cell within the body performs specialised functions.

Despite these differences, all cells share the same fundamental requirements.

They require energy to perform work.

They require materials from which new structures can be built.

They require a carefully regulated environment in which life can continue.

## Part I — What Every Cell Requires

The human body is composed of trillions of cells.

Although these cells perform very different functions, they share the same fundamental needs.

A nerve cell transmits electrical signals.

A muscle cell contracts.

A liver cell carries out countless chemical reactions.

A skin cell forms a protective barrier.

Despite their differences, every cell depends upon the same biological principles.

First, every cell requires energy.

Energy powers movement, transport, growth and communication.

Even while the body is resting, cells continuously consume energy simply to maintain the conditions necessary for life.

Second, every cell requires building materials.

Cells are not permanent structures.

Proteins are continuously replaced.

Cell membranes are repaired.

New cells are formed as old ones die.

This constant renewal depends upon a continuous supply of raw materials obtained from food.

Third, every cell requires water.

Water forms the environment in which almost every biological reaction occurs.

It transports nutrients.

Removes waste products.

Helps regulate temperature.

Maintains the structure of tissues throughout the body.

Finally, every cell depends upon regulation.

Thousands of chemical reactions must occur at the correct time, in the correct place and at the correct speed.

This regulation depends upon vitamins, minerals, hormones, enzymes and countless signalling molecules working together in a highly coordinated system.

These four requirements are universal.

Energy.

Building materials.

Water.

Regulation.

Every food consumed ultimately contributes to one or more of these needs.

Some foods primarily provide energy.

Others supply structural components.

Many contribute vitamins, minerals and other compounds that support regulation throughout the body.

Nutrition therefore is not simply the study of nutrients.

It is the study of how food enables every cell to perform its function.

The remarkable complexity of the human body begins with these surprisingly simple biological requirements.

Every meal, regardless of its origin, ultimately serves the needs of trillions of living cells.

Food contains many different substances.

The body, however, cannot use most of them immediately.

Before nutrients can support the needs of individual cells, they must first be released from the food itself.

This process begins with digestion.

## Part II — Digestion: Making Food Available

Eating food is only the beginning of nutrition.

The nutrients contained within food are often locked inside complex biological structures.

Plant cells are surrounded by cell walls.

Proteins are folded into intricate shapes.

Starches exist as tightly organised granules.

Fats are stored in specialised droplets.

Before the body can use these substances, they must first be released.

This process is known as digestion.

Digestion is not a single event.

It is a coordinated sequence of physical and chemical processes that gradually transform food into molecules small enough to be absorbed.

The process begins in the mouth.

Chewing breaks food into smaller pieces, increasing the surface area available for digestion.

Saliva moistens the food and contains enzymes that begin the digestion of certain carbohydrates.

After swallowing, the food passes through the oesophagus into the stomach.

Here, muscular movements mix the food with gastric secretions.

The acidic environment helps unfold proteins, making them more accessible to digestive enzymes.

The stomach also contributes to reducing many microorganisms that enter with the food.

From the stomach, partially digested food enters the small intestine.

This is the principal site of digestion and nutrient absorption.

Digestive enzymes from the pancreas and enzymes produced within the intestinal lining break carbohydrates into simple sugars, proteins into amino acids and fats into fatty acids and glycerol.

Bile, produced by the liver and stored in the gallbladder, helps disperse dietary fats into tiny droplets.

This greatly increases the surface area available for fat digestion.

By the time food leaves the small intestine, much of its nutritional content has been converted into forms that can cross the intestinal wall and enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system.

The remaining material continues into the large intestine.

Here, water and certain minerals are absorbed.

The intestinal microbiota also ferment components of food that escaped digestion, producing compounds that contribute to human health.

Digestion therefore is far more than the breakdown of food.

It is the process by which the body transforms complex biological materials into molecules that can support life.

Only after digestion can food truly become part of the body itself.

Digestion releases nutrients from food.

Releasing nutrients, however, is not enough.

They must still cross the intestinal wall and enter the body's transport system.

The next stage of nutrition is absorption.

## Part III — Absorption: Crossing the Intestinal Barrier

Digestion makes nutrients available.

Absorption allows the body to use them.

The inside of the digestive tract may appear to be part of the body.

Biologically, however, it is not.

Food travelling through the digestive system remains outside the body's internal environment until it crosses the intestinal wall.

The small intestine forms a remarkable biological barrier.

It must absorb essential nutrients efficiently while preventing many harmful substances from entering the bloodstream.

To achieve this, the inner surface of the small intestine is highly specialised.

Rather than being smooth, it is covered with millions of tiny finger-like projections called villi.

Each villus is itself covered with even smaller structures known as microvilli.

Together, these structures increase the surface area available for absorption enormously.

If the small intestine were completely unfolded, its surface area would cover many tens of square metres.

This vast area allows nutrients to be absorbed rapidly while food passes through the intestine.

Different nutrients cross the intestinal wall in different ways.

Simple sugars and amino acids enter specialised cells before passing into tiny blood vessels.

Most fats follow a different route.

After digestion, fatty acids are absorbed into intestinal cells, where they are reassembled into larger particles and transported through the lymphatic system before eventually entering the bloodstream.

Water also moves continuously across the intestinal wall.

Its movement is largely driven by differences in the concentration of dissolved substances on either side of the intestinal lining.

Minerals and vitamins each have their own transport mechanisms.

Some pass through readily.

Others require specialised transport proteins or are absorbed only under specific physiological conditions.

Absorption is therefore highly selective.

The intestine is not simply a sponge.

It is an active biological interface that carefully regulates what enters the body's internal environment.

Once nutrients have crossed this barrier, they are transported throughout the body.

Only then can individual cells begin to use them for energy, growth, repair and regulation.

The intestinal wall therefore represents one of the most important boundaries in human biology.

It separates the external world from the carefully controlled internal environment upon which life depends.

Crossing the intestinal wall is only the beginning of a nutrient's journey.

Once inside the body, nutrients must be delivered to billions of individual cells.

This transport depends upon one of the body's most remarkable systems.

The circulatory system.

## Part IV — Transport: Delivering Nutrients Throughout the Body

Absorption allows nutrients to enter the body.

Transport ensures they reach the places where they are needed.

The human body contains trillions of cells.

No cell is more than a short distance from a blood vessel.

This vast network allows oxygen, nutrients, hormones and other essential substances to be delivered continuously throughout the body.

At the centre of this transport system is the heart.

With every heartbeat, it pumps blood through thousands of kilometres of blood vessels.

Large arteries carry blood away from the heart.

These divide into progressively smaller vessels until they reach microscopic capillaries.

Capillaries are the smallest blood vessels in the body.

Their thin walls allow substances to move between the blood and the surrounding tissues.

Here, nutrients leave the bloodstream and enter the fluid surrounding individual cells.

Cells absorb the molecules they require while releasing waste products back into the blood.

The circulatory system therefore functions in two directions simultaneously.

It delivers essential substances to tissues.

It also removes carbon dioxide and metabolic waste for recycling or elimination.

Not every nutrient follows exactly the same route.

Most sugars, amino acids, water-soluble vitamins and minerals enter the bloodstream directly after absorption.

The liver receives these nutrients first through the hepatic portal vein.

This allows the liver to regulate nutrient levels, store certain compounds, remove toxins and process many substances before they enter the general circulation.

Most dietary fats follow a different pathway.

After absorption, they enter tiny lymphatic vessels within the intestinal villi.

The lymphatic system transports these fats before they eventually enter the bloodstream near the heart.

This alternative route prevents large fat particles from immediately entering the small blood vessels of the intestine.

Together, the circulatory and lymphatic systems ensure that nutrients are distributed efficiently throughout the body.

Every organ depends upon this continuous movement.

The brain requires glucose and oxygen to maintain its activity.

Muscles receive nutrients to support movement and repair.

The liver processes absorbed compounds.

The kidneys filter waste products from the blood.

No organ functions independently.

Each depends upon the transport system that connects the entire body into one integrated biological network.

Food therefore nourishes the body not simply because nutrients are absorbed, but because they are continuously transported to the cells that require them.

Transport delivers nutrients to every tissue.

Arrival, however, does not guarantee that nutrients are immediately used.

Each cell must decide how these molecules will contribute to its own survival.

Some are converted into energy.

Others become part of new biological structures.

Many participate in the regulation of life itself.

## Part V — Metabolism: The Chemistry of Life

Transport delivers nutrients to every cell.

Metabolism determines how those nutrients are used.

Every living cell is constantly active.

Even during sleep, billions of chemical reactions occur every second.

These reactions allow the body to generate energy, repair tissues, produce new molecules and maintain the conditions necessary for life.

Together, these reactions are known as metabolism.

Metabolism is not a single process.

It is an immense network of interconnected chemical reactions that continuously transforms matter and energy.

Some reactions build.

Others break down.

Both are essential.

The reactions that break down molecules are known as catabolic processes.

During catabolism, large molecules such as carbohydrates, fats and, when necessary, proteins are broken into smaller components.

This releases energy that cells can capture and use.

The reactions that build new molecules are known as anabolic processes.

During anabolism, cells use energy to construct proteins, cell membranes, hormones, DNA and countless other biological structures required for growth, maintenance and repair.

These two processes occur simultaneously.

While one part of a cell breaks down old proteins, another produces new ones.

While some nutrients are converted into energy, others become part of new tissues.

Life depends upon maintaining a balance between these continuous cycles.

The energy released during metabolism is not used directly.

Instead, much of it is temporarily stored in a molecule called adenosine triphosphate, or ATP.

ATP functions as the cell's immediate source of usable energy.

When energy is required for muscle contraction, nerve signalling, active transport or the synthesis of new molecules, ATP provides it.

For this reason, ATP is often described as the energy currency of the cell.

Like money in an economy, ATP is constantly produced, spent and regenerated.

The body stores only a very small amount at any one time.

Instead, it must be produced continuously to meet the demands of living cells.

Metabolism is carefully regulated.

Hormones, enzymes and signalling molecules ensure that nutrients are used efficiently according to the body's needs.

After a meal, excess nutrients may be stored for later use.

During fasting or exercise, stored reserves can be mobilised to provide energy.

This flexibility allows the body to adapt to changing circumstances while maintaining a remarkably stable internal environment.

Metabolism therefore is far more than the conversion of food into energy.

It is the process through which the body continuously renews itself.

Every heartbeat.

Every breath.

Every thought.

Every movement.

Every act of healing.

Depends upon the countless metabolic reactions occurring within trillions of cells every moment of every day.

Metabolism explains how nutrients are transformed.

Different nutrients, however, contribute in different ways.

Some primarily provide energy.

Others become structural components.

Many regulate the reactions upon which metabolism depends.

Understanding these roles begins with the body's major nutrients.

## Part VI — The Major Nutrients

Every nutrient contributes to life.

No single nutrient, however, can fulfil every requirement of the human body.

Just as a building requires different materials for its construction, the body depends upon a wide variety of substances that work together.

These substances are known as nutrients.

Although nutrients differ greatly in their structure and function, they can be grouped into several major categories.

Each supports life in a different way.

The first group consists of the macronutrients.

These are carbohydrates, fats and proteins.

They are required in relatively large amounts because they provide energy, building materials or both.

Carbohydrates are an important source of readily available energy.

Many tissues, including the brain under normal conditions, depend heavily upon glucose produced from carbohydrates.

Fats provide a concentrated source of stored energy.

They also form cell membranes, protect organs, insulate the body and enable the absorption of certain vitamins.

Proteins perform an extraordinary variety of functions.

They form muscles, skin, enzymes, hormones, antibodies and countless other biological structures.

Unlike carbohydrates and fats, proteins are not stored specifically for later use.

A continuous dietary supply of amino acids is therefore important to support the body's ongoing maintenance and repair.

The second group consists of the micronutrients.

These include vitamins and minerals.

Although they are required in much smaller quantities, their importance cannot be measured by weight alone.

Many vitamins and minerals enable enzymes to function correctly.

Others contribute to bone formation, oxygen transport, nerve signalling, immune function and the regulation of metabolism.

Without them, many essential biological processes would slow or cease altogether.

Water forms a category of its own.

Although often overlooked as a nutrient, it is the most abundant substance within the human body.

Water provides the environment in which nearly all metabolic reactions occur.

It transports nutrients, removes waste products, regulates body temperature and supports the structure of every tissue.

Finally, food contains many biologically active compounds that are not traditionally classified as essential nutrients.

These include dietary fibre, polyphenols, carotenoids and countless other phytochemicals produced by plants.

Many continue to be actively studied because of their potential contributions to long-term health.

Together, these nutrient groups allow the body to grow, repair itself, regulate its internal environment and respond to changing conditions.

Nutrition is therefore not about a single nutrient.

It is about the coordinated action of many substances working together to support the complexity of human life.

Although nutrients can be grouped into categories, their roles often overlap.

Carbohydrates provide energy.

Fats store energy and form cellular structures.

Proteins build tissues while also performing countless specialised functions.

To understand nutrition more fully, each of these major nutrients deserves closer examination.

## Part VII — Carbohydrates: The Body's Readily Available Energy

Every cell requires energy.

Carbohydrates are one of the body's principal sources of that energy.

Plants produce carbohydrates during photosynthesis.

Using energy captured from sunlight, they combine carbon dioxide and water to form sugars that serve as both an energy source and a structural material.

When humans consume plant-derived foods, much of this stored energy becomes available to the body.

During digestion, many carbohydrates are broken down into simple sugars.

The most important of these is glucose.

Glucose circulates through the bloodstream and is transported into cells, where it can be used to produce ATP, the molecule that powers cellular activities.

Many organs are able to use a variety of energy sources.

The brain, however, normally relies heavily on glucose to support its continuous activity.

Red blood cells depend almost entirely on glucose because they lack the structures needed to generate energy from fats.

For this reason, maintaining an adequate supply of glucose is essential for normal physiological function.

Not all carbohydrates are digested at the same rate.

Simple sugars are absorbed rapidly and may produce a relatively quick rise in blood glucose levels.

More complex carbohydrates, such as starches, generally require additional digestion before they can be absorbed.

The physical structure of food also influences this process.

Whole grains, legumes and many vegetables contain carbohydrates enclosed within plant tissues.

Because these structures take longer to break down, glucose is often released more gradually.

The body carefully regulates blood glucose concentration.

After a meal, the hormone insulin helps cells absorb glucose from the bloodstream.

When blood glucose falls between meals, hormones such as glucagon stimulate the release of stored glucose from the liver.

Together, these regulatory systems help maintain a stable supply of energy despite changes in food intake.

When more carbohydrates are consumed than are immediately required, some glucose is stored as glycogen.

Glycogen is found primarily in the liver and skeletal muscles.

These stores provide a readily accessible source of energy during periods of fasting or increased physical activity.

If glycogen stores become full and energy intake continues to exceed the body's needs, excess energy may eventually be converted into fat for long-term storage.

Carbohydrates therefore serve a dynamic role within human metabolism.

They provide immediate energy, contribute to short-term energy storage and support the continuous function of many tissues throughout the body.

Their importance lies not simply in the amount consumed, but in how they interact with the body's carefully regulated systems for maintaining energy balance.

Carbohydrates provide energy that is readily available.

The body, however, also requires a way to store larger amounts of energy for times when food is not immediately available.

This role is performed primarily by fats.

Far more than an energy reserve, fats are essential components of every living cell.

## Part VIII — Fats: Energy, Structure and Signalling

Fats are among the most versatile substances in human biology.

Although they are widely recognised as a concentrated source of stored energy, their functions extend far beyond energy storage.

Every cell in the human body depends upon fats.

Chemically, most dietary fats are known as triglycerides.

Each triglyceride consists of one glycerol molecule attached to three fatty acids.

The structure of these fatty acids determines many of the physical and biological properties of the fat.

During digestion, fats are broken down into fatty acids and glycerol.

These molecules are absorbed by the small intestine before being reassembled and transported through the lymphatic system into the bloodstream.

From there, they are delivered to tissues throughout the body.

One of the body's most important uses for fats is energy storage.

Fat contains more than twice as much energy per gram as carbohydrates or proteins.

This high energy density allows the body to store substantial energy reserves without requiring excessive weight or volume.

When energy intake exceeds immediate needs, excess energy is commonly stored as body fat.

During periods of fasting, prolonged exercise or limited food availability, these stores can be mobilised to provide energy for many tissues.

Fats also perform essential structural functions.

Every cell membrane is built from a double layer of specialised fat molecules called phospholipids.

These membranes separate the internal environment of the cell from its surroundings while allowing controlled communication with the outside world.

Without fats, cells could not maintain their structure or regulate the movement of substances across their membranes.

Certain fats also serve as signalling molecules.

Some are converted into compounds that help regulate inflammation, blood clotting, immune responses and many other physiological processes.

Others contribute to hormone production, including several steroid hormones that influence growth, reproduction and metabolism.

Fats are equally important for the absorption of several vitamins.

Vitamins A, D, E and K dissolve in fat rather than water.

Without adequate dietary fat, the body cannot efficiently absorb these essential nutrients.

Not all fatty acids can be produced by the human body.

Some, known as essential fatty acids, must be obtained through the diet.

These include omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids, which contribute to normal growth, brain function, vision and the regulation of many biological processes.

The body requires both families of essential fatty acids.

Maintaining an appropriate balance between them supports healthy physiological function.

Fats also provide physical protection.

Adipose tissue cushions internal organs against mechanical injury and helps insulate the body, reducing heat loss in colder environments.

Far from being an inactive storage tissue, body fat is metabolically active.

It produces hormones and signalling molecules that influence appetite, energy balance and many other aspects of physiology.

Fats therefore are far more than stored calories.

They store energy.

Build cells.

Protect organs.

Support communication between tissues.

Enable vitamin absorption.

Help regulate countless biological processes.

Understanding these diverse roles reveals why fats are not optional components of the diet, but essential contributors to human life.

Carbohydrates primarily supply readily available energy.

Fats provide concentrated energy storage and perform numerous structural and regulatory functions.

The body, however, also requires molecules capable of constructing and repairing its tissues.

These molecules are proteins.

## Part IX — Proteins: Building and Maintaining Life

Every living cell contains proteins.

From the muscles that allow movement to the enzymes that drive metabolism, proteins form the foundation of countless biological processes.

Although proteins can provide energy when necessary, this is not their primary role.

Their greatest importance lies in building, maintaining and regulating life.

Proteins are constructed from smaller molecules called amino acids.

Twenty different amino acids are commonly used by the human body to build thousands of unique proteins.

The sequence in which these amino acids are arranged determines the structure and function of each protein.

Some amino acids can be produced by the body.

Others cannot.

These are known as essential amino acids and must be obtained through the diet.

A sufficient intake of all essential amino acids allows the body to synthesise the proteins required for normal growth, maintenance and repair.

Once absorbed from the small intestine, amino acids enter the bloodstream and are transported to tissues throughout the body.

Individual cells select the amino acids they require and assemble them into new proteins according to instructions encoded within their DNA.

This process occurs continuously.

Proteins are constantly being broken down, recycled and replaced.

Unlike fats and carbohydrates, the body has no specialised storage system for excess protein.

Instead, proteins exist primarily as functional components of tissues and cells.

For this reason, a regular dietary supply of amino acids is important.

The functions of proteins are remarkably diverse.

Structural proteins provide strength to tissues such as skin, tendons and bones.

Contractile proteins enable muscles to shorten and generate movement.

Enzymes accelerate the chemical reactions that sustain metabolism.

Transport proteins carry substances such as oxygen, nutrients and minerals throughout the body.

Hormonal proteins coordinate communication between organs.

Antibodies help recognise and defend against infectious microorganisms.

Many receptors on the surfaces of cells are also proteins.

These receptors allow cells to detect hormones, nutrients and signals from neighbouring cells, enabling the body to respond to changes in its internal and external environment.

Proteins therefore are involved in nearly every aspect of human physiology.

During periods of growth, illness or recovery from injury, the demand for protein often increases as new tissues are formed and damaged structures are repaired.

When dietary energy is insufficient, proteins may also be broken down to provide energy.

Although this helps sustain life during prolonged food shortage, it also reduces the availability of proteins for their primary biological functions.

The body therefore preferentially uses carbohydrates and fats as energy sources whenever possible, preserving proteins for the specialised roles they perform.

Proteins are far more than nutrients.

They are the working molecules of life.

They build.

Repair.

Transport.

Communicate.

Defend.

Catalyse.

Without proteins, the countless processes that sustain human life could not occur.

Carbohydrates, fats and proteins provide energy, structure and the materials required to sustain life.

Yet these nutrients cannot function efficiently on their own.

Thousands of metabolic reactions depend upon much smaller substances that guide and regulate the chemistry of the body.

These are the vitamins and minerals.

## Part X — Vitamins and Minerals: Regulating Life

Carbohydrates, fats and proteins provide energy and building materials.

On their own, however, they are not enough to sustain life.

The countless chemical reactions occurring within the human body depend upon a much smaller group of substances.

These are the vitamins and minerals.

Although required only in small quantities, their importance cannot be measured by their mass.

Without them, many of the body's essential processes would slow, become inefficient or stop altogether.

Vitamins are organic compounds produced primarily by plants and microorganisms.

Humans cannot synthesise most vitamins in sufficient quantities and must therefore obtain them through the diet.

Each vitamin performs specific biological functions.

Many act as coenzymes, assisting enzymes that drive the thousands of chemical reactions required for metabolism.

Others protect cells from damage, regulate gene expression or contribute to normal growth and development.

Vitamins can be divided into two groups.

Water-soluble vitamins, including the B vitamins and vitamin C, dissolve readily in water.

Because they are not stored extensively, a regular dietary supply is generally required.

Fat-soluble vitamins—A, D, E and K—are absorbed together with dietary fats and can be stored within the body for longer periods.

Minerals differ from vitamins because they are inorganic elements.

They originate from rocks and soil, enter plants through their roots and eventually become part of the food chain.

Animals obtain these minerals by consuming plants or other animals.

Some minerals are required in relatively large amounts.

These are known as major minerals and include calcium, phosphorus, potassium, sodium and magnesium.

Others are required only in trace amounts.

These trace elements, such as iron, zinc, iodine, selenium and copper, are no less important despite their lower concentrations.

Minerals contribute to an extraordinary range of biological functions.

Calcium and phosphorus strengthen bones and teeth.

Iron enables red blood cells to transport oxygen.

Sodium and potassium help generate the electrical signals required for nerve impulses and muscle contraction.

Magnesium participates in hundreds of enzymatic reactions.

Zinc supports immune function, wound healing and protein synthesis.

Iodine is essential for the production of thyroid hormones that regulate metabolism.

The body carefully controls the concentration of these minerals.

Both deficiency and excessive intake may interfere with normal physiological function.

For this reason, maintaining a varied and balanced diet is generally more effective than focusing on individual nutrients in isolation.

Vitamins and minerals rarely work alone.

They interact continuously with proteins, carbohydrates, fats, water and one another.

Many enzymes require both a protein component and a vitamin or mineral before they become fully functional.

Nutrition therefore is not simply the presence of nutrients.

It is the coordinated interaction of many substances working together within a highly organised biological system.

Although vitamins and minerals are required only in small amounts, they make possible the countless reactions upon which human life depends.

Every nutrient discussed so far functions within a common environment.

Carbohydrates are transported through it.

Proteins are dissolved within it.

Minerals move through it.

Metabolic reactions occur inside it.

That environment is water.

Without water, none of these processes could continue.

## Part XI — Water: The Universal Medium of Life

Water is the most abundant substance in the human body.

It accounts for more than half of an adult's body mass, although the exact proportion varies with age, sex and body composition.

Every cell depends upon water.

Without it, life as we know it could not exist.

Unlike carbohydrates, fats and proteins, water provides no energy.

Instead, it creates the environment in which nearly every biological process takes place.

Within cells, water dissolves nutrients, enzymes and countless other molecules.

This allows chemical reactions to occur efficiently and enables substances to move from one location to another.

Without water, metabolism would come to a halt.

Water also serves as the body's primary transport medium.

Blood consists largely of water.

It carries oxygen from the lungs, nutrients from the digestive system and hormones from endocrine glands to tissues throughout the body.

At the same time, it transports carbon dioxide and waste products towards the lungs, kidneys and other organs responsible for their removal.

Temperature regulation depends heavily upon water.

The human body continuously generates heat through metabolism.

To prevent overheating, sweat glands release water onto the surface of the skin.

As this water evaporates, it absorbs thermal energy from the body, helping to maintain a stable internal temperature.

Water also contributes to the physical properties of tissues.

It provides volume to cells.

Lubricates joints.

Protects sensitive organs.

Maintains the flexibility of connective tissues.

Forms the major component of saliva, tears and digestive secretions.

Even the brain and eyes depend upon carefully regulated water content to function normally.

Maintaining the body's water balance requires continuous regulation.

Every day, water is lost through breathing, perspiration, urine and faeces.

These losses increase during physical activity, hot weather and illness.

To compensate, the body generates the sensation of thirst while the kidneys carefully adjust the amount of water excreted in urine.

This regulation helps maintain a relatively constant internal environment despite changing external conditions.

Water also influences the concentration of dissolved substances such as sodium, potassium and glucose.

Even relatively small changes in water balance can alter the function of cells, nerves and muscles.

For this reason, maintaining adequate hydration is essential for normal physiological function.

Although water itself contains no energy, it enables every process through which energy is produced, transported and used.

Every nutrient depends upon water.

Every cell depends upon water.

Every organ depends upon water.

Water is therefore not simply another nutrient.

It is the universal medium in which human life exists.

Carbohydrates, fats, proteins, vitamins, minerals and water together provide the essential requirements for life.

Food, however, contains many other biologically active substances.

Some are not considered essential because life can continue without them.

Nevertheless, many contribute to health, digestion and the complex relationship between humans and plants.

Among the most important are dietary fibre and phytochemicals.

## Part XII — Dietary Fibre and Phytochemicals: Beyond Essential Nutrition

Not every beneficial substance in food is classified as an essential nutrient.

Some compounds are not required for survival in the same way as proteins, vitamins or minerals.

Yet many contribute significantly to normal physiological function and long-term health.

Among the most important are dietary fibre and phytochemicals.

Dietary fibre consists primarily of components of plant cell walls that cannot be fully digested by human digestive enzymes.

Rather than being absorbed in the small intestine, much of this material reaches the large intestine largely unchanged.

For many years, fibre was considered little more than indigestible plant material.

Research has since revealed that it performs numerous important biological functions.

Fibre increases the volume of intestinal contents and helps maintain regular bowel movements.

It slows the absorption of certain nutrients, contributing to a more gradual rise in blood glucose after meals.

Some forms of fibre also bind bile acids and influence cholesterol metabolism.

Perhaps most remarkably, dietary fibre serves as a food source for the microorganisms that inhabit the large intestine.

As these microorganisms ferment certain types of fibre, they produce short-chain fatty acids such as acetate, propionate and butyrate.

These compounds provide energy for cells lining the colon and contribute to maintaining the health of the intestinal environment.

Food also contains thousands of naturally occurring compounds collectively known as phytochemicals.

Unlike vitamins, phytochemicals are not considered essential because deficiency does not produce a specific disease.

Nevertheless, many interact with biological systems in ways that continue to be actively investigated.

Examples include polyphenols, flavonoids, carotenoids, glucosinolates and numerous other plant compounds.

These substances contribute to the colours, flavours and aromas of fruits, vegetables, herbs and spices.

Within plants, many serve protective functions against sunlight, insects, microorganisms and environmental stress.

When consumed by humans, some phytochemicals exhibit antioxidant activity, while others influence cell signalling, gene expression or inflammatory responses.

The precise effects often depend upon the compound, the quantity consumed and the overall dietary pattern.

Scientific understanding of phytochemicals continues to evolve.

Many promising observations have been reported, but the biological interactions are highly complex and remain an active area of research.

For this reason, it is generally more appropriate to obtain these compounds through a varied diet rich in plant foods than to rely upon isolated supplements.

Dietary fibre and phytochemicals illustrate an important principle.

Food is more than the sum of its essential nutrients.

Whole foods contain countless interacting compounds whose combined effects may contribute to health in ways that science is still working to understand.

Nutrition therefore extends beyond preventing deficiency.

It also involves appreciating the remarkable biological complexity present within natural foods.

The body receives nutrients from every meal.

How those nutrients influence health, however, depends on far more than a single food or a single day.

Nutrition is shaped by patterns that develop over weeks, months and years.

Understanding these long-term patterns is essential for understanding health itself.

## Part XIII — Nutrition as a Pattern, Not a Product

Nutrition is often discussed one food at a time.

A fruit is described as healthy.

A snack is considered unhealthy.

A particular nutrient is praised or criticised.

Although these discussions may contain elements of truth, they rarely capture the complexity of human nutrition.

The human body does not respond to a single meal in isolation.

It responds to the combined effects of thousands of meals consumed throughout life.

Every meal contributes nutrients.

Every day influences the body's internal environment.

Over weeks, months and years, these individual choices form dietary patterns that shape long-term health.

A balanced diet does not require every meal to be perfect.

Nor does a single indulgent meal determine future health.

The body is remarkably adaptable.

It continuously adjusts to changing nutrient intake, energy demands and environmental conditions.

This adaptability provides resilience against occasional fluctuations while still responding to long-term habits.

Variety is one of the defining characteristics of a healthy dietary pattern.

Different foods provide different combinations of nutrients.

No single food contains every substance required for optimal health.

By consuming a wide range of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds and other nutritious foods, the body receives a broader spectrum of nutrients and biologically active compounds.

Energy balance also plays an important role.

When energy intake consistently exceeds the body's requirements, excess energy is stored.

When intake remains below demand for prolonged periods, the body begins to use its stored reserves.

Maintaining health therefore depends not only on the quality of food but also on the relationship between energy intake and energy expenditure.

Nutrition is also influenced by lifestyle.

Physical activity, sleep, stress, illness, age and genetics all affect how nutrients are used.

The same meal may influence different individuals in different ways because every human body is unique.

For this reason, nutrition cannot be reduced to simple rules that apply equally to everyone.

Scientific recommendations therefore focus on overall dietary patterns rather than individual foods.

A varied, balanced and sustainable way of eating consistently provides better support for health than repeatedly seeking individual foods believed to offer extraordinary benefits.

Food should not be viewed as isolated ingredients.

It should be understood as part of a lifelong relationship between biology, environment and behaviour.

Healthy nutrition is therefore not created by occasional decisions.

It is created by consistent patterns that support the body's remarkable ability to maintain and renew itself throughout life.

Food supports life.

The body, however, exists within a far broader biological system.

Nutrition interacts continuously with movement, sleep, sunlight, microorganisms, stress and the surrounding environment.

To understand health fully, food must be viewed as one part of an integrated biological whole.
