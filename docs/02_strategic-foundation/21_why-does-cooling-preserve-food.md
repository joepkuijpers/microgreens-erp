# Chapter 21 — Why Does Cooling Preserve Food?

> *"Life depends upon energy. Cooling does not remove life—it slows the pace at which life continues. Preservation begins by understanding this distinction."*

## Introduction

Harvest marks the end of cultivation.

It does not mark the end of biology.

Once separated from the parent organism, fruits, vegetables, fungi and many other biological materials remain active. Cells continue to respire. Enzymes continue to catalyse reactions. Molecules continue to move. Microorganisms continue to compete for nutrients.

These processes once supported growth, development and reproduction.

After harvest, they gradually begin to reshape the food itself.

Sugars are consumed.

Organic acids are transformed.

Pigments fade.

Textures soften.

Aroma compounds change.

Eventually, these natural processes reduce quality and limit shelf life.

Preservation therefore is not about stopping biology.

It is about influencing the environment in which biology continues.

Cooling is one of the simplest and most effective ways of achieving this.

Unlike drying, refrigeration removes nothing from the food.

Water remains.

Cells remain.

Biological organisation remains largely intact.

Instead, cooling changes one fundamental property of the environment.

Temperature.

As temperature decreases, every molecule possesses less thermal energy.

Movement becomes slower.

Collisions become less frequent.

Biological and chemical processes require more time to occur.

Nothing fundamental within the food has changed.

The environment has.

Cooling therefore preserves food by reducing the rate at which biological processes unfold.

Life does not suddenly stop.

It simply continues more slowly.

Understanding this principle forms the foundation of refrigerated preservation.

## Part I — Temperature Changes the Pace of Life

On a warm summer day, fruit ripens quickly.

Freshly harvested vegetables lose their crispness within hours.

Milk spoils sooner.

Bread develops mould more rapidly.

Place these same foods in a refrigerator and the changes become noticeably slower.

The food has not become fundamentally different.

The surrounding environment has.

Temperature influences every biological and chemical process because it changes the amount of thermal energy available within a system.

As temperature increases, molecules move more rapidly.

They collide more frequently.

The probability that reactions occur also increases.

Enzymes encounter their substrates more often.

Respiration accelerates.

Microorganisms reproduce more quickly.

Chemical reactions proceed at a faster rate.

Lowering the temperature produces the opposite effect.

Molecular movement becomes slower.

Collisions occur less frequently.

Every biological process requires more time.

Importantly, cooling does not affect only one process.

It influences nearly every process occurring within the harvested organism.

Respiration slows.

Enzyme activity decreases.

Ripening requires more time.

Microbial growth becomes less rapid.

Many chemical reactions proceed more slowly.

Each responds differently.

Yet they all share one common requirement.

They depend upon molecular movement.

Cooling therefore preserves food through a remarkably simple principle.

It reduces the pace at which biology and chemistry can operate.

Rather than stopping life, it changes the speed at which life continues.

Temperature influences every biological process.

It does not influence every organism equally.

Some continue to grow at surprisingly low temperatures.

Others become damaged long before freezing occurs.

To understand refrigeration, we must first understand that every organism has evolved within its own thermal environment.

## Part II — Every Organism Has Its Own Temperature

Although temperature influences every living organism, it does not influence every organism in the same way.

Throughout evolution, species have adapted to the environments in which they live.

Some developed in tropical forests where temperatures remain high throughout the year.

Others evolved in alpine regions, polar oceans or permanently frozen soils.

Each environment presented different challenges.

Over countless generations, organisms gradually adapted to those conditions.

As a result, every species possesses its own range of temperatures within which biological processes function most efficiently.

Within this range, growth, repair and reproduction can proceed normally.

Outside it, biological performance gradually declines.

If temperatures become too high, proteins lose their functional structure, membranes become unstable and essential metabolic processes begin to fail.

If temperatures become too low, molecular movement decreases, membranes become less fluid and many biological reactions proceed too slowly to sustain normal cellular function.

Neither extreme supports healthy life.

Every organism therefore occupies its own thermal window.

This principle applies equally to plants, animals, fungi and microorganisms.

It also explains why foods respond so differently to refrigeration.

Apples may remain in excellent condition for many months when stored close to 0°C.

Bananas, however, may develop chilling injury at temperatures well above freezing.

Potatoes stored too cold gradually convert starch into sugars, altering both flavour and cooking behaviour.

Leafy vegetables, herbs, mushrooms and berries each respond according to their own biology.

There is no universal storage temperature.

There are only temperatures that are more or less suitable for a particular biological system.

Successful preservation therefore begins by understanding the organism before selecting the environment.

Technology follows biology.

Never the other way around.

Different organisms respond differently to temperature.

Food, however, rarely contains only one living organism.

After harvest, plant tissues, microorganisms and enzymes continue to interact within the same biological environment.

Cooling changes this entire community rather than any single process alone.

## Part III — Cooling Reshapes the Biological Community

Food is never biologically isolated.

After harvest, it remains home to a complex community of living organisms and biological processes.

Plant tissues continue to respire.

Enzymes continue to modify biological structures.

Microorganisms compete for nutrients.

Yeasts, moulds and bacteria interact with one another while responding to the changing environment.

These activities do not occur independently.

Together they form a dynamic biological community.

Within every community, organisms compete for the same limited resources.

Nutrients.

Water.

Space.

Oxygen.

The organisms best adapted to their environment gradually become the most successful.

Cooling changes the rules of this competition.

Lower temperatures reduce the activity of nearly every member of the community.

Respiration slows.

Enzyme activity decreases.

Many microorganisms reproduce less rapidly.

Chemical reactions require more time.

Yet no organism responds in exactly the same way.

Some microorganisms are highly sensitive to cooling.

Others remain remarkably active at temperatures commonly used for refrigeration.

As the environment changes, the balance between competing organisms also changes.

Species that dominated at room temperature may gradually decline.

Others become relatively more successful.

The biological community is therefore continuously reorganised.

Refrigeration does not eliminate this community.

It reshapes it.

Understanding this principle is essential for preservation engineering.

Food quality is rarely determined by a single biological process.

Instead, it emerges from the combined activity of many interacting systems.

Successful preservation therefore depends upon managing the environment in which these systems compete.

Cooling preserves food because it changes the ecological balance of the biological community rather than attempting to eliminate biology itself.

Every biological community responds to its environment.

The food itself is no different.

Plant tissues also possess biological limits.

When temperatures fall below those limits, cooling itself can become a source of stress.

## Part IV — Cold Can Become Stress

Cooling preserves food because it slows biological activity.

Like every environmental factor, however, temperature has limits.

Lower temperatures are not always better.

Every living organism has evolved within a particular thermal environment.

Its cells, membranes, enzymes and metabolic pathways all function most effectively within a specific temperature range.

As long as conditions remain within that range, the organism can regulate itself successfully.

Outside it, maintaining normal biological function becomes increasingly difficult.

When temperatures rise too high, proteins lose their structure, membranes become unstable and essential cellular processes begin to fail.

When temperatures fall too low, a different challenge emerges.

Molecular movement slows.

Cell membranes become less flexible.

The transport of water, nutrients and signalling molecules becomes less efficient.

Metabolic pathways no longer operate in harmony.

Although the tissues remain alive, they begin to experience physiological stress.

Importantly, this stress can develop long before water freezes.

Many tropical and subtropical species are adapted to warm environments throughout their evolutionary history.

For these organisms, temperatures commonly considered safe for refrigeration may already lie outside their natural range.

The result is not freezing damage.

It is a disruption of normal biological function.

This phenomenon is known as chilling injury.

Bananas may darken before they ripen.

Tomatoes may lose aroma and develop an undesirable texture.

Cucumbers may develop sunken areas on their surface.

Avocados may ripen unevenly.

These symptoms differ between species.

Their underlying cause is the same.

The organism has been exposed to temperatures below those to which it has adapted.

Other species respond very differently.

Apples can often be stored close to 0°C for many months with little reduction in quality.

Many leafy vegetables also tolerate low temperatures remarkably well.

Each organism reflects its own evolutionary history.

Successful refrigeration therefore is not achieved by selecting the lowest possible temperature.

It is achieved by selecting the most appropriate temperature for the biology of the organism being preserved.

The purpose of cooling is not to challenge biology.

It is to work within its limits.

Different organisms respond differently to temperature.

Even under ideal temperatures, however, refrigeration cannot completely isolate food from its environment.

Water continues to move.

The surrounding air continues to influence the food.

Understanding this interaction explains why humidity is just as important as temperature during refrigerated storage.

## Part V — Water Continues to Seek Balance

Cooling reduces the pace of biological activity.

It does not prevent water from moving.

Water is never completely at rest.

Within every living tissue, water molecules remain in constant motion.

They move between cells.

They diffuse through tissues.

They evaporate from exposed surfaces.

These movements continue even at refrigeration temperatures.

The only difference is their pace.

Whenever the surface of a food contains more available water than the surrounding air, water begins to leave the food.

The surrounding air accepts water vapour.

The food gradually loses moisture.

This process is slower than at room temperature.

It is not eliminated.

Over time, even small losses become visible.

Leafy vegetables begin to wilt.

Fresh herbs lose their firmness.

Cheese develops a dry outer surface.

Fresh meat gradually loses moisture.

Fruit becomes less crisp.

These changes are not necessarily signs of spoilage.

They are signs that water continues to seek equilibrium with its surroundings.

This principle extends far beyond refrigeration.

It governs drying.

Freeze drying.

Frozen storage.

Even food displayed on a kitchen counter.

Net water transfer tends towards equilibrium.

Successful preservation therefore requires more than controlling temperature.

It also requires controlling the environment into which water moves.

How rapidly that movement occurs depends largely upon the surrounding air.

Humidity determines whether the air readily accepts additional water or resists further exchange.

Understanding humidity therefore becomes essential for understanding refrigerated preservation.

## Part VI — Humidity Determines the Direction of Water

Water can only leave food if the surrounding air is able to receive it.

The ability of air to accept additional water is described by its relative humidity.

At a given temperature, air with a low relative humidity can accept more water vapour before becoming saturated.

Water therefore leaves the food more readily.

At that same temperature, air with a high relative humidity is closer to saturation.

The movement of water slows.

As the surrounding air approaches saturation, very little additional evaporation can occur.

Humidity therefore determines the direction and intensity of water exchange between food and its environment.

This balance is critical during refrigerated storage.

If the air is too dry, food gradually loses moisture.

Leafy vegetables wilt.

Fresh herbs become limp.

Fruit loses firmness.

Products exposed to the air slowly become lighter as water continues to escape.

If the air is excessively humid, a different problem emerges.

Water may begin to condense on the surface of the food.

Instead of losing water, the surface remains persistently wet.

This thin layer of moisture creates favourable conditions for many microorganisms.

The objective of refrigerated storage is therefore not to maximise or minimise humidity.

It is to establish a balance appropriate for the biological characteristics of the food.

Fresh mushrooms require different conditions from onions.

Leafy vegetables differ from cured meats.

Seeds differ from fresh fruit.

Each biological system exchanges water with its environment in its own way.

Successful preservation therefore depends upon matching the humidity of the environment to the biology of the product rather than applying a single universal value.

Humidity does not preserve food by itself.

It guides the movement of water.

In doing so, it protects both biological stability and physical quality.

Temperature determines how quickly biological processes proceed.

Humidity determines how readily water moves.

One environmental factor remains.

Air itself must circulate.

Without movement, every stored product gradually creates its own local environment.

## Part VII — Air Connects Food to Its Environment

The air surrounding food is never completely still.

Even inside a refrigerator, heat, water vapour and gases are continuously exchanged between the food and the surrounding environment.

This exchange does not occur directly.

Immediately surrounding every surface exists a thin layer of air known as the boundary layer.

Although only a few millimetres thick, this layer plays an important role in preservation.

As food respires, heat is released.

As water evaporates, water vapour accumulates within the boundary layer.

Respiration also changes the concentrations of oxygen and carbon dioxide close to the surface.

If the surrounding air remains still, these changes gradually become more pronounced.

The food begins to experience conditions that differ from those elsewhere inside the refrigerator.

Its immediate environment becomes warmer.

More humid.

Lower in oxygen.

Higher in carbon dioxide.

Air movement continually renews this boundary layer.

Heat is removed.

Water vapour is carried away.

Gases become evenly distributed throughout the storage space.

As a result, the food experiences conditions that are more stable and more predictable.

This stability is one of the primary objectives of refrigerated storage.

Airflow therefore does not preserve food by itself.

It maintains the environmental conditions upon which successful preservation depends.

Like every environmental factor, however, airflow requires balance.

Insufficient air movement allows local conditions to drift away from the desired environment.

Excessive airflow accelerates water loss and increases dehydration.

The objective is therefore not maximum airflow.

It is environmental stability.

Temperature slows biological activity.

Humidity regulates water movement.

Airflow maintains environmental uniformity.

Together these three environmental factors create the conditions under which refrigeration can preserve food effectively.

Even under carefully controlled environmental conditions, refrigeration has limits.

Biology continues.

Chemistry continues.

Time continues.

To understand both the strengths and the limitations of refrigeration, we must recognise that cooling slows change.

It does not prevent it.

## Part VIII — Cooling Buys Time

Refrigeration is one of the most successful preservation technologies ever developed.

It allows fresh food to be stored for days, weeks and, for some products, even months.

It slows respiration.

Delays ripening.

Reduces the rate of microbial growth.

Preserves texture.

Maintains colour.

Extends the period during which food remains suitable for consumption.

Yet refrigeration does not stop deterioration.

Every biological and chemical process introduced throughout this book continues.

Respiration may continue at a greatly reduced rate.

Enzymes remain active.

Some microorganisms remain viable, and cold-tolerant species may remain active.

Water continues to seek equilibrium.

Chemical reactions continue to reshape molecules.

The difference lies in their speed.

Cooling does not replace biology.

It changes the pace at which biology operates.

For many foods, this reduction in activity is sufficient to preserve quality for the intended period of storage.

For others, it is not.

Fresh fish remains highly perishable even under refrigeration.

Leafy vegetables continue to lose moisture.

Tropical fruits may experience chilling injury before spoilage becomes the primary concern.

Every biological system possesses its own limitations.

For this reason, refrigeration should never be viewed as a universal solution.

It is one preservation strategy among many.

Its success depends upon the biology of the food, the conditions of storage and the purpose of preservation.

Sometimes refrigeration alone is sufficient.

Sometimes it is combined with drying, modified atmospheres, fermentation or freezing.

Successful preservation therefore is not achieved by selecting the most powerful technology.

It is achieved by selecting the technology most appropriate for the biology of the food.

## Closing Reflection

Cooling demonstrates a simple but profound principle.

Preservation does not require biology to stop.

It requires biology to change more slowly.

By reducing thermal energy, refrigeration slows the countless interactions that continue within every harvested organism.

Cells continue to function.

Enzymes continue to catalyse reactions.

Microorganisms continue to adapt.

Water continues to move.

Time continues to unfold.

The environment has changed.

The biology responds.

This relationship lies at the heart of refrigerated preservation.

Technology does not overcome biology.

It works within the boundaries that biology provides.

Understanding those boundaries allows preservation to become both more effective and more respectful of the living systems upon which it depends.

Cooling slows biological change.

Eventually, however, cooling reaches a fundamental limit.

Water remains liquid.

As long as liquid water is present, biological and chemical processes can continue.

The next step in preservation changes something far more fundamental.

The water itself.

When liquid water becomes ice, biology enters an entirely different environment.

Freezing.
