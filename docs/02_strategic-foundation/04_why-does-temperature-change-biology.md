# Chapter 4 — Why Does Temperature Change Biology?

> *Life happens through water. Energy determines its pace. Preservation begins by understanding both.*

## Introduction

A tomato ripens more quickly on a warm windowsill than inside a refrigerator.

Bread develops mould faster during summer than winter.

Fresh herbs remain crisp for longer when cooled.

These observations are so familiar that we rarely question them.

We simply accept that warmth speeds things up and cold slows things down.

But why?

Temperature cannot see.

It cannot think.

It cannot decide which reactions should become faster and which should become slower.

Yet almost every biological process responds to it.

How can something as simple as temperature influence life so profoundly?

The answer does not lie in temperature itself.

Temperature is not a force that directs biology.

It is a measure of something more fundamental.

The answer lies in energy.

Understanding how energy influences biological systems reveals why temperature affects every living organism, why preservation methods work, and why controlling energy is one of the most powerful tools available in food preservation.

# Part I — Nothing in Biology Stands Still

At first glance, living organisms appear remarkably still.

A tree stands quietly in the landscape.

A leaf rests motionless on a branch.

A seed may lie dormant for months or even years.

Even after harvest, a tomato sitting on a kitchen table appears unchanged from one moment to the next.

Yet appearances can be deceptive.

Within every living organism, movement never truly stops.

Water flows through tissues.

Sugars are transported from one cell to another.

Enzymes continually interact with the molecules around them.

Proteins are assembled and dismantled.

Cell membranes exchange substances with their surroundings.

Even microorganisms too small to be seen continue their own invisible activities.

Life is never static.

It is a continuous process of movement and change.

These movements occur over many different scales.

Some can be observed with the naked eye.

Roots grow deeper into the soil.

Leaves unfold towards the light.

Flowers open and close throughout the day.

Others remain hidden from view.

Individual molecules move through cells.

Atoms vibrate within biological structures.

Chemical reactions occur countless times every second.

Although invisible, these microscopic movements make every visible process possible.

Without them, growth would cease.

Repair would stop.

Communication between cells would end.

Life itself would become impossible.

This constant activity raises an important question.

What keeps everything moving?

Why do molecules continue to move rather than remain perfectly still?

The answer lies in one of the most fundamental concepts in both biology and physics.

Energy.

# Part II — Energy Makes Movement Possible

Imagine placing a cup of hot tea on a kitchen table.

At first, steam rises from its surface.

The cup feels warm to the touch.

Gradually, the tea cools.

After some time, it reaches the same temperature as the surrounding room.

Nothing appears to be happening.

Yet an extraordinary process has taken place.

The tea has not simply become cold.

It has transferred energy to its surroundings.

This simple observation reveals an important principle.

Energy is never static.

It is continually exchanged between objects, organisms and their environment.

The same principle applies throughout biology.

A warm leaf exchanges energy with the surrounding air.

A refrigerated apple continuously gains energy from the room around it.

Frozen food slowly absorbs energy whenever it is removed from the freezer.

Every biological system is constantly exchanging energy with its environment.

But what exactly is energy?

Unlike water, energy cannot be seen or stored in a bottle.

We cannot hold it in our hands.

Instead, we recognise energy through its effects.

Things move.

Temperatures change.

Chemical reactions occur.

Water evaporates.

Cells divide.

Seeds germinate.

Fruit ripens.

Microorganisms grow.

Without energy, none of these processes could occur.

Energy is therefore not another substance inside living organisms.

It is the capacity that allows biological change to take place.

This is why temperature matters.

Temperature does not create energy.

Instead, it tells us something about the average energy of the countless molecules within a material.

As molecular energy increases, movement becomes more vigorous.

Collisions become more frequent.

Chemical reactions become more likely.

As molecular energy decreases, movement slows.

Collisions become less frequent.

Biological processes proceed more slowly.

Temperature is therefore not the driving force behind biology.

It is an indicator of how energetically molecules are moving.

This seemingly simple relationship explains why nearly every preservation technique begins by controlling temperature.

By changing the energy available within a biological system, we change the pace at which biological processes can continue.

# Part III — Life Depends Upon Encounters

Every living organism is built from countless molecules.

Water.

Sugars.

Proteins.

Lipids.

Minerals.

DNA.

Each performs a specific role.

Yet none of these molecules can accomplish very much alone.

An enzyme cannot catalyse a reaction unless it first encounters its substrate.

A nutrient cannot nourish a cell unless it reaches the cell membrane.

A signalling molecule cannot deliver information unless it finds the correct receptor.

Every biological process begins with an encounter.

Imagine a crowded railway station.

Thousands of people move through the building.

Most simply pass one another.

Some briefly make eye contact.

Only a few meet the exact person they came to find.

Biology works in a remarkably similar way.

Inside every cell, countless molecules move continuously through water.

They drift.

They collide.

They separate again.

Most encounters achieve nothing.

Some occur between the right molecules, but in the wrong orientation.

Others happen with too little energy for a reaction to proceed.

Only a small fraction become successful biological events.

Life therefore depends not simply on movement.

It depends upon meaningful encounters.

This is where energy becomes so important.

As molecular energy increases, molecules move more rapidly.

Their paths cross more often.

Collisions become more frequent.

Some collisions also occur with enough energy to overcome the barriers that normally prevent reactions from taking place.

The probability of successful encounters increases.

Biology appears to speed up.

When energy decreases, the opposite happens.

Molecules continue to move, but more slowly.

Encounters become less frequent.

Fewer collisions have sufficient energy to produce change.

The biological clocks introduced earlier in this book begin to slow.

Nothing has been switched off.

The opportunities for biology to continue have simply become fewer.

This principle reaches far beyond enzymes.

Respiration depends upon countless successful encounters.

Growth depends upon them.

Repair depends upon them.

Microbial multiplication depends upon them.

Ripening depends upon them.

Even deterioration depends upon them.

Life is not driven by isolated reactions.

It emerges from an unimaginable number of successful molecular encounters occurring every second.

Temperature influences life because it changes the likelihood of those encounters.

# Part IV — Temperature Changes Probability

Imagine watching people leave a football stadium.

When everyone walks quickly through wide open spaces, meetings happen constantly.

Friends recognise one another.

Families regroup.

Conversations begin.

Now imagine the same crowd moving slowly through heavy snow.

The people are still present.

They can still meet.

But encounters become less frequent.

Everything simply takes longer.

Something remarkably similar happens inside living organisms.

Molecules never stop moving completely.

Even at low temperatures they continue to vibrate, rotate and drift through their surroundings.

What changes is not whether they move.

It is how likely they are to meet in ways that allow biology to continue.

As temperature increases, molecules possess more kinetic energy.

They move more rapidly.

They collide more frequently.

A greater proportion of these collisions contain enough energy for chemical reactions to proceed.

The probability of successful biological encounters increases.

As temperature decreases, molecular motion slows.

Collisions become less frequent.

Fewer collisions possess enough energy to overcome the barriers required for biological reactions.

The probability of successful encounters decreases.

This explains why temperature influences almost every aspect of biology.

Warm conditions encourage rapid growth.

Fruit ripens more quickly.

Microorganisms multiply faster.

Enzymes catalyse reactions more readily.

Cold conditions produce the opposite effect.

Growth slows.

Ripening is delayed.

Microbial activity decreases.

Many biological clocks begin to tick more slowly.

Importantly, refrigeration does not stop biology.

It changes the probability that biology can continue.

Life has not disappeared.

The opportunities for biological change have simply become less frequent.

This distinction explains why refrigerated food eventually continues to age.

Given enough time, the necessary molecular encounters still occur.

They simply occur more slowly.

Preservation therefore does not begin by stopping biology.

It begins by influencing probability.

# Part V — Cooling Biology

For thousands of years, people have recognised that food remains fresh for longer in cold conditions.

Long before thermometers existed, snow, caves and cool cellars were used to extend the useful life of harvested crops.

Today, refrigerators and freezers perform the same task with far greater precision.

The underlying principle, however, has not changed.

Cooling does not preserve food because cold possesses special protective properties.

It preserves food because reducing temperature reduces molecular energy.

As molecular energy decreases, successful biological encounters become less frequent.

Respiration slows.

Enzymatic activity decreases.

Microorganisms multiply more slowly.

Many of the biological clocks introduced earlier in this book begin to tick at a reduced pace.

The organisation of the biological system remains largely intact.

The processes responsible for change simply proceed more slowly.

Refrigeration illustrates this principle clearly.

Temperatures above freezing reduce biological activity while allowing most of the water within cells to remain liquid.

The result is not the suspension of life, but a significant reduction in its pace.

This makes refrigeration ideal for products that must remain fresh while continuing to function biologically, such as fruits, vegetables and many living plant tissues.

Freezing takes the same principle further.

As water changes from liquid to ice, molecular movement becomes far more restricted.

Available liquid water decreases dramatically.

Many biological processes can no longer continue in their normal way.

The biological clocks slow even further.

Yet freezing also introduces new challenges.

Ice crystals may damage delicate cellular structures.

Water expands as it freezes.

Cell membranes may rupture.

Upon thawing, the biological organisation described in Chapter 1 may no longer be fully preserved.

Successful preservation therefore depends not only on slowing biology but also on protecting biological structure.

Some preservation techniques extend this principle even further.

Cryopreservation uses extremely low temperatures to reduce molecular movement to an extraordinary degree.

Under carefully controlled conditions, biological systems can remain stable for years.

Yet even here, success depends upon respecting biology.

Cooling too quickly or too slowly may damage cells.

Ice formation must be carefully managed.

Protective compounds are often required to preserve cellular organisation.

Across every cooling technology, the objective remains remarkably consistent.

Not to defeat biology.

Not to stop time.

But to reduce the opportunities for biological change while preserving as much biological organisation as possible.

# Part VI — Engineering Energy

Every preservation system is, in essence, an exercise in energy management.

Whether we cool, freeze, dry or freeze-dry a product, the objective remains remarkably similar.

We are not attempting to stop biology.

We are influencing the amount of energy available for biological change.

Understanding this principle changes how preservation systems are designed.

Rather than asking,

*"Which technology should we use?"*

the better question becomes,

*"What does this biological system require?"*

Different products respond differently to changes in energy.

A leafy vegetable requires a different preservation strategy from a cereal grain.

Fresh berries behave differently from nuts.

Living seeds require conditions that would rapidly damage harvested fruit.

The technology must therefore adapt to the biology, not the other way around.

Good engineering begins by understanding the biological system.

How quickly do the biological clocks progress?

How sensitive are the tissues to freezing?

How much water remains available?

Which quality attributes must be preserved?

Only after these questions have been answered can appropriate technologies be selected.

Sometimes refrigeration is sufficient.

Sometimes freezing is necessary.

Sometimes removing water provides the greatest stability.

In other cases, combining multiple preservation methods produces the best result.

No technology is universally superior.

Each offers advantages, limitations and compromises.

Engineering is therefore not the search for a perfect preservation method.

It is the search for the most appropriate solution for a particular biological system.

The objective is not to make every biological system fit the same technology.

The objective is to understand the biology well enough to select or design the technology that fits that system.

As our understanding of biology improves, so too can the technologies we use to preserve it.

More accurate measurements.

Better process control.

Lower energy consumption.

Reduced food waste.

Higher nutritional quality.

Every improvement begins with a deeper understanding of the biology being preserved.

Technology does not replace biological understanding.

It applies biological understanding.

When engineering begins with biology, preservation becomes more efficient, more sustainable and more respectful of the living systems from which our food originates.

## Closing Reflection

Life depends upon organisation.

Life changes through countless biological clocks.

Water provides the medium through which those clocks operate.

Energy determines how quickly they can progress.

Together, these principles explain why preservation works.

Yet even when water and energy are carefully controlled, biological systems continue to exchange something equally important with their surroundings.

Every living cell breathes.

Every harvested crop continues to breathe.

To understand preservation completely, we must next understand respiration.
