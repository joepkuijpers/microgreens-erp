# Chapter 20 — Why Does Drying Preserve Food?

> *"Long before refrigeration or electricity, humanity discovered that removing water could preserve life. Preservation begins by understanding why this simple observation changed civilisation."*

## Introduction

For thousands of years, people preserved food without understanding microorganisms.

Without thermodynamics.

Without chemistry.

Without molecular biology.

Yet they observed something remarkable.

Food left in the sun often lasted longer.

Grains could be stored for months.

Dried herbs retained their usefulness.

Fish survived long journeys.

Fruit became lighter, sweeter and easier to transport.

The explanation remained unknown.

Today we understand that drying does not preserve food simply because water disappears.

It preserves food because the biological environment changes.

Water moves.

Water activity decreases.

Microorganisms lose opportunity.

Enzymes become less active.

Many chemical reactions proceed more slowly.

The same biological laws explored throughout this book continue to operate.

Only their pace has changed.

Drying therefore represents one of humanity's earliest examples of biological engineering.

Without changing biology itself, people learned to change the environment in which biology continued.

The discovery was simple.

Its consequences transformed civilisation.

## Part I — Life Depends on Available Water

Water makes life possible.

It allows molecules to move.

Enzymes to function.

Cells to exchange materials.

Microorganisms to grow.

Without water, none of these processes could occur.

Yet water is not the problem.

Life itself depends upon it.

The challenge begins after harvest.

The same water that once supported healthy growth continues to support biological activity.

Respiration continues.

Enzymes continue working.

Microorganisms continue multiplying.

Drying changes this relationship.

Rather than removing biology, it gradually limits its ability to function.

As available water decreases, biological activity becomes increasingly difficult.

The organism has not changed.

The environment has.

This distinction is fundamental.

Drying succeeds not because water is harmful.

It succeeds because life depends upon water remaining available.

Preservation works by changing that availability.

Water can be removed in many different ways.

Some methods rely upon sunlight.

Others use moving air.

Modern systems may use heat, vacuum or freezing.

Although these techniques appear very different, they all pursue the same objective.

To guide the movement of water from the food into its surroundings.

## Part II — The Journey of Water

Water does not disappear from food all at once.

Its journey begins deep within the biological structure.

Inside every cell, water surrounds proteins, carbohydrates, minerals and countless other molecules.

Some water moves freely.

Some is held more tightly.

Before water can leave the food, it must first travel to the surface.

This movement is neither instant nor uniform.

Water follows microscopic pathways through cells, tissues and tiny spaces between them.

Some tissues offer little resistance.

Others slow the journey considerably.

The structure of the organism therefore influences how easily water can escape.

A thin leaf dries differently from a thick carrot.

A mushroom behaves differently from a grain of wheat.

The destination, however, is always the same.

Eventually, water reaches the surface.

There it encounters the surrounding air.

If the air can accept additional water, molecules leave the surface and enter the atmosphere.

As surface water disappears, more water moves from within the food to replace it.

This process continues until a new balance is reached or the available water becomes increasingly difficult to remove.

Drying is therefore not a single event.

It is a continuous journey from the centre of the organism to the surrounding environment.

The speed of this journey depends upon both the food and its surroundings.

Successful drying respects both.

Water does not always leave food at the same speed.

Sometimes drying begins rapidly before gradually slowing.

Understanding why reveals another important principle of preservation.

Not all water is equally easy to remove.

## Part III — Not All Water Is Equally Available

At the beginning of drying, water often leaves food relatively quickly.

The surface is moist.

Water moves easily.

The surrounding air readily accepts it.

As drying continues, however, something begins to change.

Removing additional water becomes increasingly difficult.

The reason is simple.

Not every water molecule exists under the same conditions.

Some water occupies open spaces within cells and tissues.

It moves relatively freely and can reach the surface with little resistance.

Other water interacts closely with proteins, carbohydrates and minerals.

These molecules attract water through countless microscopic interactions.

The water remains present.

Yet it becomes increasingly difficult to remove.

As the more accessible water disappears, the remaining water is held more tightly within the biological structure.

The journey to the surface becomes longer.

The forces keeping water within the food become stronger.

Drying naturally slows.

This explains why removing the first half of the water may require only a fraction of the time needed to remove the final portion.

The process has not become less effective.

The remaining water has become less available.

This principle extends far beyond drying.

The availability of water determines whether enzymes continue to function.

Whether microorganisms continue to grow.

Whether chemical reactions proceed efficiently.

Preservation therefore depends not only upon how much water remains, but upon how much water remains available to participate in biology.

The quantity of water matters.

Its availability matters even more.

Reducing water activity does not sterilise food.

Some microorganisms and spores may survive without growing and can become active again if moisture returns.

Drying must therefore begin with safe raw materials and remain connected to hygienic handling and suitable storage.

Water leaves food because the surrounding environment can accept it.

The speed of this exchange therefore depends not only upon the food itself, but also upon the air surrounding it.

Temperature.

Humidity.

Air movement.

Together they determine how quickly water can continue its journey.

## Part IV — The Environment Determines the Pace

Water never leaves food on its own.

Its movement is always influenced by the environment surrounding it.

Three environmental factors are particularly important.

Temperature.

Humidity.

Air movement.

Each contributes in a different way.

Temperature provides energy.

As temperature increases, water molecules move more vigorously, making it easier for them to escape from the surface.

Humidity determines how readily the surrounding air can accept additional water.

Dry air encourages continued evaporation.

Moist air reduces the difference between the food and its surroundings, slowing the movement of water.

Air movement continually replaces the thin layer of humid air that forms around the surface of the food.

Without this exchange, evaporation gradually slows as the surrounding air becomes increasingly saturated.

Together, these factors create the conditions under which drying proceeds.

None acts independently.

Warm air with little movement may dry more slowly than cooler air with strong ventilation.

Very dry air may remove water rapidly at first, yet cause the surface to dry so quickly that moisture becomes trapped within the food.

Successful drying therefore requires balance rather than extremes.

The objective is not simply to remove water as quickly as possible.

It is to guide the movement of water in a manner that preserves biological organisation.

The food, the surrounding air and the flow of energy together determine the pace of drying.

Preservation engineering begins by understanding this relationship.

Drying can preserve food remarkably well.

Yet drying is never without consequence.

As water leaves, the biological structure continues to respond.

Some products remain light and crisp.

Others shrink, harden or crack.

To understand why, we must now examine how drying influences the structure of food itself.

## Part V — Drying Changes More Than Water

Drying removes water.

It cannot remove only water.

As water leaves, the biological structure responds.

Cells lose internal pressure.

Tissues contract.

Spaces between cells become smaller.

The organisation that once supported a fresh, living structure gradually changes.

Some foods tolerate this remarkably well.

Grains and seeds naturally contain relatively little water and are designed to withstand periods of dryness.

Others are far more sensitive.

Leafy vegetables may become fragile.

Fruit may shrink.

Mushrooms often become lighter and more porous.

The extent of these changes depends upon both the food and the drying conditions.

When water leaves gradually, the structure often has more opportunity to adapt.

When water is removed too rapidly, the surface may dry and stiffen before moisture from the interior can reach it.

The outer layer then becomes a barrier, slowing further drying while moisture remains trapped within.

This phenomenon is known as case hardening.

Rather than preserving quality, excessively rapid drying can reduce it.

Successful drying therefore requires balance.

The objective is not simply to remove water.

It is to remove water while preserving as much of the food's biological organisation as possible.

Texture.

Appearance.

Flavour.

Nutritional value.

All depend upon how the drying process is guided.

Every drying method represents a compromise between efficiency and quality.

Understanding that compromise allows preservation to become an informed engineering decision rather than a purely technical process.

Drying has preserved food for thousands of years.

Yet it is not suitable for every product or every purpose.

Modern preservation has therefore developed additional methods that protect food by changing the environment in different ways.

The next chapter explores perhaps the most familiar example.

Cooling.

## Part VI — Every Preservation Method Is a Compromise

Drying is one of humanity's oldest and most successful preservation technologies.

It extends shelf life.

Reduces weight.

Simplifies storage.

Allows transportation over long distances.

Requires relatively little energy when natural sunlight or ambient air can be used.

These advantages explain why dried foods remain important throughout the world.

Yet every preservation method involves compromise.

Water supports more than microbial growth.

It contributes to texture.

Carries aroma compounds.

Influences flavour perception.

Supports enzyme activity.

Maintains the structure of living tissues.

As water is removed, some of these characteristics inevitably change.

Fresh herbs become brittle.

Fruit becomes firmer and sweeter.

Mushrooms lose much of their original texture.

Leafy vegetables no longer resemble the living plants from which they originated.

These changes are not failures.

They are natural consequences of altering the biological environment.

The objective of preservation is therefore not to eliminate change.

It is to guide change towards a desirable outcome.

The most appropriate preservation method depends upon the intended purpose of the food.

A dried herb performs its role exceptionally well.

A fresh salad leaf does not benefit from complete dehydration.

Neither product is better.

Each has been preserved according to a different objective.

Successful preservation begins by defining that objective before selecting the technology.

Engineering follows purpose.

Not the other way around.

## Closing Reflection

Drying teaches one of the most important lessons in preservation.

Food is never separated from its environment.

Every molecule of water that leaves alters the biological balance within the organism.

The engineer cannot prevent every change.

The engineer can influence its direction and rate.

Drying succeeds because it works with biology rather than against it.

It changes the environment.

Biology responds.

The quality of the preserved food depends upon how well those environmental changes respect the organisation of life.

Technology alone does not preserve food.

Understanding determines how effectively it is used.

The oldest preservation methods removed water.

Another approach emerged much later.

Instead of reducing the availability of water, it reduced the energy available to every biological and chemical process.

The result transformed food preservation.

Cooling.
