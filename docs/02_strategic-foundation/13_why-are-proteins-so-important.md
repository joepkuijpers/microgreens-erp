# Chapter 13 — Why Are Proteins So Important?

> *"Every living structure is built from molecules. Proteins give many of those molecules purpose. Preservation begins by understanding both."*

## Introduction

Every living organism depends upon countless chemical reactions.

Cells grow.

They repair themselves.

They communicate.

They transport nutrients.

They respond to their environment.

Within living systems, these processes depend upon carefully organised chemical reactions.

They require molecules capable of performing highly specialised tasks.

These molecules are proteins.

Some proteins provide strength.

Others transport oxygen, nutrients or minerals.

Many act as enzymes, accelerating the chemical reactions that sustain life.

Others detect changes in the environment or help defend the organism against disease.

## Part I — Structure Creates Function

Despite their remarkable diversity, all proteins share a common characteristic.

They function because of their structure.

Unlike simple molecules, proteins are folded into complex three-dimensional shapes.

This shape determines what they can recognise, what they can bind to and what work they can perform.

A protein that loses its shape often loses its function.

For living organisms, maintaining protein structure is therefore essential.

After harvest, however, the conditions protecting these structures gradually change.

Temperature.

Water.

Acidity.

Salt.

Mechanical stress.

Each can influence how proteins behave.

Understanding proteins is therefore not simply about chemistry.

It is about understanding how biological function emerges from molecular structure.

## Part II — When Shape Is Lost

Proteins are not rigid structures.

Although they often appear stable, they are held together by many relatively weak interactions.

Hydrogen bonds.

Electrical attractions.

Hydrophobic interactions.

Together, these forces maintain the precise three-dimensional shape required for biological function.

As long as these interactions remain intact, the protein can perform its role.

When they are disturbed, the structure begins to change.

The protein unfolds.

Some regions become exposed.

Others collapse.

New interactions may form while existing ones disappear.

The original organisation is gradually lost.

This process is known as denaturation.

Denaturation does not usually destroy the protein itself.

The chain of amino acids often remains intact.

Instead, the protein loses the organisation that allowed it to function.

The consequences can be profound.

An enzyme may no longer catalyse a reaction.

A structural protein may lose its strength.

Transport proteins may no longer bind the molecules they once carried.

The molecule remains present.

Its function does not.

This distinction is essential.

Preservation is often concerned less with preventing the loss of molecules than with preserving the organisation that gives those molecules their biological purpose.

Protein structure can be disrupted in many different ways.

Heat is one of the most familiar.

Yet temperature is only one of several environmental factors capable of reshaping these remarkable molecules.

Water.

Acidity.

Salt.

Pressure.

Mechanical stress.

Each influences protein stability through different mechanisms.

Understanding these factors reveals why preservation methods produce such different results.

## Part III — Energy Reshapes Proteins

Proteins are often described as stable molecules.

In reality, they are constantly moving.

Every atom within a protein vibrates.

Small sections flex.

Entire regions shift ever so slightly.

These movements are a natural consequence of thermal energy.

At lower temperatures, the movements remain relatively small.

The interactions holding the protein together are able to maintain its overall organisation.

As temperature increases, molecular motion becomes more energetic.

Atoms move further.

Flexible regions move more frequently.

The weak interactions stabilising the folded structure are disturbed more often.

Eventually, enough of these interactions are broken that the protein can no longer maintain its original shape.

The structure begins to unfold.

Importantly, heat does not instruct a protein to unfold.

It simply increases molecular motion until the organised structure becomes increasingly difficult to maintain.

This distinction reveals an important principle.

Heat does not directly destroy biological organisation.

It increases the probability that organisation will gradually be lost.

The exact temperature at which this occurs differs between proteins.

Some remain stable under conditions that rapidly denature others.

This diversity reflects the remarkable adaptations that have evolved throughout biology.

Proteins found in Arctic fish differ from those of desert plants.

Thermophilic bacteria possess proteins capable of functioning near temperatures that would rapidly denature proteins from most other organisms.

Protein stability is therefore not fixed.

It is an evolutionary adaptation to the environment in which an organism lives.

Preservation engineering makes use of this principle.

By lowering temperature, molecular motion is reduced.

The organised structure of proteins is preserved for longer.

The biological processes depending upon those proteins also slow.

The molecules remain the same.

Only their environment has changed.

Temperature is only one influence.

Proteins also respond to changes in acidity, water availability and the chemical environment surrounding them.

To understand why the same protein behaves differently in different foods, we must next examine the environment in which proteins exist.

## Part IV — The Environment Shapes Every Protein

A protein never exists alone.

It is surrounded by water.

Dissolved minerals.

Organic acids.

Sugars.

Other proteins.

Countless molecules continuously interact with its surface.

These interactions help stabilise the folded structure.

Change the environment, and the protein responds.

Acidity alters the electrical charges carried by many amino acids.

Water influences how proteins fold and how freely they move.

Salt changes the attraction and repulsion between charged regions.

Sugars may stabilise certain structures by interacting with surrounding water.

Even pressure can alter the balance of forces maintaining the protein's shape.

None of these factors acts independently.

Together they create the environment in which proteins exist.

A protein that remains stable in one environment may unfold rapidly in another.

The molecule itself has not changed.

The conditions surrounding it have.

This principle extends far beyond proteins.

Every biological molecule responds to its environment.

Preservation therefore begins not by changing biology, but by carefully engineering the environment in which biology continues to exist.

Understanding this relationship explains why the same preservation method may produce different results in different foods.

The environment determines how biology responds.

Proteins can lose their structure.

Sometimes this loss is undesirable.

Milk proteins coagulate under particular acidic, enzymatic or thermal conditions.

Milk curdles.

Yet in many cases, controlled protein denaturation creates entirely new foods.

Bread.

Cheese.

Yoghurt.

Tofu.

Cooking itself.

The same biological process can therefore reduce quality—or create it.

Understanding this distinction reveals one of the most important ideas in food science.

## Part V — Not Every Change Is Undesirable

When discussing preservation, it is easy to assume that every biological change should be prevented.

Nature tells a different story.

Many of the foods we value exist because proteins were allowed to change.

Milk becomes yoghurt.

Curds become cheese.

Liquid egg becomes firm when heated.

Bread develops its structure during baking.

Tofu forms as soy proteins reorganise.

In each case, proteins lose one organisation while creating another.

The original structure disappears.

A new structure emerges.

Neither structure is inherently better.

Each is suited to a different purpose.

This distinction is fundamental.

Preservation is not always the prevention of change.

Sometimes it is the careful guidance of change towards a desired outcome.

The objective determines the strategy.

Fresh lettuce should remain crisp.

Cheese should mature.

Bread should develop structure.

Yoghurt should ferment.

Each product requires a different balance between stability and transformation.

Successful preservation therefore begins with a simple question.

What should remain unchanged?

What should be allowed to change?

Only after answering these questions can an appropriate preservation strategy be designed.

The intended food defines the objective.

Biology defines what is possible.

Engineering creates the conditions that support the desired outcome.

Engineering simply provides the conditions that allow the desired biological outcome to occur.

## Closing Reflection

Proteins demonstrate one of biology's most important principles.

Function emerges from organisation.

Change the organisation, and function changes with it.

Sometimes this represents deterioration.

Sometimes it represents transformation.

The difference depends not upon the biology itself, but upon the purpose we seek to achieve.

Preservation is therefore not only the preservation of individual molecules.

It is the preservation—or deliberate transformation—of biological organisation.

It is the preservation—or deliberate transformation—of biological organisation.

Proteins perform countless biological tasks.

Yet they are only one part of every living organism.

Another remarkable group of molecules provides both structure and energy.

They build cell walls.

Store biological fuel.

Support growth.

And determine many of the textures we experience every day.

To understand these remarkable molecules, we must now turn to carbohydrates.
