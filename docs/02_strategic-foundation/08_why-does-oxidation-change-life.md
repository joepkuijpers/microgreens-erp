# Chapter 8 — Why Does Oxidation Change Life?

> *Life builds order. Chemistry never stops changing it. Preservation begins by understanding both.*

## Introduction

Slice an apple and leave it on the kitchen table.

Within minutes, its pale surface begins to turn brown.

Cut an avocado and the same thing happens.

Fresh herbs slowly lose their vibrant green colour.

Cooking oils gradually develop unpleasant flavours.

Nuts become rancid.

Vitamins slowly disappear during storage.

At first glance, these changes seem unrelated.

Some affect colour.

Others alter flavour.

Some reduce nutritional value.

Others shorten shelf life.

Yet they all share a common origin.

Chemistry never stops.

Unlike respiration, chemistry does not depend upon the organism trying to remain alive.

Unlike growth, it does not require new tissues to be formed.

Unlike repair, it does not seek to preserve biological organisation.

Chemical reactions simply follow the laws of nature.

One of the most important of these processes is oxidation.

Oxidation is often described as the reaction between molecules and oxygen.

While this is frequently true, oxidation is better understood as the transfer of electrons from one molecule to another.

This seemingly simple process quietly reshapes biological systems every moment.

Some oxidation is essential for life.

Cells use carefully controlled oxidation during respiration to release energy from sugars.

Without it, life could not exist.

Outside this carefully controlled environment, however, oxidation follows no biological purpose.

Pigments change.

Lipids degrade.

Vitamins are lost.

Flavours evolve.

Biological organisation slowly becomes more difficult to maintain.

This reveals an important distinction.

Biology continuously invests in maintaining order.

Chemistry does not.

Chemistry simply follows the opportunities available to it.

To understand preservation, we must therefore understand not only the biology that builds organisation, but also the chemistry that continually reshapes it.

# Part I — Nothing Remains Unchanged

Imagine placing a freshly polished piece of iron outdoors.

At first, its surface appears bright and smooth.

Weeks later, small orange patches begin to appear.

Months pass.

The metal slowly becomes covered with rust.

No one is actively changing it.

No living organism is directing the process.

The material simply continues to interact with its surroundings.

Now consider a freshly cut apple.

Its pale flesh gradually turns brown.

The fruit has not chosen to change.

The cells are not attempting to produce a darker colour.

The changes occur because the chemistry within the tissue continues.

The same principle can be observed throughout nature.

Copper slowly develops a green surface.

Wood darkens with age.

Cooking oils become rancid.

Fresh herbs lose their bright green colour.

Vitamins gradually break down during storage.

Although these materials differ enormously, they share an important characteristic.

None remain exactly as they were.

Matter continually interacts with the world around it.

Sometimes these changes occur rapidly.

Sometimes they take years.

Some are beneficial.

Others reduce quality.

Yet all follow the same fundamental principle.

Chemistry never stands still.

Living organisms work continuously to maintain biological organisation.

Chemistry has no such objective.

It simply follows the opportunities available to it.

This is one of the great differences between biology and chemistry.

Biology maintains.

Chemistry transforms.

Oxidation is one of the most important examples of this continual transformation.

# Part II — Every Reaction Is an Exchange

Chemistry is often introduced as a collection of reactions.

Thousands of equations.

Hundreds of molecules.

Endless names and definitions.

Yet beneath this apparent complexity lies a remarkably simple idea.

Whenever molecules react, something changes.

Atoms may be rearranged.

Chemical bonds may be broken.

New bonds may be formed.

Energy may be released or absorbed.

But no reaction occurs without an exchange.

Sometimes atoms are exchanged.

Sometimes energy is exchanged.

Sometimes electrical charge is exchanged.

One of the most important exchanges in biology involves something far smaller than an atom.

Electrons.

Electrons occupy the outer regions of atoms and participate in almost every chemical bond.

Although they are extraordinarily small, their movement determines how molecules behave.

When one molecule loses electrons while another gains them, both molecules change.

Their chemical properties become different.

Their stability changes.

Their ability to participate in future reactions also changes.

This process is known as oxidation and reduction.

These two processes can never be separated.

Whenever one molecule loses electrons, another must receive them.

Every oxidation is therefore accompanied by a reduction.

Together they form what chemists call a redox reaction.

Although the terminology may appear unfamiliar, the principle is surprisingly simple.

Nothing is lost.

Electrons simply move from one molecule to another.

Life depends upon these exchanges.

Respiration releases energy through carefully controlled electron transfer.

Photosynthesis stores energy by moving electrons in the opposite direction.

Even many of the chemical changes responsible for food deterioration begin with the same fundamental process.

To understand oxidation, we must therefore follow the movement of electrons.

# Part III — Why Electrons Matter

At first sight, electrons seem insignificant.

They are unimaginably small.

Invisible to the naked eye.

Far smaller than the cells, tissues and organisms we observe every day.

Yet these tiny particles determine how matter is organised.

Every molecule exists because atoms share or transfer electrons.

The arrangement of electrons determines which atoms attract one another, which repel one another and which remain stable together.

A single change in electron distribution can alter the behaviour of an entire molecule.

Sometimes the effect is barely noticeable.

Sometimes it completely changes the properties of a biological system.

This is why oxidation matters.

When electrons move from one molecule to another, the molecules themselves change.

Some become more stable.

Others become more reactive.

Some lose their original function altogether.

Imagine removing a single brick from a carefully built arch.

The arch may continue standing.

Remove another.

Then another.

Eventually the entire structure begins to weaken.

Each individual change appears small.

Together they gradually alter the stability of the whole.

Biological organisation behaves in a similar way.

Individual oxidation reactions rarely cause immediate deterioration.

Instead, they slowly alter thousands of molecules throughout the tissue.

Pigments lose their colour.

Lipids become unstable.

Proteins change shape.

Vitamins gradually disappear.

Each reaction is small.

Together they reshape the biological organisation that life worked so carefully to create.

This explains why deterioration is often gradual rather than sudden.

Quality is not usually lost in a single event.

It is the accumulation of countless molecular changes over time.

Preservation therefore does not attempt to stop every oxidation reaction.

That would be impossible.

Instead, preservation seeks to reduce the opportunities for these reactions to occur.

By slowing chemical change, biological organisation can remain intact for longer.

# Part IV — Why Oxygen Is So Reactive

Walk through a forest after a storm.

A fallen tree slowly decays.

Leaves lying on the ground gradually disappear.

A forgotten piece of iron develops rust.

An apple left on the kitchen counter turns brown.

Although these processes appear very different, many share one common participant.

Oxygen.

At first this seems strange.

Oxygen is essential for life.

Without it, humans and many other organisms could not survive.

Plants produce it.

Animals depend upon it.

Yet the same oxygen that supports life also contributes to many of the chemical changes that reduce food quality.

This is not a contradiction.

It is a consequence of oxygen's chemical behaviour.

Oxygen has a strong tendency to accept electrons.

Whenever an opportunity arises, oxygen readily participates in chemical reactions that allow it to gain them.

This makes oxygen an exceptionally effective reaction partner.

Inside living cells, this property is carefully controlled.

During respiration, electrons are transferred through a highly organised series of reactions before finally reaching oxygen.

The energy released along the way is captured and used to support life.

Outside these carefully regulated pathways, oxygen follows no biological purpose.

It simply reacts whenever conditions allow.

Pigments become oxidised.

Lipids begin to deteriorate.

Vitamins gradually lose their activity.

Flavour compounds change.

Over time, biological organisation slowly becomes more difficult to maintain.

Oxygen is therefore neither beneficial nor harmful by itself.

Its effects depend entirely upon the context in which it reacts.

Life has evolved remarkable systems to harness oxygen safely.

Preservation seeks to limit the opportunities for oxygen to participate in reactions that reduce quality.

Engineering therefore seeks not to eliminate oxygen, but to manage the conditions under which oxygen reacts.

Understanding this distinction is fundamental.

The challenge is not oxygen itself.

The challenge is controlling when, where and how oxygen is allowed to react.

# Part V — Every Reaction Must Overcome a Barrier

If oxygen is constantly surrounding us, an obvious question remains.

Why does a freshly harvested apple not become brown the instant it is picked?

Why does cooking oil remain usable for months?

Why does a metal tool survive years before rust becomes visible?

If oxidation is always possible, why is it often so slow?

The answer lies in one of the fundamental principles of chemistry.

Not every possible reaction happens immediately.

Before molecules can react, they must first overcome an energy barrier.

Imagine pushing a heavy cart over a hill.

As long as the cart remains at the bottom, nothing changes.

Energy must first be supplied to push it uphill.

Only after reaching the top does the cart roll down the other side.

Many chemical reactions behave in a similar way.

Reacting molecules must first reach a state in which existing chemical bonds can be weakened and new bonds can begin to form.

Only then can the reaction continue.

This initial energy requirement is known as the activation energy.

The height of this barrier determines how easily a reaction occurs.

Some reactions have only a small barrier.

They proceed rapidly under ordinary conditions.

Others face a much higher barrier.

Although they are chemically possible, they may occur so slowly that little change is observed over days, months or even years.

Oxidation follows the same principle.

Oxygen may be present.

Electrons may be available.

The reaction may be possible.

Yet if the activation barrier is not overcome, very little happens.

This explains why food can remain fresh for a period after harvest.

The chemical changes responsible for deterioration do not begin all at once.

They occur gradually as countless individual molecules overcome their own energy barriers.

Preservation does not change the laws of chemistry.

Instead, it reduces the likelihood that molecules possess enough energy to cross these barriers.

In doing so, it slows the countless reactions that gradually reshape biological organisation.

# Part VI — Temperature Creates Opportunities

Every molecule is in constant motion.

Some move slowly.

Others move more rapidly.

Even within the same piece of fruit, not every molecule possesses the same amount of energy.

Some have too little energy to react.

Others have just enough to overcome the activation barrier.

Most preservation methods do not change the molecules themselves.

Instead, they change the number of molecules capable of reacting.

Temperature plays a central role in this process.

As temperature increases, molecules move more vigorously.

Collisions become more frequent.

More molecules possess sufficient energy to overcome the activation barriers that separate reactants from products.

As a result, chemical reactions occur more often.

Oxidation accelerates.

Pigments change more rapidly.

Lipids deteriorate more quickly.

Vitamins disappear sooner.

When temperature decreases, the opposite occurs.

Molecular movement slows.

Fewer molecules possess enough energy to overcome their activation barriers.

The reactions themselves remain possible.

They simply occur less frequently.

This distinction is essential.

Cooling does not prevent oxidation.

Freezing does not eliminate chemistry.

Neither changes the fundamental laws governing molecules.

Instead, they reduce the opportunities for chemical reactions to occur.

This idea should now sound familiar.

Earlier in this book, temperature was introduced as a factor that changes the pace of biology.

We can now see that the same principle applies to chemistry.

Temperature does not determine what is possible.

It determines how often possibilities become reality.

This is why controlling temperature remains one of the most powerful tools in food preservation.

It slows not only biological activity, but also the countless chemical reactions that continually reshape biological organisation.

# Closing Reflection

Life continually builds biological organisation.

Cells grow.

Proteins are assembled.

Membranes are maintained.

Pigments give colour.

Vitamins support countless biological processes.

Every part of a living organism represents an extraordinary level of organisation.

Chemistry, however, has no awareness of this organisation.

It does not distinguish between a healthy leaf and a fallen one.

Between a fresh apple and one stored for months.

Between living tissue and harvested food.

Chemical reactions simply follow the opportunities available to them.

Oxidation is one expression of this principle.

Each individual reaction is small.

Most pass unnoticed.

Yet together they gradually reshape the molecules upon which biological organisation depends.

Over time, colours fade.

Flavours change.

Textures soften.

Nutrients decline.

The remarkable organisation created by life slowly becomes more difficult to maintain.

Preservation cannot prevent chemistry.

Nor should it.

Chemical reactions are part of the same natural world that makes life possible.

Instead, preservation works by guiding the conditions under which chemistry occurs.

Temperature is lowered.

Oxygen is controlled.

Light is reduced.

Water availability is managed.

Each intervention reduces the opportunities for undesirable reactions while allowing biological organisation to endure for longer.

## Closing Reflection

Oxidation therefore teaches an important lesson.

The greatest threats to quality are rarely dramatic.

They are the result of countless small changes that accumulate over time.

Understanding these changes allows us not to fight nature, but to work with it.

That is the foundation of preservation.
