# Chapter 12 — Why Do Molecules Change?

> *"Every living structure is built from molecules. When conditions change, so do they. Preservation begins by understanding both."*

## Introduction

A leaf may remain green while its flavour changes.

Fruit may keep its shape while its aroma slowly disappears.

Seeds can remain intact for years, yet gradually lose their ability to germinate.

Sometimes change is visible.

Often it is not.

Beneath every biological structure lies another level of organisation.

Proteins.

Carbohydrates.

Lipids.

Pigments.

Vitamins.

Organic acids.

Thousands of different molecules work together to create the properties we recognise as freshness, flavour, texture and nutritional value.

These molecules are never completely still.

They continuously interact.

They exchange energy.

They form new bonds.

Old bonds break.

Some reactions are essential for life.

Others continue long after harvest.

Preservation therefore extends beyond protecting cells and tissues.

It also seeks to preserve the molecular organisation upon which biological quality depends.

To understand why preserved foods change over time, we must first understand how biological molecules respond to their environment.

## Part I — Molecules Are Never Still

Matter appears solid.

At the molecular level, it is anything but.

Every molecule is constantly moving.

They vibrate.

They rotate.

They collide with neighbouring molecules.

These movements are driven by thermal energy.

Some collisions have no lasting effect.

Others allow molecules to interact.

New bonds may form.

Existing bonds may break.

The countless interactions occurring every second give rise to the properties we observe as texture, flavour, colour and aroma.

Life depends upon this continuous molecular activity.

After harvest, the activity does not immediately stop.

The organisation simply begins to change.

Understanding preservation therefore requires understanding that quality exists not only at the level of tissues, but also at the level of molecules.

Not every molecule responds in the same way.

Some remain remarkably stable.

Others change within minutes.

To understand preservation, we must first discover why different biological molecules behave so differently.

## Part II — Stability Is Never Absolute

Not every biological molecule behaves in the same way.

Some remain remarkably stable for years.

Others begin changing almost immediately after harvest.

This difference is not accidental.

It is determined by molecular structure.

Every molecule contains atoms connected by chemical bonds.

Some of these bonds are exceptionally strong.

Others require only a small amount of energy to change.

The arrangement of atoms determines how readily a molecule reacts with its surroundings.

A stable molecule resists change.

A less stable molecule responds more readily to heat, oxygen, water, light or neighbouring molecules.

Stability, however, should not be mistaken for permanence.

Given sufficient time or changing conditions, every biological molecule can be altered.

Preservation therefore does not seek to create molecules that never change.

Instead, it seeks to create conditions in which change occurs more slowly.

This distinction is fundamental.

Quality is not preserved because chemistry stops.

Quality is preserved because chemistry proceeds at a slower pace.

Every preservation method ultimately relies on this same principle.

It changes the environment surrounding biological molecules.

The molecules then respond according to their own natural properties.

Technology cannot command molecules to remain unchanged.

It can only influence the conditions under which they exist.

Some molecules change more readily because they interact with almost everything around them.

Others require very specific conditions before they respond.

Understanding these interactions reveals why the environment is just as important as the molecules themselves.

## Part III — Molecules Respond to Their Environment

No biological molecule exists in isolation.

Every molecule is surrounded by countless others.

Water molecules.

Oxygen molecules.

Sugars.

Proteins.

Lipids.

Minerals.

Each continuously interacts with its surroundings.

Some interactions are brief.

Others result in permanent chemical change.

Whether a reaction occurs depends not only upon the molecule itself, but also upon the environment in which it exists.

Temperature influences molecular movement.

Water determines how easily molecules encounter one another.

Oxygen allows oxidation to occur.

Light provides energy capable of initiating new reactions.

Acidity changes the electrical properties of molecules, altering how they interact.

Together, these environmental conditions shape the behaviour of every biological system.

This explains why identical foods may age differently under different storage conditions.

The molecules have not changed.

The environment has.

The molecules simply respond according to the physical and chemical laws governing their existence.

Preservation therefore works by engineering the molecular environment.

Rather than preventing molecules from behaving naturally, it creates conditions in which their natural behaviour proceeds more slowly.

The food follows the same biological principles.

Only the environment has changed.

The environment influences every molecule.

Yet not every molecule performs the same role.

Some build structure.

Some store energy.

Some carry information.

Some create colour, flavour and aroma.

Understanding preservation therefore requires understanding the major families of biological molecules and the unique contributions each makes to living systems.

## Part IV — Every Molecule Has a Role

Living organisms are not built from identical molecules.

Each type performs a specific function.

Some provide structure.

Others store energy.

Some accelerate chemical reactions.

Others transport information or protect the organism from its environment.

No single molecule can perform every task.

Life depends upon cooperation.

Proteins build and repair.

Carbohydrates provide both structure and energy.

Lipids form protective barriers and store concentrated energy.

Pigments capture or reflect light.

Organic acids influence the chemical environment.

Vitamins enable countless biochemical reactions.

Although these molecules differ greatly, they share one important characteristic.

Each contributes to biological organisation.

When one group begins to change, the effects often extend far beyond the molecule itself.

A protein that unfolds may alter texture.

A pigment that breaks down changes colour.

A lipid that oxidises changes flavour.

A vitamin that degrades reduces nutritional value.

## Closing Reflection

The quality of food therefore emerges from the combined behaviour of many different molecules working together.

Preservation is not concerned with protecting a single molecule.

It seeks to preserve the relationships between them.

Engineering therefore seeks to preserve molecular organisation rather than individual molecular perfection.

Biological organisation exists because every component continues to perform its role.

As these roles are gradually lost, the properties we associate with freshness slowly disappear.

Every biological molecule contributes differently.

Some determine whether food remains firm.

Others create flavour.

Some influence nutrition.

To understand preservation, we must now examine these molecular families individually, beginning with the most versatile molecules found in every living organism.

Proteins.
