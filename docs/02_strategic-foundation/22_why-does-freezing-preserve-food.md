# Chapter 22 — Why Does Freezing Preserve Food?

> *"Cooling slows life. Freezing changes the environment in which life exists."*

## Introduction

Cooling preserves food by slowing biological activity.

Water remains liquid.

Cells continue to function.

Enzymes continue to catalyse reactions.

Microorganisms continue to grow, although much more slowly.

Eventually, however, cooling reaches a practical limit.

As long as liquid water remains available, biological and chemical processes can continue.

Freezing approaches preservation from a different perspective.

Rather than further reducing temperature alone, freezing changes the physical state of water itself.

Liquid water gradually becomes ice.

This transformation has profound consequences.

Water becomes far less available for biological reactions.

The movement of molecules becomes increasingly restricted.

Microorganisms can no longer reproduce under frozen conditions.

Enzyme activity becomes extremely limited.

Respiration effectively ceases in harvested plant tissues.

The food enters a state in which biological change proceeds only very slowly.

Importantly, freezing does not mean that food becomes biologically dead.

Many cells remain alive.

Many enzymes survive freezing.

Many microorganisms also survive.

Their activity has largely been suspended rather than eliminated.

Understanding this distinction explains both the remarkable effectiveness of freezing and the limitations that become apparent after thawing.

## Part I — Water Changes State

Water exists naturally in three physical states.

Solid.

Liquid.

Gas.

Throughout most biological systems, water exists primarily as a liquid.

Liquid water gives cells their shape.

Allows nutrients to move.

Enables enzymes to function.

Supports countless biochemical reactions.

As temperature decreases below the freezing point, liquid water gradually begins to organise into ice crystals.

This process is not merely a reduction in temperature.

It is a fundamental change in molecular organisation.

Instead of moving freely, water molecules arrange themselves into highly ordered crystalline structures.

The mobility that once supported biological activity largely disappears.

Because ice is solid, it cannot participate in biological reactions in the same way as liquid water.

Although some unfrozen water always remains within biological tissues, the amount of available liquid water decreases dramatically.

This reduction in available water explains much of freezing's remarkable preservation power.

Biology depends upon liquid water.

Freezing greatly reduces its availability.Changing water into ice does more than reduce biological activity.

It also changes the physical structure of the food itself.

To understand freezing completely, we must first understand how ice forms inside living tissues.

## Part II — Freezing Is a Gradual Process

Although freezing is often described as a single event, it is better understood as a gradual transition.

Biological tissues do not suddenly become completely frozen when they reach 0°C.

Instead, ice begins to form while a proportion of the water remains liquid.

As the temperature continues to decrease, progressively more water freezes.

The amount of liquid water available to support biological activity gradually becomes smaller.

This process continues well below the initial freezing point.

The reason is that biological tissues are not composed of pure water.

Cells contain sugars.

Minerals.

Proteins.

Organic acids.

Many other dissolved substances.

Together these compounds lower the temperature at which water freezes.

As ice crystals form, these dissolved substances become increasingly concentrated within the remaining liquid water.

The concentration continues to rise as additional water freezes.

Consequently, the remaining liquid becomes progressively more resistant to freezing.

For this reason, some water remains unfrozen even at temperatures far below 0°C.

This small fraction of liquid water remains biologically significant.

It allows certain chemical reactions to continue.

Some enzymes retain limited activity.

A number of microorganisms are capable of surviving frozen storage, even though they cannot actively grow.

Freezing therefore does not create a completely inactive biological system.

Instead, it greatly restricts the amount of liquid water available for biological processes.

The lower the temperature, the less liquid water remains available.

The less liquid water that is available, the more difficult it becomes for biological change to occur.

The preservation achieved by freezing is therefore not the result of temperature alone.

It arises from the gradual reduction in available liquid water.

As water freezes, it does not simply disappear from the tissue.

It reorganises into solid crystals.

The formation of these crystals determines much of the physical quality of frozen food.

To understand why some frozen foods retain their texture while others become soft after thawing, we must first understand how ice crystals develop.

## Part III — Ice Crystals Shape the Structure of Food

As water freezes, it organises into solid crystals.

These crystals are not simply frozen water.

They become part of the physical structure of the food.

Where they form, how quickly they develop and how large they become all influence the quality of the product after thawing.

Ice crystals may form both outside and inside cells.

The first crystals usually develop in the spaces between neighbouring cells.

As liquid water is removed from these spaces, the concentration of dissolved substances outside the cells increases.

Water within the cells naturally moves towards this increasingly concentrated environment.

This movement causes the cells to lose water and gradually shrink.

If freezing occurs slowly, the ice crystals outside the cells have more time to grow.

Larger crystals occupy more space and place greater mechanical stress on the surrounding tissues.

Delicate cell walls and membranes may become stretched, compressed or ruptured.

When the food is thawed, these damaged cells are often unable to retain water effectively.

The result may be a softer texture, increased drip loss and a reduction in overall quality.

When freezing occurs more rapidly, many smaller ice crystals tend to form throughout the tissue.

Because each crystal remains relatively small, the surrounding structures experience less physical disruption.

More cells remain intact.

More water is retained after thawing.

The original texture is therefore better preserved.

It is important to recognise that ice crystals are not inherently harmful.

They are an unavoidable consequence of freezing.

The objective of preservation engineering is not to prevent ice formation.

It is to manage the freezing process so that the biological structure is disturbed as little as possible.

Understanding this distinction explains why two foods stored at exactly the same final temperature may differ greatly in quality after thawing.

The difference often lies not in the temperature itself, but in the path taken to reach it.

Ice crystals influence the physical structure of food.

Their development, however, depends strongly upon one factor.

Time.

How quickly food passes through the freezing process largely determines the size and distribution of the crystals that form.

The rate of freezing therefore becomes one of the most important variables in preservation.

## Part IV — The Importance of Freezing Rate

Not all freezing processes are the same.

Two foods may reach exactly the same storage temperature while developing very different internal structures.

The difference lies in the speed at which freezing occurs.

When food cools slowly, it spends a relatively long time within the temperature range in which ice crystals actively grow.

During this period, existing crystals continue to attract nearby water molecules.

Instead of forming many new crystals, the existing crystals gradually become larger.

As these crystals expand, they displace surrounding tissues and increase the physical stress placed upon cells.

Water continues to move towards the growing crystals, causing further dehydration of the cells.

By the time freezing is complete, much of the original cellular structure may have been altered.

Rapid freezing follows a different path.

The temperature falls quickly through the crystal formation zone.

Large numbers of ice crystals begin to form almost simultaneously.

Because the available water is divided among many growing crystals, each crystal remains relatively small.

The freezing process is completed before individual crystals have time to grow extensively.

As a result, the biological structure is disturbed less.

After thawing, more cells remain intact.

More water is retained within the tissue.

Texture, firmness and appearance are generally preserved more effectively.

The objective of rapid freezing is therefore not to make food colder.

It is to reduce the opportunity for large ice crystals to develop.

This distinction is important.

The final storage temperature may be identical.

The preservation outcome may not.

The path taken during freezing often determines the quality experienced after thawing.

For this reason, preservation engineers pay attention not only to storage conditions, but also to the freezing process itself.

The journey into the frozen state is just as important as the frozen state itself.

Rapid freezing helps preserve biological structure.

Once the food is completely frozen, however, another question arises.

Does freezing stop deterioration forever?

The answer depends upon what continues to happen during frozen storage.

## Part V — Frozen Does Not Mean Unchanging

When food becomes frozen, biological activity is reduced to an exceptionally low level.

Microorganisms can no longer grow.

Respiration effectively ceases.

The movement of water is greatly restricted.

These changes make freezing one of the most effective methods of preserving fresh food.

Yet freezing does not create a perfectly stable system.

Time continues to pass.

Molecules continue to move, although far more slowly than in liquid water.

Some chemical reactions remain possible.

Certain enzymes retain limited activity.

Pigments may gradually change.

Fats can slowly oxidise.

Vitamins may slowly degrade during prolonged storage.

The rates of these processes are extremely low compared with those in refrigerated or fresh food.

Nevertheless, they are not zero.

The quality of frozen food therefore continues to change over time.

Physical changes may also occur.

Small ice crystals are not always permanent.

During storage, water molecules can slowly migrate through the frozen tissue.

Larger ice crystals gradually grow at the expense of smaller ones.

This process, known as recrystallisation, becomes more pronounced when frozen food experiences repeated fluctuations in temperature.

As ice crystals become larger, they increase mechanical stress on surrounding tissues.

The result may be greater cellular damage, increased drip loss after thawing and a gradual decline in texture.

For this reason, maintaining a stable storage temperature is often just as important as achieving rapid freezing.

Freezing therefore provides remarkable stability.

It does not provide complete permanence.

Every preservation method delays change.

None eliminates it entirely.

The objective of frozen storage is not to preserve food forever.

It is to slow deterioration sufficiently that quality remains acceptable throughout the intended period of storage.

## Closing Reflection

Even after months in frozen storage, much of the biological structure remains intact.

The greatest changes often occur not during freezing itself, but when the food returns to a liquid environment.

Thawing therefore becomes the final stage of the freezing process.

Understanding how frozen food is thawed is just as important as understanding how it was frozen.
