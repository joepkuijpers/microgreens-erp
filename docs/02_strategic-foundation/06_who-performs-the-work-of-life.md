# Chapter 6 — Who Performs the Work of Life?

> *Life is organised. Life changes. Life flows. Life moves. Life is sustained through continuous exchange. Every change depends upon invisible workers.*

## Introduction

A sliced apple gradually turns brown.

Fresh herbs slowly lose their aroma.

A banana becomes sweeter as it ripens.

Bread dough rises.

Seeds germinate.

Milk becomes yoghurt.

These changes appear completely different.

Yet they all have something in common.

None happen by chance.

Every biological change is carried out by countless microscopic workers operating inside living cells.

These workers are known as enzymes.

Although invisible to the naked eye, enzymes perform almost every task required to sustain life.

They build.

They repair.

They recycle.

They transport.

They defend.

They transform.

Without them, life would become motionless.

The biological clocks described throughout this book would simply stop.

Yet enzymes do not decide what should happen.

They simply perform the work that biology requires.

To understand preservation, we must therefore understand not only the biological clocks, but also the workers that keep those clocks moving.

Imagine an entire city.

Roads connect every district.

Electricity reaches every building.

Water flows through pipes.

Communication networks connect every neighbourhood.

The city is perfectly organised.

Now imagine that every worker suddenly disappears.

No engineers repair broken bridges.

No drivers deliver food.

No electricians maintain the power grid.

No cleaners remove waste.

The city still exists.

Its organisation has not disappeared.

Yet almost immediately, everything begins to slow.

Soon afterwards, nothing functions at all.

Living organisms face the same challenge.

DNA stores information.

Cells provide structure.

Water allows movement.

Energy powers biological activity.

But none of these can perform work on their own.

Something must actually carry out the countless tasks required every second.

Enzymes are those workers.

Every living cell contains thousands of different enzymes.

Each performs a specific task.

Some build complex biological molecules.

Others break them apart.

Some repair damaged DNA.

Others produce protective compounds.

Many simply prepare molecules for the next step in a much larger biological process.

Life therefore depends not only upon organisation, water, energy and exchange.

It also depends upon work.

And nearly all biological work is performed by enzymes.

# Part II — Enzymes Do Not Think

The remarkable efficiency of living organisms can easily give the impression that enzymes make decisions.

An apple ripens at the right time.

A seed begins to germinate when conditions improve.

A wound heals.

Sugars are converted into energy.

Everything appears carefully coordinated.

Yet enzymes possess no awareness.

They do not think.

They do not plan.

They do not choose which reaction should happen next.

Each enzyme performs only one specific task.

If the required molecule is present, the enzyme acts.

If it is absent, nothing happens.

This simplicity is one of biology's greatest strengths.

Instead of relying on intelligent decision-making, living organisms rely on organisation.

Every enzyme occupies a particular place.

Every reaction occurs under particular conditions.

Every product becomes the starting material for another reaction.

Together, thousands of simple reactions create behaviour that appears astonishingly complex.

Imagine a large factory.

Each worker performs only a single operation.

One tightens a bolt.

Another paints a surface.

A third inspects the finished product.

No individual worker understands how the entire factory operates.

Yet together they produce something far more complex than any one worker could create alone.

Living cells function in much the same way.

Every enzyme performs one small task.

One enzyme breaks down starch.

Another builds proteins.

Another repairs damaged DNA.

Another converts one sugar into another.

No enzyme understands the organism.

Yet together they sustain life.

This also explains why preservation is so effective.

When temperature falls...

the workers move more slowly.

When water becomes unavailable...

the workers cannot reach the materials they require.

When oxygen becomes limited...

many workers can no longer perform their normal tasks.

The workers themselves have not changed.

Only the conditions in which they operate have changed.

This is one of the most important principles of biological engineering.

Engineering rarely changes the workers.

It changes their working environment.

The biological response follows naturally.

# Part III — Every Worker Needs the Right Conditions

Imagine asking a skilled carpenter to build a table.

Now remove every tool.

Turn off the lights.

Lower the temperature until the workshop is freezing.

Scatter the materials throughout the building.

The carpenter has not forgotten the craft.

The ability to work still exists.

The environment no longer allows that work to be performed efficiently.

Enzymes face the same challenge.

An enzyme may be perfectly capable of carrying out its task.

Yet without the right conditions, its activity slows dramatically or stops altogether.

Temperature is one of the most familiar examples.

At low temperatures, molecules move more slowly.

Successful encounters become less frequent.

The enzyme spends more time waiting than working.

As temperature rises, molecular movement increases.

Encounters become more common.

Reactions proceed more rapidly.

However, every enzyme has its limits.

Excessive heat disrupts the delicate three-dimensional structure that allows the enzyme to function.

The worker remains present.

The tools no longer fit.

Water is equally important.

As we discovered earlier, life happens through water.

Water allows molecules to move.

It enables enzymes to encounter the substances upon which they act.

As available water decreases, these encounters become increasingly difficult.

The workshop becomes quieter.

Respiration also influences the working environment.

Many enzymes depend upon oxygen either directly or indirectly through the energy supplied by respiration.

When oxygen becomes limited, cells adjust their metabolism.

Some enzymatic pathways slow.

Others become more active.

The work changes because the environment has changed.

Acidity, expressed as pH, provides another example.

Small changes in pH can alter the electrical charges that hold an enzyme in its functional shape.

The enzyme itself has not disappeared.

Its working conditions have changed.

This reveals an important principle.

Enzymes rarely determine whether biology occurs.

The environment determines how effectively enzymes can perform their work.

The workers respond.

The conditions decide.

# Part IV — Life Is Built from Chains

Imagine a relay race.

No runner completes the entire course.

Each athlete runs only one section before passing the baton to the next.

The success of the team depends not on one exceptional runner, but on every successful handover.

Life works in much the same way.

No enzyme performs an entire biological process.

Each carries out only one small reaction.

When that task is complete, the product is passed to another enzyme.

That enzyme performs the next step.

Its product becomes the starting point for yet another reaction.

One small change leads to another.

Then another.

Then another.

Gradually, simple reactions become complex biological processes.

This explains why life appears so remarkably organised.

There is no central manager directing every reaction.

There is no enzyme overseeing the entire cell.

Organisation emerges because every reaction prepares the way for the next.

Like runners passing a baton, enzymes pass molecules through continuous chains of reactions.

These chains are known as metabolic pathways.

Some pathways release energy through respiration.

Others produce pigments during fruit ripening.

Some build cell walls.

Others repair damaged tissues.

Many synthesise vitamins, flavours and aromas.

Although each pathway serves a different purpose, they all follow the same principle.

Every step depends upon the successful completion of the previous one.

This creates both remarkable efficiency and remarkable vulnerability.

If one enzyme slows, every reaction that depends upon it may also slow.

If one important molecule becomes unavailable, an entire pathway may be interrupted.

A single change can therefore influence an entire biological system.

Preservation takes advantage of this principle.

We rarely need to stop every reaction.

By slowing key reactions within important pathways, many other processes naturally slow as well.

The biological clocks become quieter.

Respiration consumes reserves more slowly.

Ripening is delayed.

Quality is preserved for longer.

Life is therefore not maintained by individual reactions.

It is maintained by countless interconnected chains working together every moment.

# Part V — Life Continues Through Balance

Throughout this book, we have gradually uncovered how living systems function.

Organisation provides structure.

Water allows movement.

Energy drives change.

Respiration supplies the energy required for life.

Enzymes perform the countless tasks that sustain every cell.

Yet one important question remains.

If life depends upon so many interconnected processes, why does it not collapse whenever conditions change?

The answer lies in balance.

Living systems are never perfectly stable.

Temperature changes throughout the day.

Water availability rises and falls.

Nutrients become more or less abundant.

Light intensity varies.

Oxygen concentrations change.

Despite these continual disturbances, life usually continues.

Not because biological systems resist change.

But because they constantly adjust to it.

Some reactions become faster.

Others slow.

Resources are redirected.

New compounds are produced.

Old compounds are recycled.

Thousands of small adjustments occur simultaneously.

Together, these adjustments allow the organism to continue functioning under changing conditions.

Harvest changes this balance.

The harvested tissue remains capable of adjustment.

Yet its ability to recover becomes increasingly limited.

Water can no longer be replaced.

Sugars are no longer produced through photosynthesis.

Minerals are no longer supplied by the roots.

Every adjustment draws upon finite reserves.

Gradually, the balance shifts.

Repair becomes slower than deterioration.

Maintenance becomes more difficult than damage.

The biological system does not suddenly fail.

It slowly loses its ability to maintain itself.

This is an important distinction.

Deterioration is not a single event.

It is the gradual loss of biological balance.

Preservation therefore seeks more than slowing individual reactions.

Engineering seeks to create conditions in which biological systems can maintain their balance for as long as possible.

The longer that balance can be sustained, the longer quality can be preserved.
