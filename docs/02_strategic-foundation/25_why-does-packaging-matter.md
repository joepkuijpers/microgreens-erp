# Chapter 25 — Why Does Packaging Matter?

> *"Preservation protects food during processing. Packaging protects it afterwards."*

## Introduction

Preserving food is only part of the journey.

Once processing is complete, the food immediately begins interacting with its surroundings again.

The surrounding air contains oxygen.

Water vapour constantly moves through the environment.

Light carries energy.

Temperature continues to change throughout storage and transport.

Microorganisms remain present on surfaces and in the air.

Without protection, these environmental factors gradually influence the food once more.

Moisture can return.

Oxygen can trigger chemical reactions.

Aroma compounds may escape.

Microorganisms may once again find favourable conditions for growth.

The effectiveness of every preservation method therefore depends not only on the preservation process itself, but also on how well the preserved product is protected afterwards.

Packaging provides this protection.

Its purpose extends far beyond holding food together.

It creates a controlled environment between the food and the outside world.

By limiting unwanted exchanges with the surrounding environment, packaging helps maintain the conditions established during preservation.

Different foods require different forms of protection.

Fresh vegetables continue to respire.

Frozen foods must remain frozen.

Dried products must remain dry.

Freeze-dried foods must be protected from even small amounts of moisture.

Packaging therefore cannot be considered separately from preservation.

It is a continuation of the same biological strategy.

To understand packaging, we must first understand the environment that constantly surrounds every food product.

Even after preservation, the environment never stops changing.

The environment surrounding food is not empty space.

It contains gases.

Water vapour.

Light.

Heat.

Microorganisms.

Each continuously interacts with the product.

Understanding packaging therefore begins with understanding what must be kept out—and sometimes what must be kept in.

## Part I — The Environment Never Stops Acting

Although preserved food may appear unchanged during storage, it continues to interact with its surroundings.

This interaction never completely stops.

Water molecules move from areas of higher moisture to areas of lower moisture.

Gases diffuse through the air and, in many cases, through packaging materials.

Heat flows from warmer regions towards cooler ones.

Light transfers energy whenever it reaches the surface of the food.

These processes occur naturally.

They require no biological activity.

They are driven by the laws of physics.

Biology responds to these physical changes.

As moisture enters a dried product, microorganisms may once again find conditions suitable for growth.

As oxygen reaches fats and pigments, chemical reactions begin to alter flavour, colour and nutritional quality.

As temperatures fluctuate, reaction rates increase or decrease.

Even light can trigger changes in sensitive compounds, leading to the gradual loss of colour, flavour or vitamins.

The purpose of packaging is therefore not to stop nature.

Nature cannot be stopped.

Instead, packaging slows the exchange between the food and its surroundings.

It acts as a controlled boundary.

Some exchanges are greatly reduced.

Others are carefully regulated.

In certain situations, selected gases are even allowed to pass through the package because they help maintain food quality.

Understanding packaging therefore begins with recognising that every food product exists within a constantly changing environment.

Packaging does not remove the environment.

It manages the relationship between the environment and the food.

Not every part of the surrounding environment presents the same challenge.

For many foods, one factor influences quality more than any other.

Water.

## Part II — Water Is Always Moving

Water is rarely still.

Even after preservation, water continues to move whenever differences in moisture exist.

This movement is driven by one simple principle.

Water naturally moves from regions where it is more abundant towards regions where it is less abundant.

Although invisible, this process occurs continuously.

A dry biscuit left uncovered gradually becomes soft.

Salt absorbs moisture from the surrounding air.

Fresh vegetables slowly lose water, becoming wilted during storage.

Each example demonstrates the same physical principle.

Water seeks balance.

For preserved foods, this movement can become a significant challenge.

Dry foods depend upon maintaining a low moisture content.

If water gradually enters the product, its physical properties begin to change.

Texture may soften.

Powders may form lumps.

Crisp products lose their brittleness.

Most importantly, increasing moisture may once again create conditions that support biological activity.

Packaging therefore acts as a barrier that slows the movement of water.

The effectiveness of this barrier depends upon both the packaging material and the surrounding environment.

Some materials allow water vapour to pass relatively easily.

Others provide a much stronger barrier.

No packaging material is completely impermeable.

Instead, each slows the movement of moisture to a different extent.

The appropriate barrier depends upon the food being protected.

Fresh produce often requires some exchange of moisture to remain healthy.

Completely sealing the product may create condensation and accelerate spoilage.

Freeze-dried foods require the opposite approach.

Their extremely low moisture content must be preserved.

Even small amounts of absorbed water can reduce crispness, shorten shelf life and allow unwanted chemical or biological changes to begin.

Understanding moisture movement therefore allows packaging to be matched to the biological needs of the food.

The objective is not to prevent all movement.

It is to control it carefully enough that the preserved food remains stable throughout storage and distribution.

Water is not the only substance that moves through packaging.

The air surrounding every food product contains gases that continuously interact with biological tissues.

Among these gases, one has a particularly important influence on food quality.

Oxygen.

## Part III — Oxygen: Essential for Life, Responsible for Change

Oxygen is one of the most abundant gases in Earth's atmosphere.

It makes complex life possible.

Plants, animals and many microorganisms depend upon it.

Yet the same gas that supports life also contributes to the gradual deterioration of food.

Oxygen is highly reactive.

When it comes into contact with certain components of food, chemical reactions begin to occur.

Fats may become rancid.

Natural pigments slowly lose their colour.

Sensitive vitamins can degrade.

Aromas gradually change.

These reactions often occur slowly.

They may not be immediately visible.

Over weeks or months, however, their effects become increasingly noticeable.

Not all foods are equally sensitive to oxygen.

Products containing large amounts of unsaturated fats are particularly vulnerable to oxidation.

Dried herbs may lose their characteristic aroma.

Coffee slowly loses its freshness after roasting.

Freeze-dried fruits can become discoloured if exposed to oxygen for extended periods.

Packaging therefore often aims to reduce the amount of oxygen surrounding the food.

One approach is vacuum packaging, in which much of the air is removed before the package is sealed.

Another approach replaces the air with gases such as nitrogen or carbon dioxide.

These gases are far less reactive than oxygen and help slow many of the chemical changes that occur during storage.

In some situations, oxygen absorbers are placed inside the package.

These small sachets remove the remaining oxygen after sealing, creating an environment that further slows oxidation.

The objective is not to eliminate oxygen because it is harmful.

Rather, it is to limit unnecessary chemical reactions that reduce food quality over time.

Packaging therefore controls more than moisture.

It also manages the atmosphere surrounding the food, helping to preserve flavour, colour, nutritional quality and shelf life.

Moisture and oxygen are often the primary concerns in food packaging.

They are not the only environmental influences.

Light also carries energy.

When this energy reaches sensitive foods, it can trigger changes that continue even inside a sealed package.

## Part IV — Light: More Than Illumination

Light allows us to see food.

For the food itself, however, light is a source of energy.

When this energy is absorbed by certain molecules, chemical reactions can begin.

These reactions continue even when the food is dry, refrigerated or otherwise preserved.

The effect depends upon both the food and the type of light.

Ultraviolet light carries particularly high amounts of energy and can rapidly damage sensitive compounds.

Visible light is less energetic but may still influence food during prolonged storage.

Natural pigments are especially vulnerable.

Bright colours may gradually fade.

Chlorophyll in green vegetables slowly breaks down.

Anthocyanins in berries can lose their intensity.

Carotenoids may become less vibrant.

Light also affects flavour.

Certain aromatic compounds slowly degrade when repeatedly exposed to light.

Oils containing unsaturated fatty acids become more susceptible to oxidation.

Some vitamins, including riboflavin and vitamin C, are also sensitive to prolonged light exposure.

For this reason, packaging is often designed not only to contain food but also to regulate the amount of light reaching it.

Transparent packaging allows consumers to inspect the product before purchase.

This can improve confidence but offers less protection against light.

Opaque packaging blocks much of the incoming light, helping preserve colour, flavour and nutritional quality during extended storage.

Some products require a balance between these two approaches.

Others benefit from almost complete protection from light throughout their shelf life.

The appropriate choice depends upon the biological and chemical characteristics of the food.

Packaging therefore performs another important function.

It controls not only matter entering the package but also energy reaching the food.

By reducing unnecessary exposure to light, packaging helps preserve the quality established during processing.

Moisture.

Oxygen.

Light.

Each influences food without direct contact.

Temperature has an equally important role.

Unlike the previous factors, however, temperature affects almost every physical, chemical and biological process occurring within food.

## Part V — Temperature: The Pace of Change

Temperature influences every food product throughout its lifetime.

Whether fresh, frozen, dried or freeze-dried, food continuously responds to the amount of thermal energy present in its surroundings.

Unlike moisture or oxygen, temperature does not directly alter the composition of food.

Instead, it influences the speed at which physical, chemical and biological processes occur.

As temperature increases, molecules move more rapidly.

Chemical reactions occur more frequently.

Enzymes become more active.

Many microorganisms grow more quickly.

Conversely, lowering the temperature reduces molecular movement.

Chemical reactions slow.

Enzymatic activity decreases.

Microbial growth becomes increasingly restricted.

This explains why temperature is one of the most effective tools in food preservation.

Cooling slows deterioration.

Freezing slows it even further by converting liquid water into ice.

Even after preservation, however, temperature continues to influence food quality.

A dried product stored in a warm warehouse may deteriorate faster than the same product stored in cooler conditions.

Chocolate may soften and bloom.

Oils oxidise more rapidly.

Aromas may gradually disappear.

Repeated fluctuations in temperature can create additional challenges.

Packages may experience condensation as warm, moist air cools.

Frozen products may partially thaw before refreezing, affecting texture and quality.

Even foods with a long shelf life benefit from stable storage conditions.

Packaging cannot control temperature by itself.

It can only slow the transfer of heat between the food and its surroundings.

The storage environment therefore remains just as important as the package itself.

Successful preservation depends upon both working together.

Temperature management does not replace preservation.

It extends the protection that preservation has already created.

By slowing the processes responsible for deterioration, stable temperatures help maintain food quality throughout storage, transport and distribution.

Moisture.

Oxygen.

Light.

Temperature.

Each acts continuously upon food throughout its lifetime.

Yet one important factor remains.

Living organisms are everywhere in our environment, constantly searching for conditions that allow them to grow.

## Part VI — Microorganisms: Waiting for Opportunity

Microorganisms are present almost everywhere.

They exist in the air.

On surfaces.

In soil.

On plants.

On animals.

And naturally on many foods.

Their presence alone does not determine whether food will spoil.

What matters is whether the environment allows them to grow.

Like all living organisms, microorganisms depend upon suitable conditions.

They require water.

Many require oxygen.

They grow within specific temperature ranges.

Some are sensitive to light.

Others are unaffected by it.

When these conditions are favourable, microorganisms reproduce.

Their populations increase.

As they grow, they consume nutrients within the food and produce metabolic by-products.

These changes gradually alter flavour, texture, appearance and aroma.

Some microorganisms are responsible for desirable foods such as bread, yoghurt and cheese.

Others cause spoilage.

A smaller number can produce toxins or cause disease.

Food preservation therefore is not about eliminating all microorganisms.

In many cases, that would be neither practical nor desirable.

Instead, preservation changes the environment so that unwanted microorganisms can no longer grow or grow only very slowly.

Packaging helps maintain these protective conditions.

By limiting the movement of moisture, oxygen and contaminants, it reduces the opportunity for microorganisms to become active again.

Once the package is opened, however, the food begins interacting with the surrounding environment once more.

Fresh air enters.

Moisture may return.

New microorganisms can be introduced through handling.

For this reason, many preserved foods have different storage recommendations before and after opening.

Packaging therefore extends preservation, but it cannot preserve food indefinitely once the protective environment has been disturbed.

Successful food preservation depends upon maintaining conditions that remain unfavourable for unwanted microbial growth from production until consumption.

Packaging has now been shown to regulate moisture, oxygen, light, temperature and exposure to microorganisms.

Together these factors determine how effectively food remains protected after preservation.

The next question is no longer what packaging protects against.

It is how different packaging materials achieve that protection.

## Part VII — Packaging Materials: Different Barriers for Different Needs

No packaging material is perfect.

Each provides protection against certain environmental influences while allowing others to pass more easily.

The choice of packaging therefore depends upon the biological requirements of the food rather than the material itself.

Some foods require excellent protection from moisture.

Others are more sensitive to oxygen.

Certain products must be protected from light, while fresh fruits and vegetables may benefit from limited gas exchange.

Packaging materials differ because their microscopic structures differ.

Some contain tightly packed molecules that slow the movement of gases and water vapour.

Others contain larger spaces through which molecules can move more easily.

As a result, every material has its own barrier properties.

Glass provides an excellent barrier against gases, moisture and aromas.

It also remains chemically stable during long-term storage.

Its disadvantages are weight and fragility.

Metals such as steel and aluminium provide an almost complete barrier to light, oxygen and moisture.

This makes them highly suitable for long shelf-life products.

However, they require protective coatings to prevent unwanted reactions with certain foods.

Paper and cardboard are lightweight, renewable and easy to recycle.

On their own, however, they provide only limited protection against moisture and gases.

For this reason, they are often combined with other materials.

Plastics form a diverse group of packaging materials.

Some provide excellent moisture barriers.

Others are particularly effective against oxygen.

Many combine flexibility, low weight and low manufacturing costs.

Because no single plastic performs perfectly under all conditions, multilayer structures are commonly used.

These combine several materials, allowing each layer to contribute a specific protective function.

The objective is rarely to create the strongest possible barrier.

Instead, the goal is to create the most appropriate barrier for the food being protected.

Good packaging is therefore not defined by the material from which it is made.

It is defined by how effectively it maintains the environment required for that particular food.

Choosing the right material is only part of packaging design.

The atmosphere sealed inside the package is equally important.

Sometimes ordinary air is sufficient.

In other situations, changing the composition of the gases surrounding the food can significantly extend its quality and shelf life.

## Part VIII — The Atmosphere Inside the Package

Packaging protects food from the surrounding environment.

The environment inside the package, however, is equally important.

When food is sealed, the gases enclosed with it continue to influence its quality.

Ordinary air consists primarily of nitrogen and oxygen, with smaller amounts of carbon dioxide, argon and other gases.

Each of these gases behaves differently.

Oxygen promotes many chemical reactions and supports the growth of aerobic microorganisms.

Carbon dioxide can slow the growth of many spoilage organisms.

Nitrogen is largely unreactive and is often used simply to occupy space within the package.

By changing the composition of the atmosphere, food manufacturers can create conditions that better suit the biological needs of a particular product.

One familiar example is vacuum packaging.

Before sealing, much of the air is removed from the package.

Reducing the amount of oxygen slows oxidation and limits the growth of many microorganisms that depend upon oxygen.

Vacuum packaging also reduces the volume of the package, making storage and transport more efficient.

Not all foods are suitable for a vacuum.

Soft products may become compressed.

Fresh fruits and vegetables continue to respire after harvest and may require a controlled supply of oxygen rather than its complete removal.

For these products, modified atmosphere packaging provides an alternative.

Instead of removing all the air, the atmosphere inside the package is adjusted.

The proportions of oxygen, carbon dioxide and nitrogen are selected according to the needs of the food.

The objective is not to create a universal atmosphere.

It is to create the most appropriate atmosphere for a specific biological system.

Some products also contain oxygen absorbers.

These small sachets continue removing residual oxygen after the package has been sealed, providing additional protection during long-term storage.

Whether by removing air, replacing it or continuously modifying it, the principle remains the same.

The atmosphere surrounding the food is managed to slow the physical, chemical and biological processes responsible for deterioration.

Packaging therefore protects food not only through the material surrounding it, but also through the invisible environment enclosed within it.

Even with carefully selected materials and a controlled atmosphere, packaging cannot preserve food forever.

Over time, every barrier allows some exchange with the environment.

Understanding shelf life therefore requires understanding that protection is always a matter of degree, never of absolute isolation.

## Part IX — Understanding Shelf Life

Shelf life is often thought of as a fixed period of time.

In reality, it is the result of continuous change.

From the moment food is harvested, processed and packaged, physical, chemical and biological processes continue to occur.

Preservation slows these processes.

Packaging slows them further.

Neither stops them completely.

Shelf life therefore is not determined by a single factor.

It reflects the combined influence of the food, the preservation method, the packaging and the storage conditions.

A freeze-dried product stored in a warm, humid environment may lose quality more rapidly than the same product stored in a cool, dry place.

Fresh produce packaged in an unsuitable atmosphere may deteriorate despite being recently harvested.

Even canned foods gradually undergo slow chemical changes during long-term storage.

The objective of packaging is therefore not to create permanent stability.

Its purpose is to slow change sufficiently that the food remains safe and retains an acceptable quality for its intended period of use.

Different foods also reach the end of their shelf life for different reasons.

Some become microbiologically unsafe.

Others remain safe but lose flavour, colour or texture.

Certain products simply no longer meet the quality expected by consumers, even though they may still be edible.

For this reason, shelf life should not be understood as a precise moment at which food suddenly changes from good to bad.

It is more accurately described as a gradual decline in quality over time.

The rate of this decline depends upon how effectively the environment surrounding the food has been managed.

Every decision made during preservation contributes to this outcome.

The choice of preservation method.

The packaging material.

The atmosphere within the package.

The storage temperature.

Exposure to moisture, oxygen and light.

Together these determine how long the desired qualities of the food can be maintained.

Shelf life is therefore not created by the package alone.

It is the consequence of successfully managing the environment from harvest until consumption.

Packaging is often viewed simply as the final step before food reaches the consumer.

In reality, it is an integral part of preservation itself.

By managing the environment surrounding food, packaging extends the protection created during processing and helps preserve quality throughout the remainder of the food's journey.

## Closing Reflection

Food preservation does not end when processing is complete.

The environment continues to act upon every food product throughout storage, transport and consumption.

Water continues to move.

Oxygen continues to react.

Light continues to transfer energy.

Temperature continues to influence the speed of change.

Microorganisms continue searching for favourable conditions.

Packaging exists because these natural processes never stop.

Its purpose is not to isolate food completely from the outside world.

Such isolation is rarely possible.

Instead, packaging carefully manages the relationship between food and its environment.

By slowing the exchange of matter and energy, it extends the protection established during preservation.

Different foods require different environments.

Fresh produce must continue to respire.

Frozen foods must remain frozen.

Dried products must remain dry.

Freeze-dried foods require exceptional protection from moisture and oxygen.

There is therefore no universal packaging solution.

The most effective package is the one that provides the environment most appropriate for the biological characteristics of the food.

This principle extends beyond packaging itself.

Successful food preservation is never achieved through a single technology.

It is the result of many carefully coordinated decisions.

How the food is harvested.

How it is processed.

How it is packaged.

How it is transported.

How it is stored.

Each stage influences the next.

When these stages are designed as one continuous system, food quality can be maintained for far longer while reducing unnecessary waste.

Packaging is therefore not the final step in preservation.

It is the continuation of preservation beyond the processing facility.

It protects the environment created during preservation until the moment the food is finally consumed.

Understanding this relationship reveals a broader principle that applies throughout this book.

Food quality is not determined by one technology alone.

It is determined by how well every stage works together to support the biology of the food.

Packaging preserves the environment surrounding food.

Eventually, however, every food product leaves storage and begins its journey through distribution.

During transport, new challenges emerge.

Products experience vibration, changing temperatures, varying humidity, repeated handling and long travel distances.

Understanding how preservation extends beyond the factory therefore requires understanding the role of logistics.
