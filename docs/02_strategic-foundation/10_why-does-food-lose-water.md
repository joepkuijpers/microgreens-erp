# Chapter 10 — Why Does Food Lose Water?

> *"Life depends upon water. Preservation begins by understanding where it goes."*

## Introduction

Freshly harvested food begins to change from the very moment it is separated from the organism that produced it.

Leaves become softer.

Fruit gradually loses firmness.

Bread turns stale.

Meat slowly dries.

Seeds become lighter.

Although these changes appear different, they often share the same underlying cause.

Water is moving.

This movement never truly stops.

Water continually redistributes itself within cells, between tissues and into the surrounding air.

Unlike a living plant, harvested food can no longer regulate this process.

The biological systems responsible for replacing lost water have stopped.

Only the physical laws governing water remain.

Understanding preservation therefore requires more than knowing that water is important.

It requires understanding why water moves, what determines its direction, and how this movement influences every aspect of biological organisation.

Water is not simply a component of food.

It is constantly searching for balance.

Every movement changes the biological environment.

Every movement changes the opportunities available to enzymes, microorganisms and chemical reactions.

Preservation begins by understanding this journey.

## Part I — Water Seeks Balance

Water is never completely still.

Even within living cells, individual water molecules are constantly moving.

They collide.

They separate.

They form new interactions.

This continuous motion is a natural consequence of thermal energy.

Although these movements appear random, they collectively produce predictable patterns.

Whenever two regions contain different amounts of available water, movement occurs.

Water naturally moves from where it is more available to where it is less available.

This process continues until a new balance is reached.

The principle is universal.

It occurs within cells.

Between neighbouring tissues.

Between food and its packaging.

Between harvested crops and the surrounding air.

Every preserved product is continuously exchanging water with its environment.

The speed of this movement depends upon many factors, including temperature, humidity, airflow and the physical structure of the food itself.

The direction, however, follows a remarkably simple principle.

Water always moves towards balance.

Understanding this principle reveals why harvested food gradually loses moisture, why some products absorb water from the air, and why successful preservation depends upon controlling not only the food itself, but also the environment surrounding it.

## Part II — Air Becomes Part of the System

Water does not move into empty space.

It moves into the surrounding environment.

For harvested food, that environment is usually air.

Although air often appears empty, it constantly contains water in the form of invisible water vapour.

The amount of water already present in the air determines how easily additional water can leave the food.

Dry air readily accepts more water.

Moist air accepts far less.

As a result, the same product can lose water rapidly in one environment and only slowly in another.

The food itself may not have changed.

The surrounding air has.

This illustrates an important principle of preservation.

The environment surrounding food is not separate from the food.

It forms part of the preservation system.

Changing the surrounding air changes the movement of water.

This is why ventilation, packaging, storage conditions and climate all influence the rate at which harvested food changes.

Successful preservation therefore requires more than controlling the product alone.

It requires understanding the interaction between the product and its environment.

Food and air continuously influence one another.

Neither can be fully understood in isolation.

Air can either accept water or resist it.

But what determines how much water the air can hold?

To answer that question, we must first understand how temperature influences water itself.

## Part III — Temperature Changes the Journey

Air is never completely full.

Its ability to accept additional water depends largely upon its temperature.

As temperature increases, water molecules move more energetically.

Some escape the surface of food more easily.

At the same time, warmer air is capable of accommodating more water vapour before reaching its limit.

The result is a stronger driving force for water to leave the food.

Conversely, cooler air reaches its capacity much sooner.

As the surrounding air approaches its limit, the movement of water slows.

Eventually, a balance is reached in which little or no further water is lost.

Temperature therefore influences preservation in two important ways.

It changes the behaviour of the water within the food.

It also changes the capacity of the surrounding air to receive that water.

Neither the food nor the air can be considered independently.

They form one continuously interacting system.

Understanding this relationship explains why identical foods dry at different rates under different environmental conditions.

The difference often lies not within the food itself, but within the environment surrounding it.

Temperature changes the capacity of air.

Yet capacity alone does not determine whether water moves.

Movement depends upon the difference between the food and its surroundings.

To understand this difference, we must first understand humidity.

## Part IV — Measuring Balance

The atmosphere is constantly changing.

Sometimes it readily accepts additional water.

At other times, it is already close to its limit.

To describe this condition, we use the concept of relative humidity.

Relative humidity compares the amount of water vapour currently present in the air with the maximum amount the air could contain at the same temperature.

It is expressed as a percentage.

A relative humidity of 100% does not mean the air contains as much water as possible under every condition.

It means the air has reached its maximum capacity at its current temperature.

If the temperature changes, that capacity changes as well.

This explains why relative humidity is not simply a measure of water.

It is a measure of balance.

When the surrounding air has a low relative humidity, it can readily accept additional water.

Water therefore continues to leave the food.

As relative humidity increases, the driving force for water movement becomes progressively smaller.

Eventually, a point is reached where little or no net movement occurs.

The food and the surrounding air have approached equilibrium.

Understanding relative humidity allows us to predict how the environment will influence harvested food.

It explains why the same product dries differently in different climates, storage rooms or packaging systems.

Rather than viewing humidity as a weather measurement, preservation views it as an indicator of how strongly the environment encourages or resists the movement of water.

Relative humidity describes the condition of the air.

It tells us how close the environment is to balance.

But it does not explain the force that actually drives water from one place to another.

To understand that force, we must look beyond percentages and examine the energy contained within water itself.

## Part V — The Driving Force

Water does not move simply because air is present.

It moves because the conditions on either side are different.

Every water molecule possesses energy.

Some molecules have enough energy to escape from the surface of the food and enter the surrounding air.

Others do not.

At the same time, water molecules within the air continually collide with the food and return to its surface.

These two processes occur simultaneously.

Water is constantly leaving.

Water is constantly returning.

Whether a product gains or loses moisture depends upon which process dominates.

When more molecules leave than return, the food becomes drier.

When more return than leave, the food absorbs moisture.

When both occur at the same rate, the system has reached equilibrium.

The force behind this continuous exchange is known as vapour pressure.

Vapour pressure reflects the tendency of water molecules to escape into the surrounding air.

Differences in vapour pressure create the driving force that moves water from one location to another.

The greater the difference, the stronger the movement.

The smaller the difference, the slower the exchange.

Ultimately, water continues moving until these differences disappear.

Understanding vapour pressure transforms drying from a simple observation into a predictable physical process.

Water does not disappear.

It responds to differences in energy until balance is restored.

Water naturally moves towards balance.

Preservation therefore does not create this movement.

It guides it.

The challenge is not simply removing water, but deciding how quickly, how evenly and how gently that movement should occur.

## Part VI — Guiding the Movement

Water will always seek a new balance.

Preservation cannot prevent this natural process.

It can only influence how it unfolds.

This is one of the central ideas of preservation engineering.

The objective is rarely to remove as much water as possible.

Instead, the objective is to control the movement of water in a way that preserves biological organisation.

Water that leaves too quickly may damage delicate tissues.

Cells can collapse.

Surfaces may harden while moisture remains trapped inside.

Texture changes.

Appearance changes.

Quality is lost.

Water that leaves too slowly presents different challenges.

Microorganisms remain active for longer.

Enzymes continue their work.

Chemical reactions proceed.

The opportunity for deterioration increases.

Successful preservation therefore seeks an appropriate balance.

The rate of water removal should match the characteristics of the product and the desired outcome.

A fragile herb requires a different approach than a grain of wheat.

A strawberry behaves differently from a mushroom.

Each product possesses its own biological structure, and each structure responds differently to the movement of water.

Engineering begins by respecting these biological differences.

Only then can suitable preservation methods be selected.

Drying, freeze drying, refrigeration, packaging and ventilation are not separate disciplines.

They are different ways of guiding the same natural process.

The technology may differ.

The underlying principle remains the same.

Water continues to seek balance.

Preservation determines the path it takes.

## Closing Reflection

Engineering therefore does not oppose the movement of water. It seeks to guide that movement in a way that preserves biological organisation.

Water moves differently through every biological structure.

Some tissues release it easily.

Others resist its movement.

To understand why, we must look inside the structure of food itself.
