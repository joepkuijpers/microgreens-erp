# Chapter 24 — Why Does Freeze Drying Preserve Food?

> *"Freeze drying preserves food by removing water without first returning it to the liquid state."*

## Introduction

Throughout this book, water has repeatedly emerged as the foundation of biological activity.

It transports nutrients.

Supports enzyme activity.

Enables microorganisms to grow.

Allows countless biological and chemical reactions to occur.

Every preservation method discussed so far has influenced this relationship in a different way.

Drying removes liquid water.

Cooling slows the biological processes that depend upon it.

Freezing transforms liquid water into ice, greatly restricting biological activity while largely preserving the structure of the food.

Freeze drying combines these principles into a single preservation method.

The food is first frozen.

The biological activity associated with liquid water is therefore greatly reduced.

Instead of intentionally allowing the bulk ice to melt during drying, the frozen water is removed directly from the solid state.

Most of the frozen water therefore leaves without passing through a liquid phase.

This distinction is fundamental.

In conventional drying, water evaporates as a liquid.

During freeze drying, ice disappears as a vapour.

The biological structure experiences remarkably little disturbance.

Cells often retain much of their original architecture.

Shape.

Colour.

Texture.

Aroma.

Many nutritional compounds are also preserved more effectively than during many conventional drying processes.

Freeze drying therefore represents more than another drying technique.

It is a preservation method built upon an understanding of both freezing and drying.

Understanding freeze drying begins with understanding one remarkable physical phenomenon.

Ice does not always have to melt before it disappears.

Water normally changes from solid to liquid before becoming a gas.

Under the right conditions, however, ice can follow a different path.

It can pass directly from the solid state into water vapour.

This process is known as sublimation.

Understanding sublimation is the key to understanding freeze drying.

## Part I — Ice Can Become Vapour

Under ordinary conditions, water follows a familiar path.

Ice melts.

Liquid water forms.

As additional energy is supplied, the liquid gradually evaporates into water vapour.

Freeze drying follows a different route.

After the food has been sufficiently frozen, the surrounding pressure is greatly reduced.

When pressure and product temperature are controlled within the appropriate range, ice can become vapour without first melting.

Instead, it changes directly from the solid state into water vapour.

This remarkable process is known as sublimation.

Although the water disappears, the food never passes through a stage in which large quantities of liquid water are present.

This distinction is fundamental.

Liquid water is responsible for many of the structural changes that occur during conventional drying.

As water evaporates, surface tension develops within the tissues.

Cells shrink.

Delicate structures collapse.

Materials become denser.

Shape is gradually lost.

During freeze drying, these effects are greatly reduced.

The ice leaves the tissue while the overall biological framework remains largely supported by the frozen structure.

Instead of collapsing as water disappears, the food often retains much of its original architecture.

Tiny pores remain where ice crystals once existed.

These pores later allow water to return rapidly during rehydration.

The objective of sublimation is therefore not simply to remove water.

It is to remove water while disturbing the biological structure as little as possible.

This explains why freeze-dried foods often retain their original shape far better than foods dried by conventional methods.

Sublimation is therefore more than an unusual physical phenomenon.

It is the principle that allows freeze drying to preserve both structure and quality.

Sublimation removes frozen water remarkably gently.

The process, however, cannot begin immediately after food is placed inside a freeze dryer.

The biological structure must first be prepared.

Successful freeze drying therefore begins long before sublimation starts.

It begins with freezing itself.

## Part II — Freezing Creates the Structure

Freeze drying begins long before water is removed.

It begins with freezing.

At first glance, this may appear to be nothing more than preparation.

In reality, freezing determines much of the final quality of the freeze-dried food.

As water freezes, ice crystals form throughout the biological tissue.

These crystals temporarily occupy the spaces once filled with liquid water.

The surrounding cells and biological structures adapt to this frozen environment.

Although freezing may introduce some structural changes, it also creates a stable framework that supports the food during drying.

This frozen framework is essential.

If the food were dried directly from its liquid state, much of its internal structure would collapse as water disappeared.

Cells would shrink.

Tissues would become compressed.

Delicate biological architecture would be lost.

By freezing first, the structure remains physically supported while the ice is removed.

As sublimation progresses, the spaces once occupied by ice gradually become tiny interconnected pores.

These microscopic channels preserve much of the original internal organisation of the food.

The result is a lightweight, porous structure that closely resembles the original tissue.

This explains two remarkable characteristics of freeze-dried foods.

First, they often retain their original shape far better than conventionally dried products.

Second, they absorb water remarkably quickly during rehydration.

The pores left behind by the ice provide natural pathways through which water can return.

The freezing stage therefore serves a purpose beyond reducing biological activity.

It creates the structural template that allows freeze drying to preserve the physical organisation of the food.

Successful freeze drying begins not with sublimation.

It begins with creating the right frozen structure.

The frozen structure provides mechanical support during drying.

Removing the ice, however, requires carefully controlled environmental conditions.

Temperature alone is not enough.

Pressure also determines whether ice melts or disappears directly as vapour.

## Part III — Why Pressure Matters

Temperature alone does not determine the physical state of water.

Pressure also plays a fundamental role.

At everyday atmospheric pressure, ice normally melts before it becomes water vapour.

As heat is supplied, solid water first changes into liquid water.

Only afterwards can it evaporate.

Freeze drying requires a different pathway.

The objective is to remove ice without allowing it to melt.

To achieve this, the surrounding pressure is greatly reduced.

Under these conditions, water behaves differently.

Ice can absorb energy and change directly into water vapour without first becoming liquid.

This is the environment required for sublimation.

The vacuum itself does not dry the food.

Nor does it preserve the food directly.

Its purpose is to create the conditions under which sublimation becomes possible.

Without sufficiently low pressure, the ice would melt.

Liquid water would once again fill the biological tissues.

The advantages of freeze drying would largely be lost.

Maintaining the correct pressure therefore becomes just as important as maintaining the correct temperature.

Both variables must remain carefully balanced throughout the drying process.

If the pressure becomes too high, sublimation slows or stops.

If the temperature becomes too high, melting may occur before the ice has been removed.

Successful freeze drying therefore depends upon controlling two environmental factors simultaneously.

Temperature determines how much energy is available.

Pressure determines the path by which water leaves the food.

Neither can achieve freeze drying alone.

Together they create the conditions that allow frozen water to disappear while the biological structure remains largely intact.

Temperature and pressure create the conditions required for sublimation.

The water that leaves the food, however, must still go somewhere.

If the water vapour remained inside the drying chamber, sublimation would gradually slow and eventually stop.

Successful freeze drying therefore requires one final component.

A place for the water vapour to collect.

## Part IV — Water Must Leave the System

As sublimation progresses, ice within the food gradually becomes water vapour.

The water has left the biological tissue.

It has not left the freeze dryer.

Without somewhere for this vapour to go, the atmosphere inside the drying chamber would slowly become saturated.

As more water vapour accumulated, the movement of additional water from the food would become increasingly difficult.

Eventually, sublimation would slow dramatically or stop altogether.

Successful freeze drying therefore requires the continuous removal of water vapour from the drying chamber.

This is achieved by a condenser.

The condenser is maintained at a temperature considerably lower than that of the frozen food.

As water vapour reaches this extremely cold surface, it loses energy.

Instead of remaining a gas, it returns to the solid state as ice.

Under suitable operating conditions, this transferred water is captured without intentionally passing through a liquid phase.

This continuous transfer performs two important functions.

First, it prevents water vapour from accumulating around the food.

Second, it maintains the conditions required for sublimation to continue.

The condenser therefore does far more than collect ice.

It preserves the environmental balance within the freeze dryer.

The food loses water.

The condenser captures that water.

The vacuum maintains the low pressure.

Together these processes create a continuous flow of water from the biological tissue to the condenser.

Freeze drying is therefore not a static process.

It is a carefully controlled movement of water through changing physical states.

Solid ice within the food.

Water vapour within the chamber.

Solid ice again on the condenser.

Understanding this journey explains why freeze drying can continue for many hours while preserving the structure of the food.

This first stage is known as primary drying.

It removes ice through sublimation.

Some water, however, remains associated with the dried structure rather than existing as ice.

During secondary drying, carefully controlled heat and low pressure help remove part of this remaining water through desorption.

The required endpoint depends upon the food, its intended storage period and the protection provided after drying.

Eventually, nearly all of the frozen water and much of the remaining bound water have left the food.

The biological structure remains.

The water does not.

This transformation gives freeze-dried food a set of properties unlike those produced by any other preservation method.

## Part V — Why Freeze-Dried Food Is Different

Once freeze drying is complete, remarkably little water remains within the food.

The biological structure, however, remains largely intact.

This unusual combination gives freeze-dried food a unique set of properties.

At sufficiently low water activity, microbial growth is prevented.

Enzymes lose the medium required for most biological reactions.

Many chemical processes also slow dramatically.

The food can therefore become exceptionally stable during storage when it is protected from moisture, oxygen and unsuitable temperatures.

Freeze drying does not sterilise food.

Microorganisms or spores that survive processing may become active again if water and favourable conditions return.

At the same time, the porous structure created during freeze drying remains largely unchanged.

The microscopic spaces once occupied by ice crystals remain open throughout the tissue.

These pores make the food extremely light.

They also allow water to penetrate rapidly when the food is rehydrated.

As a result, many freeze-dried foods recover much of their original appearance within minutes after water is added.

Some even regain a texture remarkably similar to that of the fresh product.

This ability distinguishes freeze drying from most conventional drying methods.

In many traditionally dried foods, tissues shrink as water is removed.

The structure becomes denser.

Rehydration is often slower and less complete.

Freeze drying largely avoids this collapse by removing water while the biological framework remains supported by ice.

The result is a product that combines a long storage life with rapid rehydration and a high degree of structural preservation.

These advantages explain why freeze drying has become valuable wherever food quality is of particular importance.

Scientific research.

Medical applications.

Space exploration.

Emergency food supplies.

High-quality ingredients.

Long-term food storage.

Each application benefits from the same fundamental principle.

Remove the water.

Preserve the structure.

Reduce biological change.

Freeze drying therefore does not simply produce dry food.

It produces food whose biological architecture has been preserved remarkably well despite the almost complete removal of water.

These remarkable properties do not come without compromise.

Freeze drying requires time.

Energy.

Specialised equipment.

Understanding both its advantages and its limitations allows us to decide when freeze drying is the most appropriate preservation method.

## Part VI — Every Preservation Method Has Its Limits

Freeze drying offers remarkable advantages.

It preserves biological structure.

Maintains many nutritional compounds.

Produces lightweight products with an exceptionally long storage life.

Yet no preservation method is universally superior.

Every technology involves compromises.

One of the greatest challenges of freeze drying is time.

Unlike many conventional drying methods, freeze drying cannot be completed quickly.

The food must first be frozen.

Sublimation then proceeds gradually under carefully controlled conditions.

Depending on the product, the process may require many hours or even several days.

Energy is another important consideration.

Maintaining low temperatures.

Creating a vacuum.

Operating the refrigeration system.

Removing water through sublimation.

Each step requires energy.

Although freeze-dried food often stores efficiently for many years, producing it demands considerably more resources than many other preservation methods.

The equipment itself is also more complex.

A freeze dryer must accurately control temperature, pressure and the removal of water vapour throughout the process.

This makes the technology more expensive than conventional drying equipment.

Not every food benefits equally from freeze drying.

Some products already preserve well through refrigeration or conventional drying.

Others may lose little quality when frozen alone.

In these cases, the additional cost and complexity of freeze drying may offer only limited advantages.

Successful preservation therefore is not about choosing the most advanced technology.

It is about selecting the method that best matches the biology of the food, the intended storage period and the purpose of preservation.

Technology should solve a biological problem.

It should never become the objective itself.

Freeze drying demonstrates how deeply an understanding of biology and physics can improve food preservation.

It also illustrates a broader principle.

No preservation method exists in isolation.

Each builds upon the same biological foundations.

Together they provide a toolbox from which the most appropriate solution can be selected.

## Closing Reflection

Throughout this book, each preservation method has revealed a different way of influencing the relationship between water and biology.

Drying removes liquid water.

Cooling slows the biological processes that depend upon it.

Freezing transforms liquid water into ice, greatly restricting biological activity while preserving much of the food's structure.

Freeze drying combines these principles in a unique way.

The food is first stabilised by freezing.

The frozen water is then removed without returning to the liquid state.

As a result, much of the biological architecture remains intact despite the almost complete removal of water.

The goal of freeze drying is therefore not simply to produce dry food.

It is to preserve as much of the original biological organisation as possible while creating conditions in which biological change becomes extremely slow.

This demonstrates an important principle that extends beyond freeze drying itself.

The most effective preservation methods do not work by fighting biology.

They work by understanding the physical environment upon which biology depends.

When that environment changes, biological activity changes with it.

Water.

Temperature.

Pressure.

Time.

Together they determine the pace at which life continues after harvest.

Preservation is therefore not the art of stopping nature.

It is the science of guiding natural processes towards greater stability while respecting the biological structure that remains.

Freeze drying represents one of the most advanced examples of this principle.

Yet it follows the same philosophy as every preservation method discussed so far.

Understand the biology.

Understand the environment.

Then choose the technology that best supports both.

Freeze drying dramatically extends the storage life of food.

Eventually, however, every preserved product leaves storage.

It must be transported.

Handled.

Packaged.

Protected from moisture and oxygen.

Preservation therefore does not end when processing is complete.

The surrounding environment continues to influence quality throughout storage and distribution.

Understanding how food remains stable after preservation begins with understanding packaging.
