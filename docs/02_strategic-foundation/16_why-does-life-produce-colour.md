# Chapter 16 — Why Does Life Produce Colour?

> *"Before life can be eaten, it must first be seen. Preservation begins by understanding the messages biology creates."*

## Introduction

A ripe tomato is red.

A lemon is yellow.

Leaves are green.

Blueberries appear almost black.

Autumn forests become golden before winter arrives.

Colour surrounds every living organism.

Yet colour exists for far more than beauty.

Long before humans cultivated crops, colour already played an essential role in biology.

Flowers attracted pollinators.

Fruit signalled ripeness.

Leaves captured sunlight.

Bright colours warned predators.

Camouflage protected vulnerable organisms.

Many colours carry biological information.

Pigments make much of this communication possible.

These remarkable molecules absorb certain wavelengths of light while reflecting others.

The reflected light creates the colours our eyes perceive.

To a living organism, however, colour is more than appearance.

It is communication.

A change in colour often signals a change in biology.

Ripening.

Growth.

Stress.

Disease.

Ageing.

Death.

Many of the colours we observe are therefore not permanent properties.

They are visible expressions of biological change.

After harvest, this communication continues.

Pigments respond to oxygen.

Temperature.

Acidity.

Light.

Enzymes.

As these conditions change, the colours of food change with them.

Preservation is therefore concerned not only with maintaining appearance.

It seeks to preserve the biological information carried by colour itself.

Understanding pigments begins with understanding that colour is one of biology's oldest languages.

## Part I — Pigments Perform Work

Pigments do not exist only to make organisms visible.

They perform biological work.

Chlorophyll absorbs light that can be used to build sugars.

Carotenoids help collect light while also protecting tissues from excessive energy.

Anthocyanins can respond to light, temperature, acidity and stress.

Other pigments attract pollinators, guide seed dispersers or discourage animals from feeding.

The colour we see is therefore connected to function.

Green may reveal active photosynthesis.

Red may signal ripeness.

Purple may help protect young tissue from intense light.

Yellow may appear when chlorophyll is no longer maintained.

These meanings are not universal.

The same colour may arise from different pigments in different organisms.

Even within one plant, a pigment may serve several purposes.

Biological molecules rarely serve appearance alone.

Colour emerges because particular molecules interact with light in particular structures.

Change either the molecule or the structure and the colour may change with it.

## Part II — Colour Depends on Its Environment

A pigment never acts in isolation.

Its appearance depends upon the environment surrounding it.

Acidity can change the molecular form and electrical charge of a pigment.

This is why anthocyanin-rich foods may shift between red, purple and blue as pH changes.

Oxygen can alter pigments directly or support reactions that transform them.

Heat can accelerate chemical change.

Light can both reveal colour and provide the energy that damages it.

Enzymes can catalyse reactions that create new colours when tissues are cut, bruised or aged.

Water influences molecular movement and the physical structure through which light travels.

The visible result may therefore change even when some pigment remains.

A leaf can lose its bright green colour as chlorophyll breaks down.

A cut apple can become brown as previously separated enzymes and compounds meet oxygen.

A berry can darken as cell structures collapse and pigments move into new environments.

A dried product can appear paler because its surface reflects light differently.

Colour change is therefore evidence.

It tells us that molecules, structures or surroundings have changed.

Yet it does not tell us everything.

A bright colour does not automatically prove that a food is nutritious.

A faded colour does not always mean that the food is unsafe.

Colour is an indicator, not a complete diagnosis.

Good preservation uses it as one source of information alongside aroma, texture, water activity, temperature and microbiological evidence.

## Part III — Not Every Colour Change Is Damage

Preservation is sometimes described as the attempt to keep colour unchanged.

That objective is too simple.

Many valued foods depend upon controlled colour development.

Bread becomes brown during baking.

Coffee beans darken during roasting.

Fermented foods develop colours that were absent from their raw ingredients.

Fruit changes colour as it ripens.

These transformations can communicate readiness, create flavour and form part of the identity of the food.

The important question is not whether colour changes.

It is why the colour changes and whether that change is desirable.

Uncontrolled browning may indicate damage or excessive exposure.

Controlled browning may create a valued product.

Loss of green colour in a fresh microgreen may indicate declining quality.

A colour shift during a planned drying process may be acceptable if nutrition, safety and flavour remain strong.

Engineering therefore requires an intended outcome.

Without that outcome, colour can easily become a misleading target.

Technology may preserve appearance while other qualities deteriorate.

Or it may reject a nutritious product because it no longer matches an artificial visual standard.

Honest preservation does not treat colour as decoration.

It interprets colour in relation to biology and purpose.

## Part IV — Engineering with Colour

Colour can help guide preservation when it is measured carefully.

Consistent photography can record gradual change.

Defined lighting can make comparisons more reliable.

Spectral measurements can distinguish changes that the eye may miss.

Computer vision can help identify patterns across many trays or batches.

Yet every measurement requires context.

A camera responds to lighting, angle and exposure.

Human vision adapts to its surroundings.

Pigments respond differently across species and growth stages.

A numerical colour value is therefore not a biological conclusion by itself.

It becomes meaningful when linked to observations, process conditions and product quality.

For the Microgreens ERP project, colour can become part of a wider biological record.

Images may be linked to a crop identifier, batch, position and time.

Changes can then be compared with temperature, humidity, light exposure, harvest stage and storage conditions.

The objective is not to automate judgement before the biology is understood.

It is to preserve observations so that understanding can grow.

Indicator plants and visible symptoms may provide early warnings.

Laboratory analysis may later reveal which pigments or nutrients changed.

Together, these forms of evidence can transform colour from a subjective impression into a useful biological signal.

Engineering supports that process by making observation consistent.

Biology gives the observation meaning.

## Closing Reflection

Life produces colour because pigments capture energy, protect tissues and communicate with other organisms.

After harvest, those pigments continue responding to their surroundings.

Their colours can reveal changes in molecules, structure and environment.

Preservation should therefore neither ignore colour nor pursue it blindly.

It should ask what the colour represents.

What changed.

Why it changed.

And whether that change supports the intended food.

Colour is one language of biology.

It speaks through light.

Food also communicates through molecules that travel through the air, dissolve in saliva and interact with our senses.

To understand that language, we must now turn to aroma and flavour.
