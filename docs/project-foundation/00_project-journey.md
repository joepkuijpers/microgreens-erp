Project Journey
Purpose

This document explains the journey that gradually led to the creation of the Microgreens ERP project.

It is not intended as a biography.

Instead, it provides the context behind the observations, experiences and questions that eventually became the principles documented throughout the Project Foundation.

The project did not begin with software.

It did not begin with microgreens.

It began with curiosity.

Over many years, travel, education, practical work and personal experiences gradually revealed recurring patterns within food systems, agriculture, technology and society.

Rather than collecting answers, I began collecting questions.

Many of those questions eventually developed into principles.

Those principles became the foundation upon which this project is built.

Growing Up

I grew up in Oss, the Netherlands.

My childhood was stable. I enjoyed good health, had supportive parents and completed secondary education (HAVO).

When I finished school, however, I had no clear idea which direction I wanted my life to take.

Instead of immediately continuing my education, I decided to explore the world and learn through experience.

Looking back, that decision became the first step of a much longer journey.

Tanzania

At the age of eighteen, with the support of my grandfather, I travelled to Tanzania.

Through American Field Service, I joined an ecovillage project that aimed to strengthen local communities through employment and cooperation.

At that time I mainly observed rather than questioned.

Only years later did I begin asking a question that has remained with me ever since.

Why does a project that aims to empower local communities depend on volunteers from another continent?

I never found a simple answer.

Instead, I learned something more valuable.

Different cultures solve problems differently.

Different societies organise themselves differently.

That experience taught me to observe before judging, a principle that still influences every aspect of this project.

Wageningen

After returning to the Netherlands, I enrolled at Van Hall Larenstein in Wageningen, where I studied Fair Trade Management and Tropical Agriculture.

The programme introduced me to subjects including:

value chains;
international trade;
transportation;
plant science;
nutrition;
sustainable agriculture;
economics.

The programme was taught entirely in English.

Alongside my studies I became familiar with alternative ways of living through communities such as Droevendaal.

For the first time I began to see agriculture not as an isolated activity but as one interconnected system where biology, economics, logistics and society continuously influence one another.

Nepal

During my second year I completed a six-month internship on an organic tea farm in Nepal.

There I lived with and learned from Mr. Deepack Kulung.

When I asked why he chose organic cultivation, his answer was simple.

He did not want to pollute the soil for future generations.

His motivation was responsibility rather than certification.

Yet I also witnessed another reality.

Because production volumes were relatively small, there were no dedicated organic buyers.

His tea entered conventional supply chains, receiving no additional value despite the additional effort required to produce it organically.

This experience demonstrated that biological responsibility and economic systems are not always aligned.

Doing the right thing does not automatically create a sustainable business.

Australia

After returning from Nepal I realised that my interests had started moving beyond the structure of formal education.

I decided to leave my studies and travel to Australia.

There I worked in large-scale broccoli production while supporting myself through temporary work.

The experience exposed me to highly industrialised agriculture.

Later I travelled through Tasmania.

The contrast between intensive monoculture and Tasmania's biodiversity strengthened my appreciation for natural diversity, resilience and ecological balance.

Spirit Restaurant

Back in the Netherlands I worked at the organic restaurant Spirit.

Customers paid exactly for the amount of food they placed on their plates, reducing waste on the consumer side.

Behind the scenes, however, I discovered another challenge.

Large amounts of high-quality organic food still entered the waste stream.

I attempted to connect the restaurant with homeless shelters so surplus food could be redistributed.

Technically this was possible.

Economically it was not attractive.

Storage, freezing, labelling and transportation created additional costs.

The easiest solution remained disposal.

This became another recurring observation.

Waste is often not created by bad intentions.

It is created by systems that reward convenience over recovery.

Borders

Later I travelled through Israel, Palestine, the Sinai Peninsula and Egypt.

During these travels I met Sall from Guinea.

When I later tried to help him obtain a visa to visit the Netherlands, his application was rejected.

The contrast between our situations profoundly affected me.

I could cross borders with relatively little effort.

He could not.

That experience challenged many assumptions I held about freedom, opportunity and equality.

For a period I even destroyed my own passport as a personal protest.

Living without identification quickly taught me another lesson.

Rejecting systems entirely often creates new limitations rather than improving them.

Eventually I restored my documentation and continued forward with a deeper appreciation of both freedom and responsibility.

Searching for Direction

The years that followed included temporary jobs, periods of housing insecurity and an ongoing search for meaningful work.

I worked in hospitality, experienced temporary accommodation and continued exploring different directions.

Later I became fascinated by the maritime sector.

I independently studied topics such as navigation, marine communication, COLREGS, ship stability and water management.

Once again I encountered practical barriers.

Formal certification required financial resources that were beyond my reach.

Instead of abandoning learning, I continued studying independently.

This period reinforced another principle that still guides the project today.

Learning should never depend entirely on formal education.

Reflection

During a six-day fast I spent considerable time reflecting on the direction of my life.

For the first time I deliberately connected all previous experiences instead of viewing them as unrelated events.

I realised that nearly every experience pointed toward the same recurring themes:

biology;
food;
sustainability;
engineering;
logistics;
education;
technology;
continuous improvement.

Rather than continuing to search for existing work, I decided to create meaningful work myself.

Why Microgreens?

Microgreens became the practical starting point.

Not because they are the final objective.

But because they provide an accessible environment for experimentation.

They allow biology, engineering, automation, software development, preservation, logistics and sustainability to be studied together within one manageable system.

With relatively little capital, ideas can be tested, measured and continuously improved before expanding.

The Microgreens ERP project therefore became the practical laboratory in which years of observations could finally be transformed into measurable improvements.

From Observations to Principles

The project did not begin with solutions.

It began with observations.

Over time, recurring observations became questions.

Questions became principles.

Principles became the Project Foundation.

The Project Foundation then became the basis for designing the ERP, cultivation methods, documentation and the project's long-term vision.

The software is therefore not the objective.

It is one practical expression of a much broader vision.

Continuing the Journey

The journey is still ongoing.

Every experiment, every conversation, every success and every failure continues to refine the project.

The objective has never been to build software alone.

Nor has it been simply to grow microgreens.

The objective is to contribute, one practical step at a time, to a Food Chain that is more transparent, biologically responsible, sustainable and accessible.

The Microgreens ERP project is not the destination.

It is the beginning of that journey.
