# Pest Library

## Purpose

Successful biological cultivation begins with accurate pest identification.

Different pest species require different monitoring methods, environmental conditions, biological control agents and management strategies.

This document serves as the central reference for pest species that may occur during microgreen cultivation and provides the biological knowledge required to make informed cultivation decisions.

The Pest Library supports Integrated Pest Management (IPM) by helping growers identify pests early, understand their biology and select appropriate control measures.

---

## Scope

This document focuses on arthropods and other organisms capable of damaging microgreens or disrupting cultivation.

For each pest, the following information should be documented:

- Identification
- Biology
- Life cycle
- Damage symptoms
- Environmental preferences
- Monitoring methods
- Prevention
- Biological control
- ERP integration

---

## Pest Categories

For practical use, pests are grouped according to their primary habitat and behaviour.

### Flying Insects

Examples include:

- Fungus gnats
- Shore flies
- Whiteflies
- Winged aphids

Flying insects are often the first pests detected through biological monitoring or carnivorous plants.

---

### Crawling Insects

Examples include:

- Aphids
- Thrips
- Leaf miners
- Caterpillars

These species typically feed directly on plant tissue and should be monitored through routine crop inspection.

---

### Mites

Examples include:

- Spider mites
- Broad mites
- Cyclamen mites

Due to their small size, infestations often remain unnoticed until visible crop damage occurs.

---

### Soil and Root Pests

Examples include:

- Fungus gnat larvae
- Root aphids
- Root-feeding larvae

Root pests are particularly important because damage often occurs before symptoms become visible above ground.

## Early Detection

Early detection significantly reduces crop losses.

Monitoring methods include:

- Visual inspection
- Sticky traps
- Carnivorous plants
- Indicator plants
- Environmental monitoring
- Routine crop scouting

---

## Relationship with Environmental Conditions

Pest outbreaks rarely occur randomly.

Population development is often influenced by factors including:

- Humidity
- Temperature
- Air circulation
- Irrigation practices
- Crop density
- Sanitation
- Biodiversity

Understanding these relationships improves preventive cultivation strategies.

---

## ERP Integration

The ERP should support pest management by recording observations over multiple cultivation cycles.

Historical data can help identify:

- Seasonal pest trends
- High-risk cultivation areas
- Environmental correlations
- Effectiveness of biological control
- Long-term improvements

The objective is not merely to record pest observations, but to continuously improve cultivation through accumulated knowledge.
