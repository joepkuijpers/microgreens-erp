# Seed Health

## Purpose

Seed health forms the foundation of successful crop production.

The quality, viability and biological condition of a seed directly influence germination, crop uniformity, disease risk and final yield.

This document describes the factors that determine seed health and their importance within the biological cultivation strategy of the Microgreens ERP project.

---

## Introduction

Every cultivation cycle begins with a seed.

Although seeds may appear inactive, they are living organisms carrying both genetic potential and biological risks.

Healthy seeds produce vigorous seedlings, while poor-quality seeds often lead to reduced germination, uneven growth and increased susceptibility to pests and diseases.

Maintaining seed health is therefore one of the most effective preventive cultivation strategies.

---

## Seed Quality

Seed quality consists of multiple characteristics rather than a single measurement.

Important factors include:

- Genetic purity
- Physical purity
- Germination percentage
- Vigour
- Uniformity
- Moisture content
- Seed age
- Mechanical damage

High-quality seed improves cultivation consistency and reduces production losses.

---

## Seed Viability

Viability describes the ability of a seed to germinate under suitable conditions.

Viability naturally decreases over time and is influenced by:

- Storage temperature
- Relative humidity
- Moisture content
- Seed species
- Storage duration

Regular germination testing helps evaluate seed viability.

---

## Seed Vigour

Two seed lots may have identical germination percentages while performing differently during cultivation.

Seed vigour describes the strength and uniformity of germination and early seedling development.

High-vigour seeds generally produce:

- Faster germination
- Stronger seedlings
- Greater stress tolerance
- More uniform crops

---

## Seed-Borne Pathogens

Seeds may carry microorganisms capable of causing disease.

Examples include:

- Fungal pathogens
- Bacterial pathogens
- Viral pathogens

Some pathogens remain dormant until environmental conditions become favourable.

Using healthy seed significantly reduces disease risk.

---

## Seed Contamination

Seeds may become contaminated during:

- Harvest
- Processing
- Transport
- Storage

Possible contaminants include:

- Weed seeds
- Soil particles
- Insects
- Microorganisms
- Foreign materials

Careful supplier selection and quality inspection reduce contamination risks.

---

## Seed Storage

Proper storage preserves both viability and vigour.

Important storage conditions include:

- Stable temperature
- Low relative humidity
- Protection from light
- Clean storage environment
- Pest protection

Different species may require different storage conditions.

---

## Germination Testing

Routine germination testing helps monitor seed quality.

Possible observations include:

- Germination percentage
- Germination speed
- Uniformity
- Abnormal seedlings
- Non-germinated seeds

Results should be documented for future comparison.

---

## Seed Treatments

Depending on cultivation objectives, seeds may receive various treatments.

Examples include:

- Biological inoculation
- Priming
- Soaking
- Hot water treatment
- Physical cleaning

Treatments should always be scientifically justified and carefully documented.

---

## Monitoring

Seed health should be monitored before every cultivation cycle.

Possible observations include:

- Visual inspection
- Seed size
- Colour
- Odour
- Moisture
- Germination performance
- Storage condition

Monitoring helps identify problems before sowing.
