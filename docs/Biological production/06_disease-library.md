# Disease Library

## Purpose

Healthy crops depend not only on preventing pests but also on preventing the development and spread of plant diseases.

Many diseases develop gradually and remain unnoticed until significant crop damage has already occurred. Understanding the conditions that favour disease development allows preventive measures to be implemented before outbreaks occur.

This document serves as the central reference for diseases that may affect microgreens and describes their identification, biology, prevention and management within the biological cultivation strategy of the Microgreens ERP project.

---

## Scope

This document focuses on diseases caused by:

- Fungi
- Oomycetes (water moulds)
- Bacteria
- Viruses
- Physiological disorders resulting from environmental stress

For each disease, the following information should be documented:

- Identification

## Disease Categories

Diseases can be grouped according to their biological origin.

### Fungal Diseases

Common examples include:

- Botrytis (Grey mould)
- Powdery mildew
- Downy mildew
- Fusarium
- Rhizoctonia
- Alternaria

Fungal diseases are among the most common problems in indoor cultivation and are often associated with excessive humidity or poor air circulation.

---

### Oomycete Diseases

Although often treated as fungi, oomycetes belong to a different biological group.

Examples include:

- Pythium
- Phytophthora

These organisms frequently affect seeds, roots and young seedlings.

---

### Bacterial Diseases

Bacterial infections generally spread through contaminated water, tools or damaged plant tissue.

Examples include:

- Soft rot
- Bacterial leaf spot

---

### Viral Diseases

Viruses cannot be cured once plants become infected.

Transmission often occurs through insect vectors or contaminated plant material.

Although relatively uncommon in microgreens, they should still be recognised as a potential risk.

---

### Physiological Disorders

Not all crop problems are caused by pathogens.

Environmental conditions may produce symptoms resembling disease.

Examples include:

- Nutrient deficiencies
- Water stress
- Heat stress
- Cold injury
- Excessive light
- Poor germination
- Oxygen deficiency

Correct diagnosis is essential before corrective actions are taken.

## Prevention

Disease prevention is significantly more effective than disease treatment.

Preventive measures include:

- Good sanitation
- Healthy seed
- Clean equipment
- Proper irrigation
- Adequate air circulation
- Environmental monitoring
- Biological balance
- Early observation

A stable cultivation environment greatly reduces disease risk.

---

## Diagnosis

Correct diagnosis is essential before taking action.

Several diseases produce similar symptoms, making visual identification alone insufficient in some situations.

Whenever uncertainty exists, additional observations or laboratory analysis should be considered.

---

## Relationship with Environmental Conditions

Disease development is strongly influenced by environmental conditions.

Important factors include:

- Relative humidity
- Leaf wetness
- Temperature
- Air movement
- Condensation
- Irrigation practices
- Crop density
- Hygiene

Understanding these relationships enables preventive rather than reactive cultivation.

---

## ERP Integration

The ERP should support disease management by storing observations together with environmental data.

Long-term analysis may reveal:

- recurring disease patterns;
- seasonal trends;
- environmental risk factors;
- effectiveness of preventive measures;
- relationships between cultivation practices and disease occurrence.

Accumulated knowledge improves future cultivation decisions.
