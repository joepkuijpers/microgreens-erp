# Companion and Indicator Plants

## Purpose

Companion and indicator plants can contribute to healthier cultivation by supporting biological balance and providing early warning of changing environmental conditions.

Although they are widely associated with outdoor agriculture, many of the underlying principles can also be applied within controlled indoor cultivation systems.

This document describes the role of companion and indicator plants within the biological cultivation strategy of the Microgreens ERP project.

---

## Companion Plants

Companion plants are intentionally introduced because they provide one or more beneficial functions within the cultivation environment.

Their benefits may include:

- attracting beneficial organisms;
- distracting pests from the main crop;
- increasing biological diversity;
- improving the cultivation environment;
- supporting natural pest management.

The effectiveness of companion planting depends on the crop, surrounding environment and cultivation objectives.

---

## Indicator Plants

Indicator plants act as living sensors.

Certain plant species respond more quickly than the primary crop to changes in environmental conditions or pest pressure.

By observing these plants, growers may detect problems before significant crop damage occurs.

Indicator plants may reveal:

- nutrient deficiencies;
- water stress;
- excessive humidity;
- insect activity;
- disease development;
- unsuitable environmental conditions.

Early detection allows corrective actions to be taken before production is affected.

---

## Biological Diversity

Introducing carefully selected companion and indicator plants increases biological diversity within the cultivation area.

Greater diversity often results in a more stable cultivation ecosystem, reducing the likelihood of sudden pest outbreaks caused by monoculture conditions.

The objective is not to maximise the number of plant species, but to introduce diversity with a clear biological purpose.

---

## Selection Criteria

Plant selection should consider:

- compatibility with the cultivated crop;
- growth rate;
- maintenance requirements;
- environmental preferences;
- pest interactions;
- disease susceptibility;
- available cultivation space.

Plants requiring significantly different environmental conditions should generally be avoided.

---

## Indoor Applications

Possible applications include:

- monitoring entrances for incoming pests;
- placing indicator plants near cultivation racks;
- supporting populations of beneficial organisms;
- creating permanent biological observation points;
- increasing biodiversity within dedicated research areas.

The suitability of each application should be evaluated through observation and experimentation.

---

## Monitoring

Regular observations may include:

- plant health;
- leaf colour;
- growth rate;
- flowering;
- insect presence;
- disease symptoms;
- environmental changes.

Consistent observations provide valuable long-term cultivation data.

---

## ERP Integration

Potential ERP records include:

- plant species;
- cultivar;
- cultivation location;
- planting date;
- maintenance history;
- observed pests;
- observed diseases;
- environmental observations;
- photographs;
- notes.

Historical records may reveal relationships between environmental conditions and crop performance.

---

## Limitations

Companion and indicator plants support biological cultivation but do not replace good cultivation practices.

They should always be combined with:

- Integrated Pest Management;
- environmental monitoring;
- sanitation;
- biological control;
- routine crop inspections.

Their effectiveness depends on proper species selection and continuous observation.
