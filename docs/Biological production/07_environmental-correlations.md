# Environmental Correlations

## Purpose

Healthy plant growth is determined by the interaction of many environmental factors rather than by individual conditions alone.

Temperature, humidity, light, air movement, water availability and carbon dioxide continuously influence one another. These environmental conditions directly affect plant growth, pest populations, disease development and overall crop quality.

This document describes these relationships and explains how environmental data can be used to improve cultivation through observation, analysis and continuous learning.

---

## Introduction

Plants continuously respond to their environment.

Likewise, pests, pathogens and beneficial organisms also react to environmental changes.

Understanding these relationships allows growers to move beyond reacting to problems and towards preventing them before they occur.

The objective is to understand *why* biological events occur rather than simply recording *that* they occurred.

---

## Environmental Factors

Important environmental variables include:

- Air temperature
- Root-zone temperature
- Relative humidity
- Vapour Pressure Deficit (VPD)
- Light intensity
- Photoperiod
- Carbon dioxide
- Oxygen
- Air circulation
- Irrigation frequency
- Growing medium moisture
- Water quality

None of these variables should be considered independently.

---

## Plant Growth

Environmental conditions influence nearly every aspect of plant development, including:

- Germination
- Root development
- Stem elongation
- Leaf expansion
- Photosynthesis
- Respiration
- Water uptake
- Nutrient uptake
- Biomass production
- Crop quality

Optimising one factor while neglecting another rarely produces the best results.

---

## Pest Development

Many pest species reproduce more rapidly under specific environmental conditions.

Examples include:

- Warm temperatures accelerating insect development.
- Excessive moisture increasing fungus gnat populations.
- Stagnant air favouring certain flying insects.
- Plant stress increasing susceptibility to pest attack.

Understanding these relationships allows environmental management to become part of pest prevention.

---

## Disease Development

Many plant diseases depend on favourable environmental conditions.

Common contributing factors include:

- High humidity
- Leaf wetness
- Condensation
- Poor ventilation
- Overwatering
- Root oxygen deficiency

Disease prevention therefore begins with environmental management.

---

## Beneficial Organisms

Beneficial organisms also depend on suitable environmental conditions.

Factors influencing their effectiveness include:

- Temperature
- Humidity
- Light
- Availability of prey
- Biodiversity
- Habitat stability

Successful biological control requires conditions that support both the crop and the beneficial organisms.

---

## Sensor Integration

Environmental monitoring should provide continuous information about cultivation conditions.

Possible sensors include:

- Temperature
- Relative humidity
- Light intensity
- CO₂ concentration
- Oxygen concentration
- Air pressure
- Soil or substrate moisture
- Water temperature
- Water pH
- Electrical conductivity (EC)

The objective is to build an accurate picture of the cultivation environment over time.

---

## Data Analysis

Environmental data becomes valuable when analysed together with cultivation observations.

Examples include:

- Increased humidity preceding fungal disease.
- Reduced light affecting crop growth.
- Temperature fluctuations reducing germination.
- Moisture levels influencing fungus gnat populations.
- Air circulation improving disease resistance.

Patterns often become visible only after analysing multiple cultivation cycles.

---

## Continuous Learning

Each cultivation cycle provides new information.

Rather than relying solely on predefined cultivation recipes, the Microgreens ERP project continuously improves by comparing:

- Environmental conditions
- Biological observations
- Crop performance
- Harvest quality
- Yield
- Corrective actions

Knowledge accumulated over many production cycles supports increasingly informed cultivation decisions.

---

## ERP Integration

Environmental correlations combine data from multiple ERP modules, including:

- Sensor data
- Crop profiles
- Grow batches
- Pest observations
- Disease observations
- Biological monitoring
- Harvest records
- Quality assessments

By connecting these datasets, the ERP can reveal relationships that may not be obvious through observation alone.

---

## Future Development

As additional sensors and analytical methods become available, environmental correlations may support:

- Predictive pest warnings
- Disease risk estimation
- Automated environmental adjustments
- Trend analysis
- Decision support
- Artificial intelligence models

The objective is to support growers with evidence rather than assumptions.
