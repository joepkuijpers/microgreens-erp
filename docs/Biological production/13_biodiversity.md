# Biodiversity

## Purpose

Biodiversity is the diversity of life within an ecosystem.

Healthy cultivation systems depend not only on healthy plants but also on a balanced community of microorganisms, insects, fungi and other organisms that continuously interact with one another.

This document describes the importance of biodiversity within the Microgreens ERP project and how biological diversity contributes to stable, resilient and sustainable cultivation systems.

---

## Introduction

Nature functions through relationships.

Every organism influences other organisms either directly or indirectly.

Removing or introducing a single species may alter the balance of the entire cultivation environment.

Rather than attempting to eliminate all biological activity, the objective of biological cultivation is to encourage a diverse ecosystem that naturally supports healthy plant growth.

---

## What is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity exists at several levels.

### Genetic Diversity

Variation within a single species.

Genetic diversity improves resilience against changing environmental conditions, pests and diseases.

---

### Species Diversity

The variety of different organisms present within a cultivation system.

Examples include:

- Plants
- Beneficial insects
- Pollinators
- Carnivorous plants
- Microorganisms
- Soil organisms

Greater species diversity generally supports greater biological stability.

---

### Ecosystem Diversity

Different habitats support different biological communities.

Even within controlled cultivation environments, multiple microhabitats may exist.

Examples include:

- Root zones
- Growing media
- Irrigation systems
- Air spaces
- Drainage areas

Each environment supports its own biological community.

---

## Biodiversity and Plant Health

Healthy ecosystems rarely depend upon a single biological process.

Instead, plant health emerges through interactions between:

- Roots
- Beneficial microorganisms
- Water
- Nutrient availability
- Environmental conditions
- Beneficial insects
- Natural pest regulation

Greater biological diversity often results in greater system resilience.

---

## Biodiversity and Pest Management

Monocultures often favour rapid pest development.

Increasing biological diversity may contribute to:

- Improved biological control
- Reduced pest establishment
- Greater ecological stability
- Improved resilience against outbreaks

Biodiversity complements Integrated Pest Management rather than replacing it.

---

## Biodiversity and Disease Suppression

A biologically diverse cultivation system may reduce disease pressure through:

- Competition between microorganisms
- Occupation of ecological niches
- Increased biological balance
- Improved plant health

Disease prevention begins long before symptoms become visible.

---

## Biodiversity in Controlled Environments

Indoor cultivation provides fewer natural biological interactions than outdoor ecosystems.

Intentional biological diversity may therefore become an important cultivation strategy.

Examples include:

- Beneficial organisms
- Beneficial microorganisms
- Companion plants
- Indicator plants
- Carnivorous plants

The objective is not maximum diversity but functional diversity.

---

## Monitoring Biodiversity

Biodiversity should be observed over time.

Possible indicators include:

- Species richness
- Beneficial organism activity
- Pest diversity
- Root-zone biology
- Microbial diversity
- Plant health
- Ecosystem stability

Long-term observations provide greater insight than isolated measurements.

---

## ERP Integration

Potential ERP records include:

- Species observations
- Beneficial organism releases
- Pest diversity
- Plant diversity
- Microbial applications
- Environmental conditions
- Photographs
- Research observations

Combining biological observations with environmental data enables long-term evaluation of ecosystem health.

---

## Continuous Improvement

Biodiversity is not a fixed condition.

Every cultivation cycle provides opportunities to strengthen biological balance through observation, experimentation and learning.

Historical records allow the project to evaluate how biological diversity influences crop performance over time.
