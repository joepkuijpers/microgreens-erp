# Beneficial Organisms

## Purpose

Beneficial organisms play an essential role in maintaining healthy crop production through natural biological processes.

Rather than relying solely on chemical interventions, biological cultivation aims to establish a balanced ecosystem in which pests remain under control and plant health is continuously supported.

This document describes the beneficial organisms that can be integrated into the Microgreens ERP project, their biological functions, practical applications, and how observations can be recorded within the ERP.

---

## Biological Balance

In every cultivation system, organisms continuously interact with one another.

Some organisms consume plants.

Others consume those plant pests.

Some improve soil biology.

Others recycle nutrients.

Healthy cultivation is therefore not achieved by eliminating all living organisms, but by encouraging a stable biological balance.

The objective is not to create a sterile environment, but a resilient ecosystem.

---

## Types of Beneficial Organisms

Beneficial organisms can be grouped into several categories.

### Predatory Insects

Predatory insects actively consume pest species throughout their life cycle.

Examples include:

- Ladybirds
- Lacewings
- Predatory bugs

These predators are particularly effective against aphids, whiteflies and other soft-bodied insects.

---

### Predatory Mites

Predatory mites are widely used in greenhouse cultivation due to their small size and high efficiency.

Common targets include:

- Thrips
- Spider mites
- Broad mites

Because they are nearly invisible to the naked eye, monitoring is important to confirm successful establishment.

---

### Parasitic Wasps

Parasitic wasps lay their eggs inside or on pest insects.

The developing larvae consume the host, naturally reducing pest populations.

Unlike predators, they usually target specific pest species.

---

### Entomopathogenic Nematodes

Beneficial nematodes attack insect larvae living within growing media.

They are particularly useful for controlling fungus gnat larvae without disturbing the crop.

---

### Beneficial Microorganisms

Not all beneficial organisms are visible.

Many bacteria and fungi improve plant health by:

- increasing nutrient availability;
- suppressing pathogens;
- stimulating root development;
- competing with harmful microorganisms.

Because these organisms represent a specialised field of study, they are described separately in the **Beneficial Microorganisms** document.

---

## Selection Criteria

Before introducing any beneficial organism, several factors should be considered:

- Target pest species
- Environmental conditions
- Temperature
- Humidity
- Light conditions
- Crop sensitivity
- Compatibility with other biological controls

Successful biological control depends on selecting organisms that match the cultivation environment.

---

## Monitoring

Introducing beneficial organisms is only the beginning.

Regular monitoring is required to determine:

- establishment success;
- population development;
- pest suppression;
- environmental suitability;
- long-term effectiveness.

Observations should be recorded consistently to improve future cultivation cycles.

---

## ERP Integration

The ERP may record information including:

- organism species;
- supplier;
- release date;
- release location;
- quantity released;
- target pest;
- environmental conditions;
- follow-up observations;
- effectiveness;
- additional notes.

Historical records make it possible to evaluate biological control strategies over multiple cultivation cycles.
